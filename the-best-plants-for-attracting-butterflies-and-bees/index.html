The Best Plants for Attracting Butterflies and Bees


Creating a lively, buzzing garden filled with colorful butterflies and busy bees is a rewarding endeavor. Not only do these pollinators add beauty and movement to your outdoor space, but they also contribute significantly to the health and sustainability of the environment. By thoughtfully choosing the right plants, you can turn your garden into a haven for these vital creatures, supporting their populations while enhancing your own garden’s vitality.

Understanding the Role of Butterflies and Bees

Before diving into the best plant choices, it’s useful to grasp why butterflies and bees are drawn to certain flowers. Both rely heavily on nectar as a food source, which means that plants with abundant, accessible nectar are often the top contenders to attract them. Additionally, native plants tend to be particularly appealing since local pollinators have co-evolved with these species and know exactly where to find their preferred nectar sources.

Butterflies are visual creatures, attracted to bright colors like red, yellow, orange, pink, and purple. Bees, on the other hand, see colors differently, favoring blues, purples, and whites, and are especially drawn to flowers with simple shapes and landing platforms.

Key Characteristics of Plants That Attract Pollinators

  • Nectar-rich Flowers: The primary energy source for butterflies and bees.
  • Vibrant Colors: Especially in the spectrum preferred by pollinators.
  • Fragrance: Sweet or mild scents often draw in visitors.
  • Flower Shape and Size: Open, flat, or tubular flowers with accessible nectar work best.
  • Bloom Time: A range that covers spring through fall ensures a continual food supply.
  • Native Species: These often offer the most sustenance and comfort to local pollinators.

With these factors in mind, let’s explore various plants that excel at attracting butterflies and bees.

Top Plants to Attract Butterflies and Bees

1. Milkweed (Asclepias spp.)

Milkweed is renowned as a critical plant for monarch butterflies, serving as both a nectar source and the only host plant for monarch caterpillars. Its clusters of fragrant flowers bloom from late spring to summer, offering ample nectar for numerous butterfly species and bees.

  • Best varieties: Common milkweed (Asclepias syriaca), butterfly weed (Asclepias tuberosa)
  • Growing tips: Prefers full sun and well-drained soil. Milkweed thrives in gardens that mimic natural prairies or meadows.

2. Coneflower (Echinacea spp.)

Eastern purple coneflower is a magnet for pollinators due to its large, daisy-like flowers and bright purple petals. Bees love its abundant pollen, while butterflies are drawn to its nectar. Coneflowers bloom late into summer, offering a reliable food source.

  • Growing tips: Plant in full sun with moderately fertile soil. Coneflowers are drought-tolerant and easy to grow.

3. Bee Balm (Monarda didyma)

Bee balm lives up to its name by attracting a variety of bees, butterflies, and even hummingbirds. Its dense clusters of tubular flowers bloom in summer and come in shades of red, pink, and purple. Often used in herbal teas, bee balm adds both beauty and utility to your garden.

  • Growing tips: Prefers full sun to partial shade and moist, well-drained soil. It spreads easily, so give it room to grow.

4. Lavender (Lavandula spp.)

A favorite in gardens worldwide, lavender’s aromatic purple flowers are irresistible to bees and certain butterfly species. Its blooming season extends from late spring through early summer.

  • Growing tips: Requires well-drained soil and full sun. Lavender prefers drier conditions and benefits from occasional pruning to encourage bushier growth.

5. Black-eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta)

With bright yellow petals and a dark central cone, black-eyed Susans are common in wildflower mixes and pollinator gardens. Their open flower structure makes nectar and pollen easily accessible to both bees and butterflies.

  • Growing tips: These plants thrive in full sun and tolerate a wide range of soil conditions, including poorer soils.

6. Zinnias (Zinnia elegans)

Zinnias are annual flowers that provide vibrant color throughout summer into fall. Their cup-shaped blossoms radiate attraction for butterflies, especially swallowtails and monarchs, as well as a variety of bee species.

  • Growing tips: Plant in full sun. They enjoy rich, well-drained soil and benefit from deadheading to prolong blooming.

7. Cosmos (Cosmos bipinnatus)

This charming annual is a butterfly favorite, featuring delicate, daisy-like blossoms in pink, white, and shades of purple. Cosmos flowers produce lots of nectar, making them irresistible to pollinators.

  • Growing tips: Prefers full sun and tolerates drought conditions once established.

8. Goldenrod (Solidago spp.)

Often misunderstood due to its association with allergies, Goldenrod produces bright yellow flowers late in the season, providing critical nectar sources when many other plants have stopped blooming. It supports a wide diversity of bee species and butterflies preparing for migration or hibernation.

  • Growing tips: Grows best in full sun and tolerates a variety of soil types.

9. Lupine (Lupinus spp.)

With striking tall spires of pea-like flowers, lupines attract bees, especially bumblebees, due to their robust nectar production. They add a vertical element to garden design and often bloom in late spring to early summer.

  • Growing tips: Prefers sunny locations with well-drained, acidic soils and benefits from occasional mulching.

10. Phlox (Phlox paniculata and Phlox subulata)

Phlox species offer clusters of fragrant flowers in many shades of pink, white, and purple. They bloom from mid-summer to early fall and play host to a range of pollinators.

  • Growing tips: Choose tall garden phlox for sunny borders and creeping phlox for ground cover. Most varieties prefer full sun and moist, well-drained soil.

Designing a Pollinator-Friendly Garden

Beyond choosing plants, how you arrange and maintain your garden can significantly impact its attractiveness to butterflies and bees.

Provide Continuous Blooms

One of the secrets to keeping pollinators visiting all season is ensuring that your garden has flowers blooming from early spring through late fall. Combine early bloomers like crocus and redbud with mid-season giants like bee balm and coneflower, then finish with late bloomers such as goldenrod and asters.

Group Plants Together

Grouping several plants of the same species close to one another creates a stronger visual signal for pollinators. Large patches of milkweed or zinnias, for example, are easier for butterflies and bees to find than scattered individual plants.

Avoid Pesticides

Chemical pesticides and herbicides can harm pollinators directly or reduce the diversity of plants they rely on. Opt for natural pest control methods or introduce beneficial insects like ladybugs to maintain balance.

Provide Habitat

Consider leaving some areas of bare soil or fallen leaves as nesting spots for solitary bees. Incorporating logs, brush piles, or even small water sources can encourage a diverse community of pollinators.

Wildflower Meadows: A Pollinator Paradise

If space permits, planting a wildflower meadow rich with native nectar plants can be one of the best ways to attract butterflies and bees. Wildflower meadows support a wide variety of species, replicate natural habitats, and require relatively low maintenance once established.

Popular native species for meadows include a mix of clover, goldenrod, black-eyed Susan, milkweed, and asters. Such meadows create not only a feast for pollinators but also a haven for birds and other beneficial wildlife.

Final Thoughts on Creating a Buzzing Garden

Attracting butterflies and bees goes beyond simply planting pretty flowers — it’s about nurturing an ecosystem that supports their life cycles and needs. By selecting a variety of native, nectar-rich plants with staggered bloom times, providing safe shelter, and keeping your garden chemical-free, you create a vibrant sanctuary buzzing with life.

From the fluttering monarchs to the diligent honeybees, your garden can become a magnet for these pivotal creatures, enriching your outdoor space with natural beauty and the satisfaction of playing a part in pollinator conservation.

Open your garden to these essential visitors and watch as your little patch of earth bursts into color, motion, and life. The rewards go far beyond aesthetics — they resonate through local ecosystems and communities alike.
