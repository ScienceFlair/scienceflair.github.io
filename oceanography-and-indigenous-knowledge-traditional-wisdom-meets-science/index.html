Oceanography and Indigenous Knowledge: Traditional Wisdom Meets Science


Oceanography, the scientific study of the ocean, has come a long way since its inception. However, as modern science delves deeper into the mysteries of the ocean, it is becoming increasingly apparent that it’s time to merge traditional wisdom with present-day scientific knowledge, particularly Indigenous knowledge. Such a merger would enrich our research studies and lead to new insights into marine discoveries.

Indigenous people, the early inhabitants of the oceans, have accumulated vital knowledge about the marine environment through generations of observation and practice. This knowledge, often passed down orally, was and still is the basis for sustainable resource use within their communities. Many Indigenous communities consider the sea as a living, interconnected entity, with a balance that must be maintained for survival.

With the influx of scientific knowledge, the ocean is no longer an enigma, and with the help of modern technology, we can now explore the unexplored, monitor ocean shifts, and predict weather patterns more accurately. But the crux of the issue lies in the broad approach to solving oceanic predicaments.

As researchers, we’ve been extremely reliant on scientific methodologies to derive data, but we must consider tapping into Indigenous knowledge, not only to cross-verify our data but also to fill in the gaps of our understanding.

Marine Ecosystems and Indigenous Knowledge

Marine Ecosystems and Indigenous Knowledge

Marine ecosystems are intricately linked with Indigenous communities’ ancient practices and beliefs. For example, the intricate knowledge of the navigational charts called Māui-ola, used by Polynesian voyagers, ensured the successful exploration and settlement of the vast Pacific Ocean. Likewise, the Maori of New Zealand have exceptional ecological knowledge, which traces its origins to Tāniko, the first ancestor of human beings in Aotearoa. They still observe and engage in traditional practices such as kaitiakitanga (guardianship) to protect the marine environment.

In addition, the indigenous practices of midden management and clam gardening by Pacific Island communities resulted in successful long-term management of the ocean and its resources.

By understanding and incorporating the vast traditional ecological knowledge within marine research, we can learn more about marine life and their habitats, marine pollution, climate change impacts, and sustainable use of marine resources.

A Sustainable Fusion of Oceanography and Indigenous Knowledge

A Sustainable Fusion of Oceanography and Indigenous Knowledge

The conjunction of oceanographic science and Indigenous knowledge presents a unique opportunity for a more comprehensive and holistic study of the ocean. Using the Maori perspective of Ngā Rā Tānu, which encompasses a universe where everything is connected, marine researchers can strike a balance in employing technology while respecting natural resources and the ocean’s serenity.

To forge this union, universities and research institutions must collaborate closely with Indigenous communities to ensure they are keenly involved in the research process. This collaboration would bridge the gap between the academic field and Indigenous communities, helping to empower them by validating their knowledge, whilst extending scientific research methodologies.

Oceanography has come a long way, but the ocean is still a secretive, fascinating world full of beauty and peace. As new advancements lead us further out into the blue expanse, it’s crucial to ensure we tread these waters with respect and understanding. Traditional wisdom and science must unite in harmony, strengthening our knowledge, connection, and responsibility to the ocean and all its intricacies.
