The Impact of Racial and Ethnic Disparities on Health Policy


The United States has long struggled with a multifaceted healthcare system that has both its successes and failures. Unfortunately, it is a fact that this system is plagued by disparities in access to healthcare for various racial and ethnic groups, affecting millions of Americans. These disparities result in suboptimal health outcomes, unequal treatment, and higher healthcare costs. The complexities surrounding these disparities have led to a significant amount of research and attempts to rectify the situation. To grasp the enormity of this problem, it is essential to delve into the existing disparities in healthcare across various racial and ethnic groups. Introduction to Healthcare Disparities Healthcare disparities in America arise from the interplay of societal, systemic, and institutional factors. They encompass various dimensions, including access to healthcare, quality of care, and outcomes. Studies have identified inconsistent and inferior care received by certain racial and ethnic groups. For instance, research has demonstrated that black and Hispanic patients receive fewer preventive care services, which includes regular check-ups, screenings, and vaccinations. Such differential treatment impacts health outcomes negatively, leading to overlooked medical conditions, delayed treatment, and poorer overall health. The extreme instance of maternal mortality among black women, which is more than twice the rate of white women, highlights the dismal state of disparities in healthcare. Determinants of Health Disparities The root causes of racial and ethnic disparities in health policy can be pinned to a mix of factors, all of which stem from overpowering systemic, economic, and environmental determinants. Socioeconomic status significantly emphasizes this notion. People from lower-income backgrounds and minority groups lack the financial resources needed to access healthcare services. Such individuals often receive subpar treatment in hospitals and emergency departments due to underinsured status and ineffective care coordination. Moreover, education plays a critical role, with higher educated people being more likely to recognize symptoms and utilize preventive care.
