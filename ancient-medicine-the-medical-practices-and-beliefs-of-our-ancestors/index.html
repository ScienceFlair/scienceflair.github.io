Ancient Medicine: The Medical Practices and Beliefs of Our Ancestors


Our ancestors, despite being miles apart in time, had intriguing medical practices and beliefs that can often surprise us today. From industrialized nations to developing countries, we’re still learning from the wisdom of ancient cultures. This blog post will not only explore some of these ancients’ knowledge of bodies and diseases but also highlight several contemporary applications.

The Renaissance of Ancient Medicine

It’s crucial to remember that medicine in the ancient world had no scientific basis; therefore, their methods, while intriguing, often faltered. Despite this fact, many modern advancements of healthcare owe their existence to some of these forgotten practices and beliefs. One such area is the use of plant-based medicines. It’s an intriguing concept that ancient peoples cultivated knowledge of healing plants yet were centuries ahead of the West.

One notable example is the Egyptian Ebers Papyrus, a 1500 BCE medical scroll—a treasure trove of ancient medical knowledge. It has more than 700 prescriptions, the majority involving plant materials as medicines. Indigenous cultures across the globe shared similar knowledge, such as the effective use of Willow bark for pain relief or Peppermint for its soothing effects. These practices have renascent into modern pharmaceuticals, providing useful drugs like Aspirin and local anaesthetics.

The Father of Medicine

The Greek physician Hippocrates, referred to as the Father of Medicine, developed the Hippocratic Oath, a Physician’s solemn pledge in practice. He introduced the use of observation and documentation, making evidence-based medicine possible. Medical practices and beliefs of Hippocrates and his followers (known as Hippocratic School) have influenced doctors and health care practitioners worldwide.

One of the innovations the Greeks contributed to medicine was the concept of a ‘medical specialty.’ Specialization in Medicine allowed physicians to access medical procedures more accurately. Early surgical procedures are examples that contributed significantly to the progress of medical science.

Medical Practices in Ancient Civilizations

Ancient civilizations like the Chinese, Indians, and Egyptians integrated spirituality into their medical practices. In contrast, some, like the Romans and Greeks, had lesser spiritual provisions in their medical practices. In modern medicine, Eastern philosophies, such as Ayurveda and Traditional Chinese Medicine, have gained popularity. They are based on ancient medical practices.

One interesting case of Ancient Medicine is autohaemotherapy, which entailed drinking one’s urine, reportedly helpful in treating illnesses. This belief still continues today, albeit with advanced technologies, as blood transfusions and urine tests.

Social Medicine

In ancient times, societal structures had a significant impact on healthcare practices and beliefs. In Rome and Greece, society did not stigmatize diseases as they do today. People with deformities and diseases could freely interact socially. They did not segregate affliction, making it possible for the exchange of medical beliefs and practices.

Furthermore, women, often marginalized by centuries’ worth of male-dominated societies, played a significant role in Ancient Medicine. One significant example is the practice of Midwifery, originally developed as a sister profession to medicine by women who had experienced childbirth themselves.

Conclusion

We may cast our olden days’ practices in a romantic light, but it’s essential to consider life and death were often cruel and swift. While the ancients may lack our scientific understanding, we still owe much to their resourcefulness in the face of often dire circumstances. Their ingenuity laid the foundation for our advanced medicines and technologies. It’s a fascinating reminder of medicine’s adaptability and resilience through the ages. Perhaps by understanding their beliefs and practices, we can create an even better medical system for the future.
