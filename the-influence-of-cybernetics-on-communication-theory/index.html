The Influence of Cybernetics on Communication Theory


As early as the 1940s, the concept of cybernetics began to gain traction, revolutionizing the way humans understood the world and our interactions with it. The term “cybernetics” was coined by mathematician Norbert Wiener, who defined it as the study of “control and communication in the animal and the machine.” Little did Wiener know that his work would have a profound impact on the development of communication theory.

In the 1950s and 1960s, cybernetics began to influence the field of communication studies. Scholars such as Gregory Bateson and Margaret Mead applied cybernetic principles to the study of human communication, exploring the ways in which information is processed, stored, and retrieved. They argued that communication is not just a one-way process, but rather a dynamic and recursive process that involves feedback, control, and adaptation.

One of the key concepts in cybernetics is the idea of the “homeostatic system.” A homeostatic system is a self-regulating system that maintains a stable internal environment despite changes in the external environment. In the context of communication, this means that individuals and groups use feedback mechanisms to adjust their behavior and maintain a sense of stability and coherence.

Cybernetics also introduced the concept of “information” as a fundamental aspect of communication. According to Wiener, information is not just a passive entity, but rather an active force that shapes and directs human behavior. This idea challenged the traditional view of information as a neutral, objective entity, and instead saw it as a dynamic and subjective force that is shaped by the context in which it is communicated.

The influence of cybernetics on communication theory can be seen in several areas. First, it led to a greater emphasis on the role of feedback in communication. Cybernetic scholars argued that feedback is essential for effective communication, as it allows individuals and groups to adjust their behavior and adapt to changing circumstances. This emphasis on feedback has had a lasting impact on the field of communication studies, and continues to shape our understanding of the importance of feedback in communication.

Second, cybernetics introduced the concept of “self-regulation” to the study of communication. According to cybernetic theory, individuals and groups are able to regulate their own behavior through internal feedback mechanisms, without the need for external control. This idea has had a profound impact on our understanding of human communication, and has led to a greater emphasis on the role of individual agency and autonomy in communication.

Finally, cybernetics has influenced the way we think about the relationship between communication and technology. Cybernetic scholars argued that technology is not just a tool for communication, but rather an integral part of the communication process itself. This idea has led to a greater emphasis on the study of technology in communication, and has shaped our understanding of the ways in which technology shapes and is shaped by human communication.

In conclusion, the influence of cybernetics on communication theory has been profound. The concepts of feedback, self-regulation, and information have all had a lasting impact on our understanding of human communication, and continue to shape the field of communication studies today. As we move forward, it is essential that we continue to draw on the insights of cybernetics, and explore the ways in which it can inform and improve our understanding of communication in the digital age.

The influence of cybernetics on communication theory is not limited to the 1950s and 1960s. In the 1970s and 1980s, the concept of “social cybernetics” emerged, which applied cybernetic principles to the study of social systems and social change. This approach emphasized the role of feedback and self-regulation in social systems, and explored the ways in which social systems adapt and change over time.

In the 1990s and 2000s, the concept of “complexity theory” emerged, which built on the insights of cybernetics and social cybernetics. Complexity theory emphasizes the interconnectedness and interdependence of complex systems, and explores the ways in which these systems adapt and change over time. This approach has had a profound impact on the field of communication studies, and has led to a greater emphasis on the study of complex systems and networks.

In recent years, the influence of cybernetics on communication theory has continued to grow. The rise of social media and other digital technologies has led to a greater emphasis on the study of online communication, and the ways in which these technologies shape and are shaped by human communication. Cybernetic principles such as feedback and self-regulation continue to play a key role in this research, as scholars explore the ways in which individuals and groups adapt and change in response to new technologies and social media platforms.

In conclusion, the influence of cybernetics on communication theory is a long and complex one, spanning several decades and multiple disciplines. From its early roots in the 1940s to its continued relevance today, cybernetics has played a profound role in shaping our understanding of human communication. As we move forward, it is essential that we continue to draw on the insights of cybernetics, and explore the ways in which it can inform and improve our understanding of communication in the digital age.
