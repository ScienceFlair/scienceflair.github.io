Marine Turtles and Crocodiles: Our Most Ancient Survivors


When we think about the wonders of the animal kingdom, one would typically focus on the more flamboyant creatures of land, air, and sea. Animals like lions, eagles, and dolphins often take center stage. Yet, beneath the surface of both land and sea, there are two ancient survivors that have managed to persevere through the geological ages: marine turtles and crocodiles. These remarkable reptiles hold incredible secrets that reveal so much about the evolution of our beloved planet and its diversifying ecosystems.

Marine turtles have roamed the Earth for over 100 million years, which places them as some of the oldest living creatures worldwide. They have traveled vast distances across time and space, inhabiting every major ocean of the world. A peculiar sight to see when we think of marine turtles is undoubtedly the sight of their return to the same beach where they initially hatched over two decades or more prior, guided by some mysterious navigational powers – a captivating phenomenon known as “natal homing.”

Their diverse species include the smaller Hawksbill, known for its shy and elusive nature, and the giant Leatherback, capable of diving more than 300 meters under the sea in search of its food. These majestic creatures implant memories in their brains during their first experiences and return to the precise beach where they were born, an act that spans tens of thousands of nautical miles and decades to complete.

These reptiles, classified under the superorder Euarchontoglires, belong to the order Testudines, which comprises more than two dozen species today, despite the global challenges they face. These challenges come in many forms, including pollution, habitat loss, climate change, and the ever-rising threat of poaching for their beautiful shells and meat. Conservation efforts are ongoing, and many organizations work toward protecting these marvelous beings and their unique set of experiences.

On the other hand, crocodiles have also swum the waters of our planet for millions of years – some fossils from Madagascar are not older than 200 million years. Over time, they have evolved and diversified into around 14-24 species of these efficient predators. Crocodiles belong to the order Crocodilia and are closely related to alligators, caimans, gharials, and their relatives.

These beings are not your typical warm-blooded mammals or birds, but their self-awareness, emotions, and even capacity for love have shown them to be complex creatures. For instance, both male and female crocodiles exhibit parent-like care toward their young, guarding and protecting them for as long as two years.

Today, despite their fearsome reputation, many consider crocodiles as majestic and awe-inspiring, though habitat loss and human encroachment remain leading threats to their continued survival. Conservation efforts are vital, and many species of crocodiles are under the protection of national and international laws.

So, when we walk alongside beaches, rivers, and lakes, let us remember that we coexist with our planet’s two ancient survivors, each carrying secrets and profound knowledge from the temporal depths of our Earth. As we face the future together, we must continue our efforts in protecting these living legends before they become no more than relics of the prehistoric past.

Not only are marine turtles and crocodiles significant as ancient species, but they also maintain the delicate ecosystems they call home. From regulating predatory balance to providing sustenance for myriad aquatic species, their importance to ecosystems cannot be overstated.

In a world ever-changing and ever-racing towards a more synthetic future, let us default to a page from the past and offer these ancient beasts the respect and care they deserve in ensuring their continued existence for generations to come. All that’s left is for us to protect their fragile habitats and reigniting the deep-rooted connection we share with them. In doing so, we bring honor to the indelible mark they have left on the chronicles of Earth’s history.
