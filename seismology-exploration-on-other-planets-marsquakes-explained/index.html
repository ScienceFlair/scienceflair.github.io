Seismology Exploration on Other Planets: Marsquakes Explained


The study of earthquakes on Earth, known as seismology, has long been a cornerstone of understanding the planet’s interior. But what happens when we take this science beyond our world? While earthquakes dominate Earth, elsewhere in the solar system, planetary scientists investigate their alien counterparts. On Mars, these seismic events—dubbed “marsquakes”—have given researchers a fresh perspective on the Red Planet’s inner workings. Unveiling their mysteries not only sharpens our understanding of Mars but also enriches our knowledge of planetary evolution across the solar system.

Mars and Its Seismic Secrets

Mars, often considered Earth’s little red sibling, holds many secrets beneath its rust-colored surface. Unlike our planet, it lacks tectonic plates—the massive, convectively driven slabs of rock that shift and collide, generating most earthquakes. Scientists initially assumed this absence might render Mars seismically inactive. However, recent missions have challenged that assumption. Data suggests Mars is far from a dead world; its quakes serve as a window into phenomena both familiar and uncharted.

Just as earthquakes provide clues about Earth’s layers, faults, and molten core, marsquakes offer a similar, albeit alien, map of the Martian interior. By studying seismic activity on Mars, researchers explore questions integral to planetary science: How do planets form and change over time? What remnants of Mars’ geologic past influence its present? And, ultimately, could understanding Mars’ seismicity help us predict conditions on exoplanets and prepare for future human exploration?

NASA’s InSight Mission: Pioneering Martian Seismology

Central to the breakthrough in our understanding of marsquakes was NASA’s InSight (Interior Exploration using Seismic Investigations, Geodesy, and Heat Transport) mission. Launched in 2018, this lander was designed specifically to probe beneath Mars’ surface, offering scientists an unprecedented look at its interior structure. One of InSight’s key instruments is the Seismic Experiment for Interior Structure (SEIS), a highly sensitive seismometer capable of detecting the faint vibrations caused by quakes, meteorite impacts, and even shifting dust.

InSight’s early observations sent ripples through the scientific community. In April 2019, it detected the first-ever marsquake—a historic milestone. Since then, the lander has recorded hundreds of seismic events, each adding a new piece to the planetary puzzle. While most detected marsquakes have been relatively small compared to Earth’s quakes, their diversity tells a rich story. Some seem to originate within the crust, while others come from deep in the mantle. This range in depth and intensity has provided critical insights into how energy moves through Mars’ subsurface.

The Anatomy of a Marsquake

Unlike their terrestrial counterparts, marsquakes are primarily caused by the gradual cooling of the planet, which contracts the crust and creates stress that fractures the surface. On Earth, plate tectonics and volcanic activity dominate the seismic landscape, but Mars lacks the same driving forces. Instead, its quakes manifest from the stresses within a single, rigid planetary shell—akin to a cooling, cracking bowl.

Interestingly, Mars’ crust and mantle act like amplifying mediums for seismic waves. Due to the planet’s thinner atmosphere and quieter environment, its seismic signals can travel much farther than they might on Earth. This quietude has made Mars an ideal laboratory for studying even subtle seismic phenomena that might be drowned out by Earth’s geological “noise.”

Among the notable seismic behaviors observed were “high-frequency” marsquakes, which resemble shallow, localized tremors on Earth, and “low-frequency” events, indicative of more profound and powerful movements in the mid-mantle region. These marsquakes revealed Mars is likely still geologically active in some capacity, overturning long-held assumptions of a planet frozen in time.

Lessons from Marsquakes: What Do They Tell Us?

Marsquakes have revolutionized researchers’ understanding of the Red Planet’s interior. A critical finding concerns Mars’ core, which has been revealed as larger and less dense than initially estimated. The seismic waves passing through the core hint at a liquid, iron-rich composition complemented by lighter elements like sulfur. This discovery provides invaluable clues not just about Mars’ past but also about the geological forces that shaped its entire planetary system billions of years ago.

Moreover, seismic data has sketched a layered portrait of Mars’ lithosphere, mantle, and crust. The planet’s crust, much thicker than Earth’s, supports the idea of an ancient, more volcanically active Mars with resources once capable of supporting liquid water. The mantle beneath hints at lingering heat—incredibly subtle yet still influential—and it raises questions about whether Mars could still sustain volcanic activity in specific regions.

Perhaps most intriguingly, these findings shed light on one of Mars’ most enduring mysteries: the lack of a global magnetic field. Unlike Earth, Mars lost its magnetic shield early in its history, leaving it exposed to solar winds and earning its atmosphere-stripping fate. By studying marsquakes and the thermal processes occurring deep in the planet, scientists aim to better understand why this magnetic field vanished and how it might relate to habitable conditions for ancient Martian life.

Comparative Planetology: Bridging Martian and Terrestrial Worlds

The study of marsquakes does more than illuminate Mars itself; it offers a broader comparative view of planetary evolution. By juxtaposing the seismic and geological differences between Earth and Mars, researchers deepen their understanding of why two planets with shared origins took such dramatically different paths.

Earth’s dynamic tectonics fuel ongoing volcanic activity, renewing its surface and replenishing its atmosphere. In contrast, Mars’ lack of tectonic mobility left it geologically inert for much of its recorded history. By studying these divergent paths, scientists can piece together the story of planet formation in the broader solar system, unveiling the critical factors that decide a planet’s fate.

This comparative approach extends outward as well. Jupiter’s moon Europa and Saturn’s moon Enceladus, considered prime targets in the search for extraterrestrial life, may also experience “quakes” beneath their icy surfaces. A robust understanding of how seismicity operates on Mars could help interpret data from future missions to these worlds, contributing to a better understanding of their geology and potential habitability.

Challenges in Detecting and Interpreting Marsquakes

Despite the many successes of missions like InSight, studying seismic activity on another planet is far from straightforward. For one, Mars’ low surface pressure and diminished atmospheric density make it a challenging target for traditional seismology tools. The signals picked up by SEIS must also compete with noise from wind, temperature fluctuations, and operational vibrations, complicating their analysis.

Additionally, the lack of a global seismic network poses unique difficulties. On Earth, seismologists deploy a grid of instruments worldwide to triangulate and study quake sources. On Mars, by contrast, all data comes from a single lander. Although highly sophisticated, InSight’s instruments have limitations, requiring researchers to adapt their methods and model seismic activity with less precision than they can achieve on Earth.

An even bigger obstacle arose when Mars’ persistent coating of dust began to affect InSight’s solar panels, limiting the lander’s power supply and forcing NASA to wind down operations in late 2022. While the mission delivered an incredible wealth of data, its conclusions remain limited to what it could achieve during its active lifespan. Future efforts will likely build on InSight’s framework while tackling power generation and instrumentation challenges in new ways.

Seismic Insights for Human Exploration and Beyond

Practical applications of Martian seismology extend beyond academic research. For eventual human missions to Mars, understanding marsquakes is essential for safety and infrastructure planning. Quakes—especially high-amplitude events—could affect underground habitats, robotics, or resource extraction efforts. With the detailed data collected so far, mission planners can better anticipate how Mars’ shifting terrain might influence long-term colonization efforts.

Looking beyond Mars, the ongoing exploration of planetary seismicity could also enhance humanity’s approach to other worlds. Whether unlocking answers about Venus’ scorching landscape, the moon’s cooling crust, or Europa’s hidden ocean, seismology acts as a universal lens for studying planetary bodies.

The Trembling Future of Planetary Science

Marsquakes mark an exciting chapter in planetary exploration. From InSight’s quiet observations of Mars’ subsurface to the ambitious dreams of applying its lessons elsewhere, the role of seismology in unraveling the secrets of the cosmos is just beginning. Each quake reverberates with opportunity, carrying messages from deep underground that speak of the forces shaping not only Mars but countless other worlds awaiting exploration.

As we listen more closely to these distant planetary tremors, we come one step closer to deciphering the universe’s geologic rhythms—a journey that opens pathways to understanding our own origins and, perhaps, the landscapes of worlds yet untouched. Marsquakes, in all their subtlety, remind us to look beneath the surface, for sometimes the quietest whispers hold the most profound stories.
