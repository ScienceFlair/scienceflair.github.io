Exploring Ethical Issues in Global Health Equity


When it comes to addressing global health equity, there is no shortage of pressing challenges and difficult questions. But, perhaps the most complex aspect of striving for health equity is navigating the myriad of ethical issues that arise along the way.

In the three key areas of 1) Resource Allocation, 2) Research and Development, and 3) Access to Health Care, we grapple with some of the most thought-provoking and contentious ethical dilemmas.

Resource Allocation

Allocating health resources responsibly presents one of the first barriers to global health equity. How do we prioritize which countries, regions, and communities receive what resources, in a way that is fair and effective?

The Lancet Commission on Global Health estimates that a fund of $200 billion should be spent on achieving universal health coverage globally – a significant investment, but one that may not be forthcoming from developed countries. There’s an unspoken ethical dilemma in discussing who should bear the burden of this huge cost. Should it be the wealthier nations that have profited from producing and selling healthcare commodities? Or should it be those who are least able to pay?

Research and Development

The next issue is research and development for global health. New drugs and medical treatments must be developed to create a healthier and more robust global citizenry, but who gets to benefit and who pays for these innovations?

Drug development is capital-intensive and involves sizeable investments. Smaller, developing countries, or those with less purchasing power, are typically excluded from reaping the benefits of these innovations, despite having a disproportionately high burden of diseases. It’s a classic example of a catch-22 situation.

Access to Healthcare

Lastly, and perhaps most noticeably, is access to health care. Despite the WHO’s Universal Health Coverage (UHC) initiative, many low-resource settings continue to grapple with questions of healthcare access.

The principle of non-discriminatory access, even across borders, is an admirable goal. However, practical realities, such as a lack of infrastructure, qualified personnel, and financial commitment, make it difficult to achieve. The challenge is in finding ways to justify and allocate the available resources in an equitable and fair manner.

Global Health in a Nutshell

Global health equity is an elusive goal, but it’s one we can’t afford to ignore. The challenge of making health systems more equitable and fair globally is not just an architectural or financial one; it’s also a moral one. As we strive for advancements in medical services, we must ensure that we don’t create new inequalities or exacerbate existing ones.

With these points in mind, it becomes clear that addressing the inequalities within health systems globally will take a concerted effort: one that involves partnerships with stakeholders and all the necessary institutions, a healthy dose of long-term commitment, and a deep understanding of the moral dilemmas that each step presents.

We’re still working on finding solutions and making headway, but these challenges should be as much a rallying cry as they are a sobering reminder of how far we still have to go.
