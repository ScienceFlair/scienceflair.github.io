How to Perform a Motion Study for Industrial Process Improvements


Industrial process improvements are essential to boost productivity, reduce waste, and optimize operations. One of the most effective methods for enhancing efficiency is performing a motion study. This systematic approach focuses on analyzing the movements involved in a process to identify inefficiencies, reduce unnecessary actions, and streamline workflows. In this post, we’ll explore how to conduct a motion study for industrial process improvements, step by step, to ensure your processes are operating at their highest potential.

What is a Motion Study?

A motion study involves the detailed examination and analysis of the physical movements involved in a task or process. The goal is to eliminate unnecessary motions, optimize the sequence of tasks, and identify opportunities to simplify procedures to improve productivity. The methodology used in motion studies is closely linked to principles of industrial engineering, such as time and motion studies, which analyze both the time taken for tasks and the motions required to complete them.

Why Conduct a Motion Study?

There are several reasons why motion studies are crucial for industrial process improvement:

  • Eliminate Wasted Effort: By analyzing movements, a motion study helps identify tasks that can be eliminated, combined, or simplified to reduce effort and time.
  • Enhance Ergonomics: Motion studies can highlight tasks that may cause unnecessary strain or discomfort, offering insights into how to design more ergonomic workstations or methods.
  • Boost Productivity: By optimizing processes, motion studies can help reduce cycle times, thereby increasing overall productivity.
  • Improve Worker Safety: Identifying and addressing inefficient motions or hazardous practices can improve the safety and well-being of workers.

Steps to Conduct a Motion Study

Performing a motion study requires a structured approach. Here’s a step-by-step guide to conducting a motion study for industrial process improvements:

1. Define the Objective of the Study

Before you begin, it’s important to clearly define the goal of the motion study. Are you trying to improve efficiency, reduce waste, enhance ergonomics, or all of the above? By setting specific objectives, you can focus your efforts and align your study with the overall goals of the organization.

2. Select the Process to Study

Choose the specific process or task you want to analyze. Ideally, this should be a repetitive task where there is potential for significant improvement. The task could be part of a larger process, such as assembly, material handling, or equipment maintenance.

Ensure that the process selected is representative of your overall production workflow, as the insights you gain can often be applied to similar tasks across your operation.

3. Gather Data on the Current Process

Before making improvements, it’s essential to collect data on the current process. This will serve as your baseline and help you track improvements over time. There are various ways to gather data, including:

  • Observation: Watch the process as it occurs and take detailed notes on each step involved.
  • Time Measurement: Record how long each step takes and whether there are any delays.
  • Motion Capture: Use tools or video recordings to track the worker’s movements and identify inefficiencies.
  • Interviews: Speak with workers or operators to gain insight into the process and identify potential pain points.

4. Break the Process Down into Tasks and Movements

Once you have gathered sufficient data, break the process down into individual tasks and motions. Analyze each step to determine whether it is necessary, whether it can be combined with other tasks, or if it can be eliminated entirely.

In this phase, focus on categorizing the types of motions involved. For example, you can classify motions as:

  • Grasping: Reaching for tools or materials.
  • Transporting: Moving items from one location to another.
  • Positioning: Adjusting an item or part for the next step in the process.
  • Assembly: Putting parts together.
  • Disposal: Removing or discarding waste or materials.

5. Identify and Eliminate Wasteful Movements

One of the primary goals of a motion study is to eliminate unnecessary motions, which often result in wasted time and effort. For each movement identified in the process, ask whether it adds value to the task. If not, consider ways to eliminate or reduce it.

There are several types of waste that motion studies often aim to eliminate:

  • Excessive motion: Movements that are redundant or unnecessary, such as walking back and forth.
  • Motion inefficiency: Movements that could be more fluid, reducing the time spent on each task.
  • Excessive waiting: Delays that occur between actions or during transitions from one step to another.

Look for patterns where tasks can be rearranged or combined to minimize movement, such as adjusting workstations to reduce the distance workers need to travel.

6. Analyze Ergonomics

As you analyze the movements involved in the process, it’s also crucial to consider the ergonomic impact of each motion. Poor ergonomics can lead to repetitive strain injuries, worker fatigue, and lower productivity over time.

Identify motions or postures that may lead to discomfort or potential injury. For example, frequent bending, reaching overhead, or twisting the body can cause long-term harm to workers. The motion study should explore ways to modify these movements, such as:

  • Adjusting the height of workstations to reduce bending.
  • Reorganizing tools and materials to minimize reaching.
  • Introducing mechanical aids, such as hoists or conveyors, to reduce lifting.

7. Develop an Improved Process

Based on your analysis, develop an improved version of the process that minimizes unnecessary motions and enhances ergonomics. This might involve:

  • Reorganizing the workspace: Arrange tools and materials in a way that reduces the number of movements required during the process.
  • Automating repetitive tasks: Introduce machines or automated systems to perform repetitive motions, allowing workers to focus on higher-value tasks.
  • Redesigning tasks: Break down complex tasks into smaller steps that require fewer movements or less effort.

For example, if you identified that workers were spending too much time walking to a tool station, consider relocating the tools closer to the work area to reduce the number of steps needed.

8. Test and Implement Changes

Once you’ve developed an improved process, it’s time to test the changes. Implement the new process on a small scale and measure the results. Monitor the workers as they perform the tasks and track whether the changes lead to improvements in efficiency, time, and ergonomics.

It’s essential to involve workers in this phase, as they may provide valuable feedback on the feasibility of the changes and offer suggestions for further improvement. Make sure that they have the necessary training and tools to adopt the new methods.

9. Evaluate and Monitor Progress

After implementing the changes, continuously evaluate the progress and impact of the motion study. Use the same methods you employed initially (such as time measurements and worker feedback) to assess whether the improvements are delivering the desired results.

Regular monitoring will ensure that the process remains efficient and sustainable in the long run. Additionally, you can continue to make refinements based on real-time data and observations, allowing you to make continuous improvements.

10. Document and Standardize the New Process

Once the new process has been successfully implemented and optimized, document all changes and establish standard operating procedures (SOPs). These documents should outline the improved process and any new equipment, tools, or workflows introduced.

By standardizing the improved process, you ensure that all workers follow the same steps, leading to consistent results and continued efficiency improvements. Additionally, documenting the changes makes it easier to train new employees and maintain best practices across the organization.

Conclusion

A motion study is a powerful tool for industrial process improvement, helping organizations eliminate inefficiencies, reduce waste, and enhance worker productivity. By following a systematic approach and focusing on both motion and ergonomics, you can significantly improve the effectiveness of your processes. From analyzing the current workflow to implementing changes, motion studies provide actionable insights that lead to tangible improvements in your operations.

Remember, the key to successful motion studies lies in continuous evaluation and adaptation. As processes evolve, periodic reviews and adjustments will ensure that your operations stay at the cutting edge of efficiency and safety.
