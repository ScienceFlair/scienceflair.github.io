Do Sanctions Actually Work? An Economic Analysis


Sanctions have been a cornerstone of international law and diplomacy for centuries, used by nations to influence the behavior of other countries and shape global events. The concept of economic sanctions has evolved over time, but their purpose has remained the same: to persuade another country to change its policies or actions by restricting its access to foreign goods, services, and credits. But do sanctions actually work? In this post, we'll delve into the effectiveness of economic sanctions and provide an in-depth analysis of their impact on the targeted country's economy, GDP, and global trade. Economic Sanctions and Their Various Types Economic sanctions are a powerful tool in the arsenal of international relations, with historians identifying six categories of sanctions: traditional sanctions, selective restrictions, sectoral sanctions, freezing assets, blocking corporations, and asymmetric sanctions.

Traditional sanctions, also known as comprehensive sanctions, are blanket restrictions placed on all exports and imports of a targeted country, covering all sectors and segments of its economy. Selective sanctions, on the other hand, focus on crippling specific sectors of the targeted economy, such as oil, metals, defense equipment, or technology. Sectoral sanctions, similar to sectoral sanctions, target specific sectors but only suspend certain types of economic exchanges, leaving the broader sector intact. Freezing assets involves freezing the assets of targeted individuals, organizations, or entities, usually in financial institutions. Blocking corporations restricts specific businesses or companies from developing economic relationships with a targeted country or entity. Asymmetric sanctions target countries or individuals that support a targeted regime by using weaker sanctions, like boycotts or visa bans.

The Impact of Economic Sanctions on GDP One of the most critical metrics for evaluating the effectiveness of economic sanctions is how they affect the targeted country's GDP. Since World War II, numerous studies have shown that sanctions have a minor impact on GDP, amounting to a 0.5-2% decline, with the average drop being around 1%. This is due in part to the fact that countries have diversified their economic interests and have access to substitute markets and suppliers. However, if sanctions are prolonged or imposed during critical economic periods, resulting in disruptions to global supply chains and commodity shortages, the impact can be more pronounced. Sanctions can exacerbate existing economic challenges, such as inflation, unemployment, and changes in currency exchange rates, which might lead to significant economic losses. Do Sanctions Have a Lasting Impact on Global Trade? While some argue that sanctions severely limit global trade and hinder economic growth, others claim that trade is resilient and can bypass traditional barriers. Historical examples of successful trading channels and resilient industries, such as China's double-digit economic growth in the face of heavy sanctions and quagmiring going-over the past two decades, offer compelling evidence of trade's adaptability. Case in point: Taiwan's economy has seen tremendous growth despite being isolated by China. Experts argue that Taiwan's neoliberal policies have enabled the country to remain adept in the rapidly globalized market, defying predictions of global sanctions hindering commerce. Metric to Measure Sanction Effectiveness Measuring the effectiveness of economic sanctions is crucial in determining their impact. Import substitution: examining the degree to which a country replaces foreign goods with domestic products in the face of sanctions. Trade diversion: determining the extent to which trade shifts from sanctioned countries to other markets.
