Marine Invasions: Biologists Race Against Time to understand and Prevent Invasive Species


The vast expanse of the ocean covers two-thirds of our planet Earth. Despite this seemingly unending expanse, the ocean suffers from a growing problem that could potentially lead to catastrophe: Marine invasions by invasive species. These invaders often arrive uninvited via ship ballast water or by stowing away on vessels, wreaking havoc on our ecosystems and economies. Biologists are in a race against time to understand these invaders in order to prevent long-term damage to our world’s vulnerable marine environments.

Invasive species are non-native organisms that arrive in an ecosystem where they did not naturally occur, and they can have severe and devastating consequences.1 These species outcompete native species for food, space, and refuge, leading to a decrease in biodiversity and the disappearance of native species.2 For instance, the infamous zebra mussel (Dreissena polymorpha) native to the Caspian Sea, has invaded many waterways around the world causing billions of dollars in damages and disrupting ecosystems.3 It’s a tale of a single species utilizing its adaptability and reproduction prowess to create a domino effect with the potential to impact global fishery exports.

One of the key areas in understanding an invasive species is to study its behavior, growth rates, and reproduction patterns. For example, Australian silverstripe rabitfish (Siganus argenteus) allows biologist researchers to assess its potential invasion capacities in various Mediterranean areas.4 Understanding the specific species behaviors makes it possible to predict and test their impacts on local ecosystems.

Preventing invasions from occurring is of the utmost importance. To prevent further invasions biologists study ballast water,5 a common transport mechanism for invasive species. Protections like treating ballast water with UV light or heat, ensuring line-use gear is inspected on fishing boats to remove hitchhikers, and regulating the movement of ships and their cargo can significantly reduce the risk of invasions.6 Many countries have already implemented ballast water management programs in ports and coastal areas which control the release of non-native species into our oceans.7

Another way to mitigate the impacts of invasive species is to develop biological control methods. These methods involve the introduction of another species to control the invasive one. An example of successful biological control is the cactus moth (Cactoblastis cactorum), native to South America, which was introduced to Australia in 1925 to combat the spiny pear cactus and was successful in controlling it.8 We must, however, exercise caution when implementing such strategies as unintended consequences may lead to even more complex ecological issues if not managed correctly.

Not only are biologists racing against time to understand invasive species, but conservationists are fighting to preserve the beauty and intricacies of our marine environments.9 The potential destruction of marine ecosystems by invasive species is a daunting challenge that requires cooperation and dedication from scientists, policy makers, and every individual on this planet. We must take proactive steps to mitigate the threat of marine invasions and salvage the vibrancy of our ocean’s ecosystems.

There is still much to learn about marine invasions and invasive species, but it’s our responsibility to understand and protect this breathtaking world. The time is now for biologists to work tirelessly on unraveling the mysteries of invasive species and to prevent them from declaring war on marine ecosystems. As we forge forward, the future of our oceans relies on our ability to understand these threats and implement viable solutions before we lose any more of our planet’s marine gems.
