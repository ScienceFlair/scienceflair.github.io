The Role of Insects in Biodiversity: Why We Need Them


Insects are essential to the web of life. They often go unnoticed or are considered mere nuisances, but their value to ecosystems, agriculture, and even human survival is immeasurable. If we take a closer look at their roles in biodiversity, it becomes clear that insects are not just integral to the natural world—they are indispensable.

Insects as Pollinators: Silent Architects of Our Ecosystems

More than 75% of the world’s flowering plant species rely on animal pollinators, and a significant share of that responsibility falls on the humble shoulders (or wings) of insects. Bees, butterflies, beetles, flies, and moths are among the most effective of these pollinating species. By transferring pollen from one flower to another, they ensure the reproduction of plants that form the basis of ecosystems around the globe.

The significance of this process extends far beyond wild flora. Pollinator insects are responsible for the successful cultivation of a wide array of crops, including fruits, nuts, and vegetables. Without them, food production would plummet, affecting millions of humans and animals dependent on these crops for sustenance. Crops like almonds, apples, and tomatoes often depend on bees and similar insect species to grow at the scale humanity has come to rely upon.

Unfortunately, habitat loss, pesticide use, and climate change are causing sharp declines in pollinator populations. If we fail to protect these quiet workers, the ripple effects could devastate ecosystems and global food security alike.

Decomposers and Soil Health: The Insects Beneath Our Feet

Long after we’ve enjoyed a meal, insects get to work cleaning up what we leave behind. Species like dung beetles, ants, and various flies are nature’s ultimate recycling team, breaking down organic waste such as dead plants, feces, and animal carcasses. This process does more than just tidy up the environment—it enriches the soil with nutrients, making it fertile for future plant growth.

Soil health is the foundation of productive agriculture and robust ecosystems. Without the decomposition services provided by insects, organic matter would pile up, and soil would eventually become depleted of the nutrients needed to support plant and microbial life. This, in turn, would affect the entire food chain, disrupting ecosystems from the bottom up.

As keystone species in many decomposer communities, insects play an essential yet silent role in the nutrient cycling that fuels biodiversity. Protecting these species is critical for maintaining the health and resilience of the planet’s soils.

Insects as a Food Source: Sustaining Biodiversity

Insects are a dietary staple for many creatures, from birds and amphibians to reptiles and fish. They form the base of many food chains, supporting larger species whose survival depends on the abundance of insect populations. For example, migratory songbirds rely heavily on caterpillars and other insects to build the energy reserves needed for their long-distance journeys.

Beyond serving other species, insects have also become an important food source for humans. Edible insects such as crickets, mealworms, and grasshoppers are rich in protein, vitamins, and minerals. They present an eco-friendly alternative to traditional livestock farming due to their lower greenhouse gas emissions and minimal resource requirements. With the global population continuing to rise, insect-based food products could help meet the demand for sustainable protein sources.

This reliance on insects as a food source emphasizes how their well-being directly affects biodiversity and human survival alike. Without a stable population of insects, entire ecosystems could collapse.

Natural Pest Control: The Insects That Protect Crops

Not all insects are pests; in fact, many help control pest populations and protect crops. Predatory insects such as ladybugs, beetles, and praying mantises feed on aphids, caterpillars, and other agricultural pests, providing a natural and eco-friendly alternative to chemical pesticides. Parasitic wasps, too, play a role by laying their eggs inside pest species like caterpillars, curbing their populations in the process.

This natural pest control contributes significantly to maintaining plant health and reducing crop losses. By promoting insect biodiversity on farms, farmers can enjoy the benefits of ecosystem services without over-reliance on synthetic chemicals, which can harm non-target species and even human health. This balance is vital for sustainable farming practices and conservation efforts.

Insects as Indicators of Environmental Health

Insects are not just participants in ecosystems—they are also highly effective indicators of environmental health. Because they are sensitive to changes in temperature, moisture, and chemical composition, shifts in insect populations often signal broader problems in the environment. For example, a decline in aquatic insects such as mayflies or dragonflies can indicate pollution or habitat degradation in freshwater ecosystems.

Scientists often use insect biodiversity and abundance to monitor habitats and assess the impact of human activities, such as deforestation or urbanization. By understanding what happens to insect populations, we gain valuable insights into how ecosystems are faring as a whole.

Cultural and Economic Contributions

Beyond ecological benefits, insects have inspired human cultures for centuries. From honeybees producing honey and beeswax to silk moths yielding the raw material for silk, insects have been key contributors to human economies. They feature prominently in art, mythology, and folklore, symbolizing attributes such as resilience, transformation, and industriousness.

In recent years, advancements in science have revealed even more potential for insects to benefit humanity. Consider the applications of spider silk in biotechnology or the use of ants in studying navigation and collective intelligence. Whether through their direct contributions to industry or indirect benefits to biodiversity, insects continue to fuel human innovation and well-being.

Urgency in Conservation Efforts

The role insects play in biodiversity cannot be overstated, yet they face a multitude of threats that imperil their survival. Habitat destruction, pesticide overuse, climate change, and invasive species are causing widespread declines in their populations. The “insect apocalypse,” as scientists have termed it, could unravel ecosystems and agricultural systems that depend on these creatures.

Efforts to protect insects must include conserving natural habitats, reducing the use of harmful chemicals, and fostering sustainable farming practices. Policies that encourage biodiversity and public awareness campaigns can help people understand why insects are vital to life on Earth. Simple actions like planting native flowers or reducing pesticide use in gardens can make a measurable difference.

Creating safe corridors for insect movement and investing in ecological research are other critical steps needed to support insect populations. By prioritizing the health of these small but powerful creatures, we can ensure the stability and resilience of Earth’s ecosystems for generations to come.

A World Built on the Backs of Insects

Insects might not draw the same admiration as tigers or whales, but they are no less significant in supporting life on Earth. They pollinate, recycle nutrients, control pests, and serve as vital food sources—all indispensable functions that underpin biodiversity. Each beetle, bee, and butterfly plays a role in maintaining the intricate balance of our ecosystems.

When we advocate for conservation, we must include insects in the conversation. Protecting these unsung heroes is not just an environmental imperative—it is a responsibility toward ourselves and future generations. A world without insects would undoubtedly be a less vibrant, less functional, and less habitable place. It is time we give insects the recognition they deserve and take decisive steps to safeguard their future.

Every wingbeat matters.
