Sticky Situations: The Advantages and Perils of Adhesion in the Animal Kingdom


The animal kingdom is filled with fascinating creatures, each uniquely equipped to navigate their environments in pursuit of survival. One such remarkable adaptation is the ability to adhere or stick to different surfaces. From geckos effortlessly climbing walls to the sneaky sticky feet of some insects, the marvels of adhesion in the animal world are nothing short of extraordinary.

Adhesion, essentially the tendency of dissimilar particles or surfaces to cling to one another, plays a crucial role in various aspects of an animal’s life. It aids in climbing, hunting, sensory perception, and even courtship. Delving into the countless advantages and perils of adhesion, let us explore some of the most captivating sticky situations that animals encounter.

First on the list are our humble housemates - the spiders and daddy longlegs. These arachnids have remarkably sticky feet that allow them to traverse a myriad of surfaces. Many spiders deploy a adhesive secreted by the spigots on the signature fangs, but unlike insects that have a multi-purpose mouthful, spiders rely solely on those two hinged appendages to get to their meal, and trust me, they are very meticulous about it.

Arguably one of the most impressive examples of adhesion is found in geckos, such as the tokay gecko (Gekko gecko) and the Madagascar day gecko (Phelsuma madagascariensis). Their toes are covered with millions of hair-like structures known as setae, which further break down into even tinier structures called spatulae. These delicate spatulae form van der Waals bonds with surfaces, granting the geckos the ability to stick to practically anything without expending much energy. This feature has inspired numerous human applications, such as the development of “gecko tape.”

Another enchanting case study in the realm of adhesion is the gecko’s close relative, the tree frog. Frogs like the Araivorandan mint frog (Boophis mentissimus) showcase a similar adhesive system that allows them to cling to wet surfaces without any problems. In contrast, the rock hyrax (Procavia capensis), a furry, herbivorous mammal, brings a different twist on adhesion by utilizing its clawed feet for gripping and maintaining a secure foothold on rocky surfaces.

Insects, too, have their fair share of sticky exploits. Take, for example, the brown marmorated stink bug (Halyomorpha halys), which holds an uncanny ability to adhere to any surface in search of a suitable place to lay eggs. Meanwhile, the achievement of the whip spider (Theraphosidae) is its ability to stick to just about any surface and maneuver with ease, all the while demonstrating inexplicable dexterity.

When it comes to mating, some insects opt for the stickiness route to secure their partners. The male bolas spider (Masteria boliviana) utilizes a modified sticky silk to catch both flying prey and prospective mates. Not only that, but the morpho butterflies demonstrate the same tactic by males using their hind legs coated in a mucous substance, allowing them to stick to receptive females.

However, adhesion is not always advantageous. For some organisms, excessive stickiness can lead to negative consequences. Consider the adorable yet potentially destructive Woodpecker ant (Temnothorax crassispinosus). These ants apply sticky droplets on seed legs to manipulate the fodder and prevent it from falling, but sometimes they affix themselves to unintended surfaces, including each other and their trapped seeds.

Moreover, the famous sugar glider (Petaurus breviceps) possum faces a similar challenge while navigating their environment. While their adhesive membranes enable them to glide through the air, they can become stuck or entangled if they accidentally land on trees with sticky secretions from the cotton trees (Cochlospermum spp.).

It turns out that adhesion has its downsides. In a previously mentioned example, the bathroom wall-climbing gecko might face difficulty in finding its way back down due to its inherent affinity for sticking to surfaces. The same goes for other creatures, who might display impaired movement or even become trapped by their own sticky means of locomotion.

These sticky situations are a testament to the complexity of adhesion in the animal kingdom. Adapting to various environments and predicaments, these fascinating creatures showcase the interesting advantages and perils of adhesion. Whether it’s sticking to walls, gliding through the sky, or capturing a mate, the wildlife we coexist with is indeed a marvel of natures ingenuity. Just imagine what more we can learn from our animalistic friends.
