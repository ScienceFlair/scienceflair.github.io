Conservation Prioritization: How Biologists Decide What to Save


Conservation biology is a field fraught with urgency and difficult choices. With shrinking budgets, limited resources, and an overwhelming number of species and ecosystems under threat, how exactly do biologists decide what to save? This vital question sits at the heart of conservation efforts across the globe. Prioritizing conservation targets is not just a matter of emotional appeal or charisma of species—it is a sophisticated process that blends science, economics, ethics, and practical feasibility.

In this exploration, we’ll dive deep into the world of conservation prioritization, uncovering the principles, methods, and real-world strategies that guide biologists in their mission to preserve biodiversity.

The Challenge of Limited Resources

Conservation needs are immense. The natural world is facing a biodiversity crisis often called the Sixth Mass Extinction. Habitat loss, climate change, pollution, invasive species, and unsustainable exploitation are pushing countless species to the brink. Governments and organizations cannot fund every conservation project or save every species facing extinction. This scarcity forces biologists and conservationists into a triage process where they must evaluate and prioritize efforts to maximize positive outcomes.

Foundations of Conservation Prioritization

Understanding Biodiversity Value

The first step is determining what is worth prioritizing. Not all species or ecosystems carry the same ecological, genetic, or cultural value. Several dimensions come into play:

  • Ecological significance: Some species act as keystone species, whose presence or absence dramatically affects ecosystem function. Protecting a keystone species can have ripple effects that benefit a broad range of organisms.

  • Evolutionary uniqueness: Species representing unique branches of the tree of life, with few close relatives, may be given higher priority because their loss means losing entire evolutionary lineages.

  • Endemism and rarity: Species found only in limited areas (endemics) or those with very small populations may be prioritized due to their heightened vulnerability.

  • Cultural and economic importance: Species or ecosystems that provide cultural identity or essential ecosystem services can be valued for their direct or indirect contributions to human well-being.

Setting Conservation Objectives

Prioritization begins with clear goals: Are we aiming to maximize species saved? Preserve ecosystem services? Protect evolutionary potential? Different objectives lead to markedly different strategies.

Some common conservation objectives include:

  • Preventing species extinctions: Focused on saving species on the verge of disappearing.

  • Preserving ecosystem function: Ensuring ecosystems continue to operate and provide services like clean water, carbon storage, and pollination.

  • Maintaining genetic diversity: Safeguarding the total genetic variation within and among populations to support resilience.

  • Fostering sustainable human-nature interactions: Balancing conservation goals with local community needs.

Factors Influencing Prioritization

Certain factors are systematically weighed when making decisions, such as:

  • Threat level: How immediate and severe is the threat to a species or habitat?

  • Probability of success: What are the chances that conservation measures will be effective?

  • Cost of intervention: What resources (financial, human, technological) are needed?

  • Reversibility: Can the population or ecosystem recover if threats are mitigated?

  • Social and political contexts: Are local people supportive? Is there political will?

By assessing these factors, biologists and conservation planners can better allocate limited resources.

Quantitative Approaches: From Theory to Practice

Qualitative judgments alone cannot guide modern conservation sufficiently. Quantitative frameworks provide transparency and repeatability. Several sophisticated models and tools have been developed:

1. Priority Indexes

One seminal approach is the development of ranking systems or priority indexes that score species or areas based on multiple criteria, such as threat level, irreplaceability, and feasibility. These scores then guide investment decisions.

Examples include the EDGE of Existence program which prioritizes species based on evolutionary distinctiveness and global endangerment status, highlighting “EDGE species” for urgent conservation.

2. Systematic Conservation Planning

This approach aims to select protected areas that together maximize biodiversity conservation while minimizing costs or conflicts. Tools like Marxan, a spatial optimization software, help identify reserve networks that efficiently protect targets under constraints.

3. Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

Cost-effectiveness balances the cost of conservation actions against expected benefits, such as the number of species saved or hectares protected. It helps ensure resources go where they deliver the biggest return on investment.

4. Metapopulation and Viability Models

Biologists use population viability analyses (PVAs) to model population trends under different scenarios. This helps assess species viability and the relative impact of conservation interventions, supporting informed prioritization.

Case Studies in Conservation Prioritization

Saving Madagascar’s Lemurs

Madagascar is home to hundreds of lemur species, many of which are critically endangered. Conservation biologists had to prioritize among many candidates. They focused on species with restricted ranges, high evolutionary uniqueness, and where community-led conservation was feasible. This multi-criteria process guided finite funds toward projects balancing biological importance and local engagement.

Coral Reef Protection

Coral reefs provide vital services but face existential threats from bleaching and pollution. Conservation planners employ spatial planning tools to select reef areas that maximize biodiversity preservation while minimizing human conflicts. Priority areas often combine high coral diversity with resilience to climate change, directing restoration and protection efforts.

The Human Dimension: Ethics, Equity, and Stakeholder Engagement

Prioritization is not merely logical calculation; it profoundly impacts human lives. Decisions affect indigenous peoples, local communities, and stakeholders with diverse values and livelihoods tied to nature.

Effective prioritization involves:

  • Incorporating Indigenous Knowledge: Indigenous peoples often have deep ecological knowledge crucial for success.

  • Ensuring Social Justice: Prioritization should avoid marginalizing vulnerable communities and strive for equitable benefit sharing.

  • Building Durable Partnerships: Long-term conservation requires buy-in from local stakeholders to sustain initiatives.

The Role of Emerging Technologies

Technological advances increasingly inform prioritization:

  • Environmental DNA (eDNA) allows rapid biodiversity assessments in hard-to-access environments.

  • Remote sensing through satellites provides detailed data on habitat change and ecosystem health.

  • Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning enhance predictions of species distributions and threat dynamics.

These tools help refine prioritization by improving data breadth and analysis power.

Challenges and Controversies

Prioritization raises difficult questions and faces criticism:

  • Charismatic Bias: Popular “flagship” species may attract funds at the expense of less-known but equally important species.

  • Static vs Dynamic Priorities: Changing environmental conditions mean priorities must adapt, but decision-making processes can lag.

  • Ethical Quandaries: Deciding to allocate resources in a way that condemns some species or habitats raises moral concerns.

Ongoing dialogue among scientists, policymakers, and the public is essential to navigate these complexities.

Toward Adaptive and Inclusive Prioritization

Conservation is an ever-evolving landscape. Adaptive prioritization frameworks respond dynamically to new threats, scientific discoveries, and social shifts. Increasingly, conservationists integrate:

  • Scenario planning to anticipate future environmental conditions.

  • Participatory approaches giving voice to diverse stakeholders.

  • Multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to transparently balance competing objectives.

By embracing flexibility and collaboration, conservation prioritization can become more effective and just.

Final Thoughts

Conservation prioritization stands as both a science and an art. It requires rigorous data, analytical tools, ethical reflection, and pragmatic compromise. Decisions made today shape the living world legacy we leave for generations.

In a world of finite resources and infinite needs, the challenge remains vast—but so too is human ingenuity and commitment. Through judicious prioritization, biologists strive to focus efforts where they matter most, striving to save not just individual species but the integrity of ecosystems and the intricate web of life itself.

By continuously integrating new science, engaging stakeholders meaningfully, and balancing diverse values, conservation prioritization can guide humanity’s stewardship amid unprecedented environmental change. The question of “what to save” may never be fully settled, but the pursuit of thoughtful, data-driven answers is vital for a thriving planet.
