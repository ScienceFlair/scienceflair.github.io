How Health Policy Affects Prescription Drug Prices


Prescription drug prices have become a hot button issue in the United States, with many Americans struggling to afford the medicines they need. The main culprits, it often seems, are pharmaceutical companies and their greedy pricing strategies. However, the reality is far more complex, and health policy plays a significant role in shaping prescription drug costs. In this post, we'll delve into the intricacies of the relationship between health policy and prescription drug prices. The Pendulum of Policy: How Permissive Regulations Can Fuel Price Increases The United States has a long history of permissive regulatory policies that have allowed the pharmaceutical industry to flourish. The 1984 Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act, which exempted mergers and acquisitions of pharma companies from antitrust review, laid the groundwork for the industry's consolidation. This consolidation has led to fewer, more powerful pharma companies that can dictate prices, contributing to skyrocketing costs. The Medicaid pic and Game of Price Concessions In the 1990s, pharmaceutical companies began offering discounts and rebates to Medicaid, which had been created under Title XIX of the Social Security Act to provide health insurance to low-income families. These price concessions were initially voluntary but quickly became mandatory through state-by-state negotiations. As more states entered into these agreements, discount prices became the norm, but it raised questions about the long-term viability of this system. The Center Stage: Medicare and the Impact of Part D With the passage of Medicare Part D in 2003, the stage was set for a major shift in the pharma landscape. This program, which provides coverage for medication to elderly Americans, was instrumental in liberalizing the patient assistance program landscape. As more Americans entered the program, pharma companies saw a massive growth in diabetes, cholesterol, and other medication prescriptions, driving up costs even further. The Unsung Heroes of Health Policy: How Government Programs Can Rein in Costs One of the lesser-known aspects of health policy is its role in tempering pharma costs. Programs such as Medicaid, the Veterans Administration, and Medicare have built-in mechanisms that help control costs. For instance, the control prices they set for Medicare ensure that negotiated prices do not exceed negotiated ceiling prices. These programs take the biggest chunk of overall pharma spending but spend less on prescription drugs than private payers due to this natural tug-of-war over discounted costs.
