How to Grow Succulents Like a Pro


Succulents have become one of the most popular plants for home gardeners, and for good reason. These hardy plants are not only visually striking but also incredibly easy to care for. Whether you’re a seasoned plant enthusiast or a beginner, growing succulents successfully can be a rewarding and enjoyable experience. In this guide, we’ll walk you through everything you need to know to grow succulents like a pro, from selecting the right varieties to understanding their care needs, and even troubleshooting common problems.

Understanding Succulent Basics

Before diving into the specifics of succulent care, it’s important to understand what makes these plants so unique. Succulents are plants that have thick, fleshy parts designed to store water. This adaptation allows them to thrive in arid environments where other plants might struggle. While they are generally low-maintenance, succulents do have specific needs when it comes to light, soil, water, and temperature.

Popular Succulent Varieties

Succulents come in a wide range of varieties, each with its own unique characteristics. Some of the most popular succulent types include:

  • Aloe Vera: Known for its medicinal properties, Aloe Vera is easy to grow and thrives in bright, indirect light.
  • Echeveria: With its rosette-shaped leaves, this succulent is a favorite among collectors for its diverse colors and patterns.
  • Sedum: Known for its low-growing habit, Sedum is a great ground cover or container plant.
  • Crassula: The jade plant, a member of this group, is prized for its thick, glossy leaves and ability to grow into a small tree-like shape.
  • Cactus: While technically not all succulents, cacti are often grouped in with them. They typically require even less water than other succulents and thrive in full sunlight.

Understanding the type of succulent you’re working with is the first step to successful cultivation. Each variety has slightly different care requirements, but they all share a few common characteristics.

Selecting the Right Soil

One of the most important aspects of succulent care is choosing the right soil. Succulents require well-draining soil to prevent their roots from sitting in water, which can lead to root rot. The best soil for succulents is a mix that allows excess water to escape quickly, preventing the roots from staying wet for too long.

Choosing or Making Succulent Soil

You can either purchase a pre-mixed cactus and succulent soil from your local garden center or make your own. To create your own mix, combine the following:

  • Two parts potting soil: This provides the base for your mix.
  • One part sand: Sand helps with drainage, preventing the soil from becoming too compacted.
  • One part perlite or pumice: These materials further enhance drainage and aeration in the soil.

It’s also possible to add organic matter, such as compost, but keep in mind that succulents prefer soil that is not too rich in nutrients. The key is to ensure your mix promotes fast drainage, as succulents are adapted to dry conditions.

Choosing the Right Pot

Just as important as the soil is the pot you choose for your succulents. The right pot will help ensure that your plants are happy and healthy. When selecting a pot, keep the following in mind:

  • Drainage holes: The most important feature for any succulent pot is drainage. Without proper drainage, water can accumulate at the bottom of the pot, leading to root rot. Always choose a pot with drainage holes, preferably on the bottom.
  • Size: Succulents don’t need huge pots. In fact, planting them in a pot that’s too large can lead to overwatering, as the soil will retain more moisture than the plant can absorb. Choose a pot that is just a little larger than the root ball of your plant.
  • Material: Terra cotta pots are a great choice for succulents because they are porous and allow the soil to dry out more quickly. Plastic or ceramic pots are also acceptable, but they tend to retain moisture longer than terra cotta.

Proper Lighting for Succulents

Light is one of the most important factors in growing succulents successfully. These plants thrive in bright, indirect sunlight. While some varieties can handle direct sunlight, others may need a bit more protection from the harsh midday rays.

How Much Sunlight Do Succulents Need?

Succulents generally require around 6 hours of bright, indirect light per day. If your succulents are placed in a location where they’re not getting enough light, they may start to stretch out or “etiolate,” growing tall and leggy in an attempt to reach for more light. This can weaken the plant over time and make it more susceptible to disease.

  • Bright, indirect light: A south or west-facing window is ideal for most succulents. If you’re growing succulents indoors, consider placing them near a window that gets plenty of sunlight throughout the day.
  • Artificial grow lights: If you don’t have access to a sunny window, consider using a grow light. LED or fluorescent grow lights can provide the right spectrum of light for succulents to thrive.

If you live in an area with long winters or limited sunlight, consider using a grow light to supplement natural light. This can help keep your plants healthy and happy year-round.

Watering Succulents the Right Way

One of the most common mistakes people make when growing succulents is overwatering. Succulents are drought-tolerant plants that store water in their leaves and stems. As a result, they don’t need to be watered as frequently as other plants.

How Often Should You Water Succulents?

The general rule of thumb is to water succulents only when the soil is completely dry. Depending on the time of year and the environment your plant is in, this could mean watering once every 1-2 weeks. In hotter months, succulents may need watering more frequently, while in cooler months, you can water less often.

  • Water thoroughly: When you do water, make sure you water deeply. Allow the water to soak through the bottom of the pot, ensuring that the roots are getting enough moisture. However, don’t let your plant sit in standing water, as this can lead to root rot.
  • Use the “dry” method: Stick your finger into the soil to test moisture levels. If it feels dry at least 1-2 inches below the surface, it’s time to water. If it still feels moist, wait a few more days before checking again.
  • Water less in the winter: During the colder months, succulents go into a dormant period and require less water. Be cautious not to overwater, as this can cause the plant to rot.

If you notice your succulent’s leaves turning yellow or mushy, it could be a sign of overwatering. On the other hand, if the leaves are wrinkled or shriveled, your plant might not be getting enough water.

Temperature and Humidity

Succulents generally prefer warm temperatures and low humidity. They are native to arid regions, and they thrive in environments with temperatures ranging from 60°F to 80°F (15°C to 27°C). Avoid placing your succulents in areas with cold drafts or temperatures below 50°F (10°C), as this can cause damage to the plant.

Humidity

Succulents don’t do well in high humidity. If you live in a particularly humid area, consider placing your succulents in a dry spot with good airflow. Avoid misting your succulents, as this can create a damp environment that encourages rot.

Fertilizing Succulents

Succulents are not heavy feeders, and in most cases, they do not require frequent fertilization. However, feeding them occasionally during their growing season can help promote healthy growth and vibrant color.

How to Fertilize Succulents

  • Use a balanced, diluted fertilizer: A balanced liquid fertilizer that is diluted to about one-quarter strength is perfect for succulents. Apply it once a month during the growing season (spring and summer), but avoid fertilizing during the winter months when the plants are dormant.
  • Avoid over-fertilizing: Over-fertilizing succulents can lead to leggy growth and may even burn the roots. It’s better to err on the side of under-fertilizing than over-fertilizing.

Troubleshooting Common Succulent Problems

Even with the best care, succulents can sometimes face challenges. Here are some of the most common issues and how to fix them:

1. Yellowing Leaves

If your succulent’s leaves are turning yellow, it could be a sign of overwatering. Check the soil to ensure it is not too wet. If the soil is soggy, remove the plant from the pot, let it dry out for a day or two, and repot it in fresh, well-draining soil.

2. Stretching or Leggy Growth

When succulents don’t get enough light, they tend to stretch out in an attempt to reach more sunlight. This is called “etiolation.” To prevent this, ensure your plant is receiving enough bright, indirect sunlight or place it under a grow light.

3. Root Rot

Root rot is a common issue with succulents, often caused by overwatering or poor drainage. If you notice mushy, blackened roots when you remove the plant from the pot, it’s time to cut away the rotted parts and repot the plant in fresh soil. Be sure to allow the plant to dry out for a day before replanting.

4. Pests

Succulents are generally resistant to pests, but they can sometimes attract aphids, mealybugs, or scale insects. If you notice small pests on your plants, remove them gently with a cotton swab dipped in rubbing alcohol or use an insecticidal soap to treat the problem.

Conclusion

Growing succulents is a fulfilling hobby that anyone can enjoy. By providing the right soil, lighting, watering schedule, and temperature, you can help your plants thrive. Remember that succulents are low-maintenance but require some attention to detail. With patience and care, you’ll be able to cultivate a beautiful and healthy succulent garden that will thrive for years to come.
