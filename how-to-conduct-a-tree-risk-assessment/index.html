How to Conduct a Tree Risk Assessment


Trees are a vital part of our environment, providing numerous benefits such as shade, improved air quality, and aesthetic value. However, they also come with potential risks, especially if they are unhealthy or located near buildings, roadways, or public spaces. A tree risk assessment is a critical process that ensures both the health of the trees and the safety of people and property around them. This guide will provide a step-by-step approach to conducting an effective tree risk assessment.

Understanding Tree Risk Assessment

Tree risk assessment is the process of evaluating the condition of a tree, identifying potential hazards, and determining the likelihood of failure. It helps property owners, arborists, and land managers make informed decisions about tree management. Conducting a thorough risk assessment requires knowledge of tree biology, environmental interactions, and advanced observation skills.

The goal is not to eliminate all risk, as that’s neither practical nor necessary. Instead, the focus is to mitigate risks to an acceptable level, balancing the safety of people and property with the benefits trees provide.

Step 1: Define the Scope and Objectives

The first step in any tree risk assessment is to define the scope and purpose of the evaluation. Clarify why the assessment is being conducted and what outcomes are desired. Are you evaluating a single tree, a group of trees, or an entire property? Are you concerned about safety, ecological value, or property damage?

For instance, if you are assessing a tree near a children’s playground, the priority may be minimizing any immediate hazards that could harm children. On the other hand, a tree located in a remote forested area might only require basic health monitoring.

Step 2: Identify Site Conditions and Context

Understanding the context of the tree’s location is crucial. Trees do not exist in isolation; their surroundings greatly influence their growth, health, and potential risks. When evaluating site conditions, consider the following:

  1. Tree Location: Is the tree near buildings, busy streets, or high-traffic areas? Trees in urban areas often pose higher risks due to their proximity to people and property.
  2. Soil Quality: Assess the condition and health of the soil. Poor soil compaction, drainage issues, or nutrient deficiencies can weaken a tree’s stability.
  3. Weather Patterns: Are there strong winds, heavy rains, snow, or other environmental stresses that could affect the tree?
  4. Surrounding Vegetation: Is the tree competing for resources with other trees or vegetation? This can impact its overall health and structural integrity.

Step 3: Conduct a Visual Inspection

A visual inspection is the foundation of any tree risk assessment. Using the “ground-to-crown” approach, examine the tree from its base to its highest branches for visible signs of defects or health concerns. Key areas to evaluate include:

Trunk and Bark

  • Look for cracks, cavities, or decayed wood, which are indicators of internal structural weakness.
  • Note any growth anomalies, such as bulges or seams, which could suggest stress or damage.
  • Check for missing bark or deep wounds, as these can expose the tree to pests or diseases.

Root System

  • Inspect the visible root zone for damage, decay, or soil disturbances.
  • Check for fungal growth near the base of the trunk, as fungi are often associated with root rot.
  • Look for heaving soil or exposed roots, which may indicate instability.

Canopy and Branches

  • Observe the condition of the branches for deadwood, hanging limbs, or broken branches.
  • Examine leaf color and density, as thinning foliage or discoloration may indicate disease or pest infestations.
  • Assess the branch structure for signs of poor attachment or a risk of failure.

Step 4: Evaluate Structural Integrity and Health

While the visual inspection provides an overview, the next step is to delve deeper into the tree’s structural integrity and health. This stage may require more specialized tools and techniques, such as:

  1. Resistograph Testing: This tool measures wood density and strength to evaluate internal decay or cavities.
  2. Increment Borers: These are used to extract tree cores for studying growth patterns and identifying stress events.
  3. Tomographic Scans: Advanced imaging tools like sonic or electrical tomography provide a cross-section view of a tree’s interior.
  4. Leaf and Soil Analysis: Testing soil nutrients and examining leaves for specific deficiencies can reveal underlying health concerns.

Step 5: Assess Target Risk and the Likelihood of Impact

Even the healthiest tree can pose a risk if it’s located in a sensitive area. The “target” refers to what’s at risk should part of the tree fail, such as people, vehicles, infrastructure, or other property. Assessing target risk involves:

  • Identifying the likelihood of people or vehicles being near the tree during a potential failure.
  • Considering both the size of potential falling branches and the distance they could travel.
  • Factoring in dynamic targets, such as playgrounds or parking lots that see occasional activity.

Risk assessment is often quantified using qualitative measures, such as categorizing the likelihood of failure as “low,” “moderate,” or “high.”

Step 6: Assign a Risk Rating

Many arborists use standardized tree risk rating systems to quantify the level of risk. These systems typically evaluate three factors:

  1. Likelihood of Failure: How probable is it that the tree or a part of it will fail under normal conditions?
  2. Likelihood of Impact: If the tree fails, how likely is it to strike a target?
  3. Consequences of Failure: What is the expected severity of damage if the failure occurs?

These factors are combined into a numerical or categorical score, guiding the next steps for mitigation.

Step 7: Define Mitigation Strategies

After determining the risk level, the final step is devising strategies to address any identified hazards. Common mitigation measures include:

  • Pruning: Removing dead, damaged, or overhanging branches to reduce the likelihood of failure.
  • Cabling and Bracing: Providing structural support to weak or vulnerable branches.
  • Soil and Root Care: Improving soil quality, irrigation, and mulching to enhance tree health.
  • Tree Removal: In extreme cases where the risk cannot be mitigated, removal may be necessary. Always consult a professional arborist for this task.

Remember that mitigation should strike a balance between safety and preserving the tree’s benefits.

Best Practices for Ongoing Tree Management

Tree risk assessment is not a one-time event; it should be part of an ongoing tree-management plan. Here are a few tips to maintain trees responsibly:

  1. Schedule regular inspections, especially for older or high-risk trees.
  2. Monitor environmental changes, such as new construction or climate shifts, that could impact tree health.
  3. Keep a detailed record of assessments, treatments, and any incidents for future reference.
  4. Consult certified arborists for professional assessments and complex interventions.

The Value of Professional Expertise

While property owners can perform basic inspections, certain situations may require advanced expertise. Certified arborists bring specialized tools, extensive knowledge, and adherence to industry standards to ensure a comprehensive assessment. When in doubt, always seek professional help to address significant risks or complex cases.

Conducting a tree risk assessment protects not only property and people but also ensures the long-term health and sustainability of trees. By following these steps and staying vigilant, you can enjoy the myriad benefits of trees without compromising safety. Thoughtful tree management is an investment in both your landscape and your community’s well-being.
