The Evolution of Economic Theories: From Keynes to Friedman


The field of economics has undergone a significant transformation over the centuries, with various theories emerging to explain the intricacies of economic behavior and decision-making. In this blog post, we will explore the evolution of economic theories from the early 20th century to the present day, with a focus on the contributions of John Maynard Keynes and Milton Friedman. The Great Depression and the Emergence of Keynesian Economics The Great Depression of the 1930s was a pivotal moment in economic history, leading to a fundamental shift in the way economists thought about the economy. John Maynard Keynes, a British economist,8 was at the forefront of this paradigm shift. His book, "The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money," published in 1936, offered a new perspective on the role of government in stabilizing the economy. Keynesian economics posits that aggregate demand, rather than supply, drives economic growth. In times of economic downturn, when aggregate demand is low, the government should intervene by increasing government spending and cutting taxes to stimulate economic activity. This approach was revolutionary, as it challenged the traditional view that economic downturns were a natural correction to economic imbalances. The Neoclassical Rebuttal: The Rise of Monetarism Not everyone agreed with Keynes' ideas, however. Milton Friedman, a Nobel laureate and economist, emerged as a prominent critic of Keynesian economics. Friedman, a champion of the Chicago School of economics, argued that the economy is best understood as a system of individual choices and market forces. In his 1963 book, "A Monetary History of the United States," Friedman and his co-author Anna Schwartz countered the Keynesian view, suggesting that the Great Depression was not the result of a lack of aggregate demand, but rather a monetary policy mistake. Friedman's monetarist school of thought emphasizes the role of the money supply in driving economic activity. He argued that the government should focus on maintaining a stable money supply, rather than trying to manipulate the economy through fiscal policy. This approach is based on the idea that the economy will self-correct and return to equilibrium, without the need for government intervention. The Revolution in Time-Preference Theory Another significant development in economic theory was the emergence of general equilibrium theory, which builds on the concept of consumer choice and the instability of the economy. This theory, championed by Lionel Robbins and others, rejected the neoclassical assumption that economic agents make decisions based on their current income and savings rates. Instead, the focus shifted to consumer preferences and how they change over time. In the 1950s and '60s, the rise of information economics and the discovery of the Prisoner's Dilemma by economists such as Arrow and Debreu added new dimensions to mainstream economic theory. These ideas about modeling decision-making have had a lasting impact on the theory of human behavior and analysis. The Neoclassical Synthesis and Modern Developments In the decades following the Keynesian-Monetarist debate, neoclassical synthesis emerged as a fusion of the two approaches. This perspective, endorsed by economists like Paul Samuelson, mainstreams the perspectives of both Keynes and Friedman.
