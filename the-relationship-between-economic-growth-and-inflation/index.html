The Relationship Between Economic Growth and Inflation


Economic Growth and inflation are two terms intrinsic to macroeconomic theory. While the two are intimately correlated, they may create mixed feelings when discussed independently. To achieve economic growth, a country needs to regularly expand its output of goods and services. Inflation measures the rate at which the average price of goods and services changes over time.

Economic growth provides the backbone for a country’s progress, fueling job creation, increasing productivity, and enhancing overall prosperity. But it does not operate in a vacuum. As such, inflation is a crucial factor that often goes hand-in-hand with economic growth.

The most fundamental relationship between economic growth and inflation can be conceptualized in two essential ways - Classical theory and Keynesian theory.

According to Classical Economics, inflation primarily stems from the supply side of the economy. When demand increases due to economic growth, businesses strive to meet consumers’ needs, creating a larger demand for resources. As prices for these resources increase, the producer often passes the burden down the supply chain, resulting in price increases overall. Classical theory advocates posit that this inflation is transitory and self-regulating, rebounding into considerable economic growth.

On the other hand, John Maynard Keynes largely introduces a dependency of inflation on aggregate demand in his Keynesian theory. According to him, as aggregate demand surpasses aggregate supply, firms must increase prices to clear the market, inducing inflation. Unable to charge consumers higher prices due to lack of price-setting power, workers and consumers experience economic stagnation.

Interestingly, both perspectives validate the symbiotic relationship between economic growth and inflation. Classical economists may see a direct correlation, whilst Keynesians may often see a concerning indirect correlation.

The Messtermichael hypothesis adds another layer to their comical romance. Extensive economic growth could lead to higher inflation, which in turn causes larger interest rates, hindering growth and leading to stagflation. Then, a stagflation is characterized by stagnant growth rates, high unemployment rates, and high inflation.

True understanding of the Economy requires an understanding of both. We delude ourselves if we think we can selectively pick between economic growth and inflation alleviation, hope for a handsome package deal. The success of an economy rests on a precarious see-saw, finely balancing inflation and growth.

However, when left unchecked, inflation – considered a monster in disguise – can hurt the well-being of an economy. Persistent and high inflation can cause significant damage to the economy. It erodes the purchasing power of the population, leading to heightened uncertainty and expectation of similar price increases. Subsequently, consumers and businesses defer investment and spending decisions. Persistent inflation can also lead to a self-fulfilling prophecy: the truly fundamental cause is not an increase in the demand for goods and services but the hit to aggregate supply through expectations of similar price increases.

Achieving the impossible balance of sustainable growth and controlled inflation falls on the purview of the government’s macro-economic policies and its execution. Monetary policies such as controlling the money supply, managing bond yields, and adjusting interest rates can impact inflation, and fiscal policies - beneficially inhibiting or detrimentally enhancing - can foster, hinder, or steer economic growth.

Ultimately, prosperity is an economic oxymoron, dependent both on sustenance and moderation. The delicate balance of economic growth and inflation control is exemplified in the Keynesian and Classical theories. More than mundane numbers, each percentage point increase represents the collective effort of millions, the dreams burned and achieved, an embodiment of human endeavor and resilience.
