Earthquake Preparedness Tips for Schools and Businesses


Earthquakes are unpredictable natural events that can have devastating consequences for schools, businesses, and entire communities. Preparing for an earthquake not only reduces the risk of injuries and property damage but also ensures a faster recovery when disruptions occur. Establishing a robust safety plan is paramount for both schools and businesses—a plan that prioritizes safety while minimizing operational interruptions.

Understanding the Importance of Earthquake Preparedness

While earthquakes themselves cannot be prevented, the impact they cause can be reduced with proper preparedness. Schools are responsible for the safety of their students, staff, and faculty, making a clear and well-practiced emergency response essential. Businesses, on the other hand, must prioritize employee safety while protecting critical assets, infrastructure, and operations.

The lessons learned from past earthquakes highlight the widespread consequences of inadequate planning: injuries, loss of life, destroyed infrastructure, disrupted services, and severe economic losses. By proactively preparing for these events, you increase the likelihood of a positive outcome for everyone involved.

Building an Effective Earthquake Preparedness Plan

An earthquake preparedness plan is a living document, one that evolves to accommodate new information and shifting needs. Below, we outline key considerations and actionable steps to help schools and organizations build a robust foundation for safety.

1. Conduct Risk Assessments

Both schools and businesses should begin with a thorough risk assessment:

  • Identify Hazards: Pinpoint potential hazards in and around your buildings. These can include tall bookshelves, unsecured light fixtures, electrical wiring, and exterior features such as windows and overhead signage.
  • Evaluate Structural Safety: Hire licensed engineers to inspect buildings for vulnerabilities. Retrofitting older structures can significantly reduce the risk of structural failure during an earthquake.
  • Geographic Risks: Understand the school’s or business’s location relative to seismic zones. Higher risk areas require more extensive preparations.

Document these findings and prioritize mitigation strategies for the most critical risks.

2. Create Emergency Responses

Schools and businesses must develop clear, simple emergency response plans that address the immediate aftermath of an earthquake. Key components of such plans include:

  • Evacuation Routes: Designate safe exit routes and post clear signage in high-visibility areas. Conduct regular drills to ensure everyone knows how to access these routes during an emergency.
  • Assembly Areas: Establish outdoor assembly points away from buildings, power lines, and trees where people can gather for safety and roll calls.
  • Drop, Cover, and Hold On Procedures: Educate all participants on the “Drop, Cover, and Hold On” technique, which reduces the risk of falling debris injuries during shaking.

Ensure emergency protocols are accessible and readable by all. For schools, keep the language age-appropriate for students. For businesses, consider making translated versions available for diverse workforces.

3. Stock Emergency Supplies

A well-stocked emergency kit can make a significant difference in the aftermath of an earthquake. Equip schools and office buildings with sufficient supplies, including:

  • First aid kits
  • Water and non-perishable food for a minimum of 72 hours
  • Flashlights with extra batteries
  • Portable radios for receiving emergency alerts
  • Blankets, gloves, and dust masks
  • Basic tools, such as wrenches to turn off utilities

Periodically check and replenish your supplies to ensure all items remain usable.

4. Train Staff and Students

Preparedness hinges on informed and trained individuals. Schools should incorporate earthquake drills into their curriculums and businesses should regularly host training sessions. Flashcards, videos, and role-playing can engage younger children, while businesses can use workshops or even simulated drills for adult employees.

Earthquake training for all participants should include:

  • Recognizing and responding to earthquake signs
  • Correctly executing the “Drop, Cover, and Hold On” technique
  • Operating emergency response equipment, such as fire extinguishers
  • Communicating effectively during emergencies to avoid panic

Trainings should also address the needs of individuals who may require assistance, such as those with disabilities or mobility challenges.

5. Prepare for Communication Challenges

Communication is critical during and after an earthquake. Establish protocols that prioritize disseminating accurate and timely information.

For schools:

  • Designate faculty members to contact parents and guardians with updates on student safety and pickup procedures.
  • Equip each classroom with walkie-talkies or similar devices for internal communication since cellular networks can fail.

For businesses:

  • Maintain an updated contact tree to ensure all employees can be reached during emergencies.
  • Test your company’s emergency alert systems regularly to confirm the technology works.

Both environments should utilize social media and local news outlets to share updates with the broader community during large-scale incidents.

Mitigating Damage: Strengthening Physical Spaces

Mitigation strategies focus on minimizing damage to the physical environment, which benefits both safety and continuity.

  • Use brackets, straps, or fasteners to secure shelves, filing cabinets, and kitchen appliances.
  • Install shatter-resistant film on windows and glass doors.
  • Position heavy items on low shelves to prevent them from falling.
  • Anchor computers, printers, and other equipment to desks or walls.
  • In science or art classrooms, use secure storage for chemicals, paints, and supplies.

Retrofitting a building for seismic resiliency is especially important in older structures. Consulting with structural engineers to identify reinforcements for walls, roofs, and foundations can reduce long-term financial risks.

Supporting Emotional Well-Being After an Earthquake

It’s important for schools and businesses alike to address emotional well-being following an earthquake. The psychological toll of experiencing a natural disaster often lingers, particularly among young students and employees who have experienced trauma.

To aid recovery:

  • Offer counseling services to provide emotional support.
  • Encourage team-building opportunities that foster a sense of community and mutual care.
  • Provide professional development or workshops for educators and managers on recognizing post-traumatic stress symptoms.

Students and employees who feel their health and emotions are cared for are more likely to recover quickly and remain productive.

Recovery and Continuity Planning

Once safety has been reestablished, focus on returning to regular operations. For schools, ensure classrooms are cleaned, repaired, and safe for returning students. For businesses, enforce continuity strategies that ensure minimal disruption.

Key post-earthquake recovery tips include:

  • Assessing structural damage with qualified professionals
  • Restocking emergency supplies
  • Conducting follow-up debriefings with staff to identify gaps in preparedness
  • Revisiting policies and plans for future improvement

Incorporate these practices into general operations to ensure long-term resilience.

Taking Action Today

Earthquake preparedness is not something to delay—it requires immediate attention, collaboration, and a proactive mindset. Whether you’re leading a school or running a business, taking the time to prepare today can save lives, protect property, and preserve community stability.

By implementing these steps, you create safe environments where people feel empowered and secure, even in the face of uncertainty. Start small, involve everyone, and make preparedness part of your culture. The peace of mind these measures bring is invaluable in ensuring swift and effective responses to earthquakes when they occur.
