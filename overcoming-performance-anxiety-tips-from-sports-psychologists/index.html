Overcoming Performance Anxiety: Tips from Sports Psychologists


Performance anxiety is a common challenge faced by athletes and professionals at all levels, from beginners stepping into their first competition to seasoned veterans pursuing peak performance under pressure. The mental and emotional intensity of striving to achieve one’s best can, at times, be overwhelming. However, understanding and addressing performance anxiety isn’t just beneficial for sports—it can provide strategies applicable in any pressure-filled scenario, whether it’s giving a presentation, taking an exam, or meeting an important deadline.

To better understand how to tackle this, insights from sports psychologists can offer evidence-backed strategies to conquer fears, manage nerves, and unlock your true potential when it matters most.

Recognize and Understand Performance Anxiety

The first step to overcoming performance anxiety is understanding what it is and why it occurs. Simply put, performance anxiety is the fear of underperforming or failing in high-pressure situations. This fear often triggers our body’s fight-or-flight response, leading to physical symptoms like sweating, increased heart rate, shallow breathing, and even tremors.

It’s important to recognize that some level of anxiety is normal—and can even be beneficial. This state of heightened alertness, referred to as eustress, can promote focus, enhance decision-making, and sharpen reactions. Problems arise, however, when anxiety becomes excessive. Instead of boosting performance, it can derail focus, drain energy, and create a mental block.

Identifying the triggers of performance anxiety is crucial. Take time to reflect on what causes your anxiety to spike. Is it fear of judgment? An intense desire to meet the expectations of others? Pressure to maintain perfection? Knowing your unique triggers is the foundation for growth.

Embrace Mental Preparation Strategies

Combatting performance anxiety starts long before you step into the competitive environment. Mental preparation enables you to adjust how you think and feel when faced with pressure. Sports psychologists often recommend the following strategies for mastering your mindset:

1. Visualization

Also known as mental imagery or mental rehearsal, visualization is a technique in which you practice performing well by imagining it in vivid detail. Studies have shown that mentally simulating positive outcomes helps not only with confidence but also with improving actual performance.

Close your eyes and visualize yourself succeeding in your specific task. Picture every facet: the environment, your movements, the sound of the crowd, and the satisfaction of achieving your goal. The more detail you add to these mental images, the more your brain becomes accustomed to perceiving that success is achievable.

2. Self-Talk Restructuring

How you talk to yourself matters. Negative self-talk—such as “I always mess this part up” or “What if I fail?”—can intensify anxiety and undermine confidence. Sports psychologists encourage athletes to practice positive self-talk instead.

Replace a pessimistic narrative with empowering statements like, “I’ve trained for this moment” or “I am ready and capable.” Repeated affirmations create new neural pathways in the brain, reinforcing a more optimistic mindset over time.

3. Mindfulness Practices

Mindfulness is the practice of staying present and nonjudgmental in the moment, which can help diminish the weight of future-focused worries or past regrets. Techniques such as meditation, grounding exercises, and mindful breathing calm your nervous system and redirect your focus to what’s within your control.

Simple mindfulness exercises, such as breathing deeply for a count of four seconds and exhaling for six, can be profoundly effective in real time. They engage your parasympathetic nervous system, promoting relaxation and breaking the cycle of anxiety.

Build a Resilient Routine

Developing consistent, resilience-building habits allows you to proactively manage performance anxiety rather than reacting to it. Elite athletes often cultivate pre-performance routines and rituals to help them transition into their optimal mental state.

1. Create a Pre-Performance Ritual

Rituals signal to your brain and body that it’s time to focus. Whether it’s stretching, reciting a mantra, putting on specific gear, or listening to a particular playlist, creating structured actions can instill a sense of familiarity and control.

Consistency is key. Over time, these practices become associated with successful performances, building a psychological bridge to confidence and preparedness.

2. Balance Rest and Recovery

Burnout and exhaustion can exacerbate performance anxiety. Rest is as important as training. Prioritize quality sleep, nutritious meals, and proper hydration to ensure you’re operating at peak physical and mental capacity.

Additionally, incorporate time for relaxation and hobbies unrelated to your performance goals. Fostering balance prevents becoming overly consumed by high-stakes scenarios.

3. Set Realistic Goals

Breaking larger aspirations into smaller, actionable steps leads to a sense of achievement while reducing the pressure to succeed all at once. For instance, instead of declaring “I need to win,” set process-based goals such as “I will execute my technique with precision” or “I’ll enjoy giving my best effort.”

Process-oriented goals help you focus on behaviors you can control, removing fixation on the final outcome.

Build Coping Strategies for the Moment

Even with preparation, anxiety may occasionally creep in when it’s game time. Learning to manage it in the moment is vital to maintaining performance.

1. Focus on the Present

When anxiety starts to rise during a high-pressure situation, grounding techniques can keep you anchored in the present. Focus on sensory details, such as how the environment feels, sounds, and smells. These cues pull your mind away from catapulting into worst-case scenarios.

Another strategy is to focus on your breathing. Long, diaphragmatic breaths can instantly slow your heart rate and help regulate your nervous system.

2. Reframe the Pressure

Instead of interpreting nervousness as something negative, try to view it as a sign of excitement. Sports psychologists encourage reframing your inner dialogue to recognize that the “butterflies” in your stomach are your body’s way of preparing for action.

For example, instead of saying, “I’m so anxious,” say, “I’m ready and energized for the challenge ahead.” This simple shift in mindset can diminish the anxiety’s psychological grip.

3. Revisit Your Why

When the stakes feel enormous, zoom out and remind yourself why you participate in your activity or sport in the first place. Are you doing this because you love the thrill? To grow as a person? To connect with a community? Revisiting your purpose can take the sting out of moments of doubt, transforming pressure into intrinsic motivation.

Seek Support When Needed

Performance anxiety can often feel isolating, but recognizing that you don’t have to navigate it alone is empowering.

Sports psychologists, mental performance coaches, or even mentors can provide individualized tools to help you manage anxiety and improve focus. Having someone to guide you through the mental side of performance can be a game changer, providing perspective and actionable strategies you may not have considered before.

Additionally, confiding in teammates, close friends, or trusted loved ones can offer emotional support and a safe space to process fears.

The Growth Mindset: See Anxiety as a Challenge

Lastly, it helps to shift your perspective on performance anxiety entirely. Rather than seeing it as a hurdle, view it as an opportunity for growth. Facing moments of pressure head-on and finding ways to overcome them builds mental resilience and adaptability.

Elite athletes often talk about embracing discomfort as a necessary part of improvement. Indeed, overcoming performance anxiety is a skill like any other—it can be learned, developed, and refined through dedicated practice and self-awareness.

In the journey toward overcoming performance anxiety, there’s no one-size-fits-all approach. Different strategies resonate with different individuals, and it’s worth experimenting to see which tools work best for you. With patience, persistence, and perspective, you can transform your relationship with anxiety, turning it from a perceived hindrance into one of your greatest assets.
