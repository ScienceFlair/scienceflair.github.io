Photogrammetry at Crime Scenes: Taking Measurements From Photos


Photogrammetry, the science of obtaining accurate measurements from photographs, has revolutionized the way forensic experts examine and document various aspects of crime scenes. This powerful technique allows investigators to collect valuable evidence, even if the original scene has been compromised or altered. By understanding how photogrammetry works and its applications, law enforcement agencies can dramatically improve their ability to solve cases. In this blog post, we will dive deeper into photogrammetry’s role in crime investigations.

The Basics of Photogrammetry

Photogrammetry is a non-contact technology that utilizes photographs to create three-dimensional (3D) models or measurements of objects. It works by capturing multiple images of an object or scene from different angles. These images act as inputs into photogrammetric software, which uses principles of geometry and mathematical algorithms to piece together the images, generate precise measurements, and reconstruct the original object in a digital format.

There are two primary photogrammetric methods used in crime scenes: close-range photogrammetry and classical photogrammetry.

  1. Close-range photogrammetry: As the term suggests, this method involves capturing images of an object or scene up-close. The cameras used in close-range photogrammetry are typically handheld or tripod-mounted. The photographs produced are then used to create accurate 3D models of the objects or to measure specific details found in a scene, like measurements, angles, and distances.

  2. Classical photogrammetry: This approach uses aerial photographs, often taken by drones or airplanes, to create detailed maps and elevation models. The aerial images cover a larger area of the scene and enable the users to make accurate measurements at scale.

Applications of Photogrammetry in Crime Scenes

Photogrammetry’s ability to produce precise and accurate measurements from photographs has made it an invaluable tool in crime investigations. Here are some applications of photogrammetry at crime scenes:

  1. Scene reconstruction: One of the most important applications of photogrammetry in crime scenes is the reconstruction of events. By stitching together forensic photos, investigators can develop a comprehensive understanding of how and why a crime occurred. In many cases, photogrammetry provides crucial evidence that could guide experts’ conclusions and help establish culpability.

  2. Documentation of evidence: Photogrammetry can document and record evidence found at crime scenes with high accuracy and precision. This aspect is critical when dealing with fragile or perishable evidence like pollen, bones, and burned debris. The 3D models generated by photogrammetry allow experts to examine these delicate items virtually without causing further damage.

  3. Measurement of bloodstains: Bloodstain pattern analysis is a pivotal aspect of forensic investigations. Photogrammetry enables investigators to accurately measure the size, shape, and angle of bloodstains in a crime scene. Moreover, by utilizing photogrammetry, the bloodstain patterns can be mapped and analyzed for possible connections to the suspect or the events that unfolded at the scene.

  4. Crime scene walkthrough and exploration: Photogrammetry allows investigators to virtually “walk through” a crime scene, inspecting and examining evidence without the risk of contamination or loss of evidence during the actual walkthrough. This technique undoubtedly leads to a more organized and thorough forensic examination.

  5. Crime scene mapping and scale modeling: Photogrammetry provides a method to generate highly accurate topographical maps and scale models of crime scenes. These models are helpful in understanding the spatial relationship among various components and events during the crime. They also serve as assistants in the planning of reconstruction experiments or court presentations.

Benefits of Photogrammetry at Crime Scenes

The use of photogrammetry has many advantages, including:

  1. Time efficiency: Photogrammetry allows investigators to save time in processing evidence, reducing manual labor and human error in measuring distances, angles, and minor details.
  2. Accuracy: Photogrammetry provides high-accuracy results that are crucial for legal proceedings.
  3. Documentation: Photogrammetry offers reliable photo documentation of crime scenes, valuable for use in court and post-analysis.
  4. Remote access: With photogrammetric models, investigators can access the crime scene remotely for further examination, which is particularly useful if the original scene has been altered or destroyed.

Conclusion

Photogrammetry has become an integral part of forensic investigations, playing a vital role in documenting and reconstructing crime scenes. By allowing investigators to obtain accurate measurements and create 3D models from photographic evidence, photogrammetry empowers forensic experts to enhance their understanding and analysis of a specific crime scene. As technology continues to evolve, it is expected that photogrammetry will remain at the forefront of forensic methods, revolutionizing the landscape of criminal investigations and achieving better outcomes in legal proceedings.
