Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): Strategies for Managing Emotional Dysregulation


Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is a powerful approach to managing and overcoming emotional dysregulation. Developed by psychologist Dr. Marsha Linehan in the late 1980s, DBT has since been adapted for a wide range of populations struggling with emotion-related difficulties. This blog post will discuss some essential strategies in DBT for successfully managing emotional dysregulation.

  1. Mindfulness

The practice of mindfulness is rooted in Eastern meditation techniques and has been adapted for use in DBT. The fundamental premise of mindfulness is to be fully present in the moment, acknowledging our thoughts and feelings without judgment. Mindfulness helps individuals become more aware of their emotions and enables them to respond skillfully instead of reacting impulsively.

Integrating mindfulness into daily life can look like paying full attention while eating or engaging in an activity, pursuing a walking meditation, or simply pausing to acknowledge and label emotions as they arise. By building the habit of mindfulness, individuals can develop a greater understanding and acceptance of their emotional experiences, ultimately mitigating the intense emotional storm present in emotional dysregulation.

  1. Distress Tolerance

Distress tolerance involves learning skills to cope with emotional pain and discomfort without making things worse. This is crucial for managing emotional dysregulation since individuals are often overwhelmed by their emotions to the point of being immobilized.

One distress tolerance skill is the “dial it down” method. This involves completely engaging the senses (sight, touch, sound, taste, and smell) to ground oneself in the present moment and reduce intense emotions. Another vital skill in distress tolerance is the “sit with it” method, wherein a person acknowledges their emotions without attempting to change or escape from them. This acceptance can help alleviate the discomfort associated with emotional dysregulation, allowing for greater control over spurred behaviors.

  1. Emotional Regulation

A hallmark of emotional dysregulation is difficulty managing emotions in a healthy way. DBT offers several effective emotional regulation strategies to help individuals develop greater control over their emotional experiences.

One of these is “opposite action”- acting in ways that oppose the urge to engage in maladaptive behaviors. For example, if the urge to shut down and isolate is strong, one might actively choose to initiate contact with a friend or participate in a social activity.

Also, the “half-smile” technique involves physically altering one’s emotional state by consciously lifting the corners of the mouth to create a smile despite feeling down or unhappy. By doing so, chemical changes occur in the brain that can alter emotional experience, contributing to fewer mood swings and a balanced emotional state.

  1. Interpersonal Effectiveness

Interpersonal effectiveness is a core skill in DBT, as emotional dysregulation often wreaks havoc on communication and relationships. Learning to communicate with others in an assertive, honest, and validating way can greatly improve emotional well-being and interpersonal relationships.

One critical element of interpersonal effectiveness is “self-respect” (sometimes referred to as assertiveness). Practicing ways to ask for what you need without demanding or apologizing can improve relationships and increase self-esteem. Another potent skill is “other-validation,” wherein one recognizes and acknowledges another person’s perspective or feelings, even when there is disagreement.

  1. Building a Support System

Finally, a vital DBT strategy for managing emotional dysregulation is to build a reliable support system. Humans are social creatures, and having people we can count on in times of distress can make all the difference.

Seeking support from friends, family, or a DBT skills group can be incredibly beneficial. Sharing experiences and learning from others who struggle with similar emotional challenges can reinforce DBT skills. Additionally, having individuals invested in one’s growth can help reign in moments of vulnerability and provide the encouragement needed to continue pursuing a life of emotional sobriety.

In conclusion, DBT offers several highly effective strategies for individuals struggling with emotional dysregulation. By honing mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotional regulation, interpersonal effectiveness, and support network skills, one can harness the power of DBT to create a life characterized by balance and harmony.

As our understanding of emotional dysregulation and DBT continues to evolve, these five core strategies form the foundation for millions of individuals seeking to carve out a serene life amidst life’s emotional turmoils. The journey to emotional resilience might not always be a smooth one, but DBT skills provide signposts along the way, lighting the path towards a life rich with fulfillment and purpose.
