The Impact of Space Debris on Satellite Operations


Space debris, or orbital debris, refers to all human-made objects in Earth’s orbit that no longer serve a functional purpose. Once limited to dead satellites and spent rocket stages, space debris has grown exponentially with the number of operational satellites in orbit. While usually invisible from the ground, space debris poses significant risks to functioning satellites and astronaut safety. This post will examine the growing threat of space debris and its consequences on satellite operations.

The amount of space debris in low Earth orbit (LEO) has been increasing exponentially since the start of space exploration. The world’s first artificial satellite, Sputnik 1, lofted in 1957, still remains part of this clutter. As of 2021, there are somewhere around 24,000 bits of space debris identified in LEO. Over 6,000 of them have been cataloged as ‘active’ because they could potentially be removed by debris removal systems, should those become a reality.

Collisions can result in the creation of new space debris or costly repairs, loss of function, or even catastrophic loss of assets. In 1996, the Russian Cosmos 2251 communications satellite collided with a British Sky-Satellite, chunking off a piece of both spacecraft and making the space debris problem even worse. These space collisions can happen at amazing speeds. When Cosmos 2251 and Sky-Satellite collided, they were moving at 29,000 kilometers per day (km/h) relative to one another. Imagine trying to control a high-speed car race when the road itself is littered with debris and hidden traps.

Space debris also poses a risk to ongoing missions. It requires satellite operators to carry out specific mitigation techniques, including raising the orbit of operational satellites to reduce the risk of collision. However, this increases the cost of satellite operations for both governmental and private organizations alike.

Space debris can impact satellite operations in several ways. As with the Cosmos 2251 and Sky-Satellite collision, debris can damage or destroy satellites. These occurrences can reduce the lifespan of satellites and affect their ability to function accurately, leading to incomplete or inaccurate scientific observations. The growth of space debris has also raised concerns around the feasibility of building space infrastructure, such as space hotels and space elevators.

While international conventions like the 1967 Outer Space Treaty and the 2007 ICAO Global Venus Flyby Space Debris Mitigation Standard have addressed space debris issues, the effectiveness of these measures remains uncertain. In recent years, the commercial and private sectors have started collectively pushing for practical solutions to the space debris problem.

One such solution is the creation of space lanes for debris to flow into. Companies like Effective Space Solutions propose their space traffic management system, clearing designated orbits of space debris by hoovering or deflecting it into specific zones. Other strategies involve passive technolo肥gies like space mines, activated only when they sense a collision threat, and active ones like space microrobots that could clean up debris directly.

The success of these solutions will undoubtedly play a huge role in determining the future of satellites and keeping our orbits both functional and safe. Despite the difficulties in their implementation, they hold great potential in mitigating the growing threat of space debris on satellite operations and driving forward the further exploration and development of space resources.

It’s a race against time to clean up this orbital litter box and feeding our curiosity for the stars beyond. With promising technologies and international cooperation, the future of satellite operations looks towards a cleaner environment and better operating conditions. The challenge remains to ensure that space debris remains a thing of the past, allowing satellites to operate better and free in the vast cosmos, unhampered by the literal detritus of past human endeavours.
