10 Remarkable Scientists That Revolutionized Material Science


Material science, the study of the properties of matter and how it changes, has seen revolutionary advancements due to the innovative discoveries and contributions of these 10 remarkable scientists. From understanding the fabric of our universe to creating materials capable of altering our world, these scientists have made incredible strides in their respective fields, shaping our future, and forever changing the landscape of material science. Let’s take a closer look at their contributions, in no particular order.

  1. Max Perutz: Pioneer of Molecular Biology

Lauded for his contributions to the understanding of hemoglobin, Max Perutz along with partner John Kendrew, cracked the structure of this critical protein using x-ray crystallography. Their work laid the foundation for a whole field of structural biology, revealing the complex machinery within our cells, and offering insights into the causes of numerous blood-related diseases.

  1. Richard Feynman: “Plenty of Room at the Bottom”

Considered one of the best physicists of the 20th century, Richard Feynman, in his lecture “There’s Plenty of Room at the Bottom,” challenged scientists to think small. He envisioned the ability to manipulate atoms and molecules at the nanoscale, which laid the groundwork for the field of nanotechnology. His Nobel Prize-winning contributions to quantum mechanics, along with his lateral thinking, have inspired generations of scientists.

  1. Nobel Prize Winners: Alan Heeger, Alan MacDiarmid, and Hideki Shirakawa

These three scientists revolutionized the field of electronics when they discovered the technological breakthrough called ‘conductive polymers’, specifically in 1975, when they accidentally found that doped plastics could be made to conduct electricity. The result? The birth of the “plastic revolution” in electronics, creating a new generation of super-conductive and flexible materials.

Moore, Stein, and Stein introduced the world to the concept of sequencing amino acids on proteins, specifically in the enzyme lysozyme. This technique facilitated the analysis of protein structure and functions. Their work led to a new era in meddling with enzymes at the molecular level, paving the way for the drug formulation industry.

  1. Rosalind Franklin: X-Ray Crystallography

Despite facing gender-based challenges, Rosalind Franklin was instrumental in understanding the complexities of the molecular structures of DNA, RNA, viruses, coal, and graphite. Her work heavily influenced James Watson and Francis Crick’s discovery of the DNA double helix molecule structure. Her contribution to the field, along with her perseverance in the face of adversity, has left an everlasting mark on material science and our understanding of life itself.

  1. Joachim Frank: Cryo-electron microscopy

In 1975, Joachim Frank developed the idea of using computer tools in analyzing electron microscopy images. This groundbreaking approach would give birth to cryo-electron microscopy, making it possible to analyze complex macromolecules at near-atomic resolutions, thus laying the foundation for significant advancements in the study of proteins and other complex biological structures.

  1. Ada Yonath: Structuring Life’s Cradle

Ada Yonath’s relentless efforts in understanding the structure of the ribosome, the factory where proteins are made, earned her the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2009. Her work provided vital insight into antibiotic action and the molecular basis of diseases like cancer and diabetes.

  1. Venkatraman Ramakrishnan

Ramakrishnan used cryo-electron microscopy and x-ray crystallography to determine the structure of the ribosome, especially the location of the mRNA (messenger RNA) within the ribosome. This discovery provided a better understanding of the translation process and how some antibiotics work, offering insights into the development of better drugs.

  1. Thomas Steitz

Steitz’s work on understanding the structure of the ribosome made a significant contribution to material science by aiding in the elucidation of the processes of transcription and translation. His research on the RNA enzyme complex opened doors to potential new antibiotic drugs that may target specific pathogens.

  1. Dorothy Crowfoot Hodgkin

Hodgkin’s talent in the field of biochemistry won her many accolades including the Nobel Prize in Chemistry. She was known for her pioneering work in determining the structures of complex biological molecules like penicillin, vitamin B12, and insulin, helping revolutionize our understanding of life and the creation of better drugs.

These scientists transcended the limitations of their times, forging new trails in the world of material science. Their work has enabled the world to push the boundaries of creativity and innovation, creating new-age materials that continue to shape our present and future. From aiding in the production of antibiotics to the birth of flexible electronics, these scientists not only revolutionized the material science field but have also greatly enhanced the quality of life on Earth.
