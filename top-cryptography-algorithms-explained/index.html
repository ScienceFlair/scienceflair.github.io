Top Cryptography Algorithms Explained


Cryptography is the practice of securing communication and data by converting the original information, known as plaintext, into an alternative form of representation, known as ciphertext. The process of converting plaintext into ciphertext is called encryption, while the reverse process of converting ciphertext back to plaintext is called decryption.

Cryptography has proven to be a valuable tool in securing information, protecting sensitive data, and preserving privacy across various applications, including financial transactions, data storage, and secure communication channels.

There are numerous cryptographic algorithms available, but in this post, we’ll discuss some of the most widely-used algorithms: the Data Encryption Standard (DES), the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES), the Rivest–Shamir–Adleman (RSA) algorithm, and the Blowfish algorithm.

Data Encryption Standard (DES)

DES, proposed by IBM in 1975, was the first standardized cryptographic algorithm. It uses a symmetric-key approach, meaning the same key is used for both encryption and decryption. The algorithm applies an Expansion Permutation, Key Permutation, and Finite Field on the 64-bit data blocks to produce the 64-bit ciphertext.

DES has since been deprecated due to its relatively weak security compared to current standards. However, it remains an important historical reference and forms the basis for understanding modern encryption principles.

Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)

AES, also known as Rijndael, became the new standard after the obsolescence of DES. Introduced by Belgian cryptographers Joan Daemen and Vincent Rijmen in 1998, AES won the U.S. government’s advanced encryption standard competition in 2000 and became the standard for securing sensitive but unclassified information.

AES can process data in blocks of 128 bits, using either 192 or 256 bits as its key. Like DES, AES is a symmetric-key algorithm but more secure and faster, with multiple designs to cater to different platforms and demands.

Rivest–Shamir–Adleman (RSA) Algorithm

RSA, named after its inventors Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman, uses a public-key cryptosystem, meaning each user has a pair of keys: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption. The keys are mathematically related but intentionally difficult to derive from one another.

RSA is most commonly used for digital signatures, ensuring the sender’s identity, and secure data transmission, assuring confidentiality. RSA, being slower than symmetric algorithms, often is used for key exchange when combined with a symmetric encryption algorithm.

Blowfish Algorithm

Blowfish was developed by Bruce Schneier in 1993, designed to be a high-performance and efficient algorithm with a relatively small key size of 18 words, compared to AES’s substantial requirement.

Blowfish encrypts data in 64-bit blocks and uses a variable-length key, with a maximum length of 128 bits. It does so through five rounds of subkey generation and permutation, mixing, and traditional substitution and permutation methods followed by a final processing step.

Blowfish has lost popularity due to the preference for faster algorithms such as AES, but its efficient key management and relatively simple construction make it applicable in certain scenarios where speed is not a primary concern.

Conclusion

In this post, we explored four of the top cryptographic algorithms: DES, AES, RSA, and Blowfish. Each algorithm serves its distinct purpose, with varying degrees of security, speed, and application:

  • DES, the first standardized algorithm, now deprecated due to weak security.
  • AES, the current encryption standard, processing data faster and more securely than its predecessor, offering many key variants.
  • RSA, the public-key cryptosystem best suited for digital signatures and key exchanges.
  • Blowfish, efficient and widely used, yet losing popularity due to faster alternatives.

Understanding these algorithms and their purposes can assist in making informed decisions when choosing the appropriate encryption method for any application. Solid encryption practices ensure security, preserve privacy, and protect vital information.
