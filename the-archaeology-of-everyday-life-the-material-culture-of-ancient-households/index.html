The Archaeology of Everyday Life: The Material Culture of Ancient Households


Archaeology, the study of human history and prehistory through the excavation of material remains, offers us fascinating insights into the lives of people who lived thousands of years ago. This is particularly true in the area of ancient households, where everyday objects and structures can reveal much about the daily lives of our ancestors.

Ancient households are not just dwellings but are also microcosms of society, reflecting the culture, values, and beliefs of the people who lived within them. Excavating these sites provides a unique window into the past, revealing how people of different times and places managed their lives.

One key aspect of ancient households is their organization. Household organization varied depending on the culture and social structure. In some societies, households were part of larger, more extended family units. In others, the household was more nuclear, with close family members living together.

In Athenian households of ancient Greece, for instance, the nuclear family typically consisted of a father, his wife, their children, and sometimes slaves or non-family members living under the same roof. This organization was centered around the hearth, a crucial focal point in the house. This focal point was not only a place for cooking but also a gathering place for the family.

Similarly, ancient Roman households were built around the principle of the “paterfamilias,” the father of the family who had final authority over the household. The family would typically comprise nuclear family members, along with any slaves, freedmen, or other dependents. The layout of the house, with a series of interconnected rooms, highlighted one of the central features that separated the Roman household from the Greek: privacy.

Contrarily, the indigenous Pueblo people of the American Southwest had a lesser reliance on privacy. Their households were built around a central hearth, with smaller cooking areas scattered throughout the household. Family organization was centered on a communal lifestyle, with several generations living and working together in one large ceremonial room.

Apart from organization, households also contain crucial material culture, objects that had value to the people who used and created them. Material culture tells us about practical concerns like work and industry, but also about the less tangible aspects of life like belief and identity.

For example, artifacts discovered in ancient Greek households, such as pots and figurines, reveal much about daily life. Pots, for instance, were used for food storage, cooking, and serving. The decorations on these pots tell us more about the culture’s adornment trends and the objects’ social significance during that time.

Similarly, terracotta figurines tell us about religious beliefs. Such artifacts reveal how the ancients worshipped their gods and how those gods were viewed in society. Not only do they inform us of the cultural views, but they also deny us a glimpse of the marvelous craftsmanship of the artisans who created them.

Lastly, ancient households provide us with information about the economy too. Excavations of well-preserved sites like Pompeii reveal common commercial practices. By understanding these practices, we can learn more about trade, economy, and money management in ancient societies.

Through archeological explorations of ancient households, we gain a profound perspective on what life in the past was like. Every object, every structure tells a story. By unravelling these stories, we not only learn about our ancestors but also draw connections to the present.

The material culture of ancient households offers us fascinating insights into the past, from the daily chores and rituals to beliefs and social structures. By exploring their archaeology, we construct an enriching narrative about the past and deepen our understanding of human history.
