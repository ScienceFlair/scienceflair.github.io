Global Health Policy: An Intersectional Approach


In a world where health issues transcend borders and impact populations in diverse ways, the need for a comprehensive and inclusive approach to global health policy has never been more evident. Global health policy, at its core, concerns the decisions, actions, and strategies implemented by governments, organizations, and stakeholders to address health challenges on a worldwide scale. However, to truly address the complex and interconnected nature of health disparities, an intersectional approach is crucial.

Understanding Intersectionality in Global Health Policy

Intersectionality, a concept first introduced by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw, emphasizes the interconnected nature of social categorizations such as race, gender, class, and more, and how these intersect to create unique experiences of discrimination and privilege. Applying this framework to global health policy acknowledges that individuals exist at the intersection of multiple identities and factors that influence their health outcomes.

When crafting global health policies through an intersectional lens, policymakers consider how various social determinants of health interact to shape disparities. For example, a policy designed to improve access to healthcare services must account for not just geographical barriers but also socio-economic status, gender norms, cultural beliefs, and other intersecting factors that influence an individual’s ability to seek and receive care.

Advantages of an Intersectional Approach

  1. Tailored Interventions: By recognizing the diversity of experiences within populations, an intersectional approach allows for more tailored and effective interventions. Policies can be designed to address the specific needs of marginalized groups who may face multiple layers of discrimination.

  2. Holistic Solutions: Rather than addressing health issues in isolation, an intersectional approach encourages holistic solutions that consider the broader social, economic, and political contexts in which health disparities arise. This can lead to more sustainable and impactful outcomes.

  3. Promoting Equity: Prioritizing intersectionality in global health policy promotes equity by challenging existing power structures and addressing systemic inequalities. It shifts the focus from a one-size-fits-all approach to one that aims to reduce disparities and promote health equity for all.

Challenges and Considerations

While an intersectional approach offers numerous benefits, implementing it in global health policy comes with challenges. One key challenge is the need for data collection and analysis that captures the complexity of intersecting identities and experiences. Additionally, shifting existing policy frameworks to be more inclusive and equitable requires buy-in from diverse stakeholders and a commitment to dismantling entrenched systems of oppression.

Moving Towards a More Inclusive Future

As the global community grapples with pressing health issues such as the COVID-19 pandemic, climate change, and emerging infectious diseases, the importance of adopting an intersectional approach to global health policy cannot be overstated. By centering diversity, equity, and inclusion in policy-making processes, we can work towards a future where health outcomes are improved for all individuals, regardless of their social identities.

In conclusion, global health policy must evolve to embrace intersectionality as a guiding principle in addressing health disparities and promoting well-being on a global scale. By recognizing the interconnected nature of health determinants and advocating for policies that are inclusive and equitable, we can pave the way for a healthier and more just world for generations to come.

Remember, a world where health policies are intersectional is a world where everyone has the opportunity to thrive. Let’s continue to push for policies that prioritize inclusivity and equity in global health initiatives.

Embracing intersectionality is not just a theoretical concept but a practical necessity in creating impactful and equitable health policies worldwide.
