Fungal Fashion: Exploring the Use of Mushroom Materials in Textiles and Apparel


Have you ever considered the true cost of fashion? The industry is notorious for its negative impact on the environment, generating mountains of waste and pollution as well as exacerbating social issues, such as exploitation and inequality. This has led to an increasing call for eco-friendly alternatives, and a new, unusual contender has emerged: mycelium, the vegetative part of a fungus that produces mushrooms. Welcome to the fascinating world of fungal fashion – the next big textile revolution.

Before we delve into the potential of mycelium in fashion, let’s first have a look at the broader textile landscape and why we need a sustainable alternative. The industry is the second most polluting globally, behind only oil. Every year, billions of gallons of water, mega-tons of chemical pesticides and fertilizers, and vast amounts of energy are consumed in cotton production, not to mention the impact of dyes, finishes, and transporting garments all around the world. In addition, fast fashion has created a culture of disposable clothing, further compounding the industry’s environmental drawbacks. It’s not a pretty picture.

Mycelium, on the other hand, is proving to be a game-changer. Made of organic waste and bound together by a natural adhesive, it can be molded into textiles using a one-step process, biodegrading within weeks after disposal. MycoTEX, the company behind much of this research, says it could eliminate the need for toxic dyes and finishes. Moreover, this sustainable material uses significantly less energy than traditional textiles, and no fertilizers, pesticides, or water is necessary in its cultivation.

Admittedly, pursuing commercial fashion production with mycelium remains a challenge. The material currently has its limitations: it can be quite delicate and is better suited for lightweight clothing. However, scientists and fashion designers are working hard to develop new techniques in mycelium cultivation, aiming to make the material more robust and versatile. Mycelium-based leather alternatives, like those from Mushroom Materials – famous for its mushroom leather – have already gained traction, prompting designer Stella McCartney to launch a eco-friendly mushroom leather handbag collection.

Alongside the development of fungi-based textiles, designers and companies are experimenting with innovative ways to incorporate mycelium into existing fashion practices. By collaborating with biologists, designers create unique clothing that is both avant-garde and sustainable. London-based fashion designer Philip Currie is an excellent example. He has showcased garments made from mushroom materials, including a beautifully crafted, seamless dress that highlights the beauty of mycelium textiles. Similarly, organic clothing company, Vegea, creates fabrics from cypress tree leftovers, making use of fungal technology to produce fully bio-based garments with a zero environmental impact.

Apart from the ecological benefits, mycelium-based textiles also boast a unique aesthetic appeal that sets them apart from standard fabrics. The material can naturally incorporate a range of diverse textures and patterns, inspired by nature itself, making it an exciting option for designers and buyers alike.

But why stop at clothing? The versatility of mycelium textile also opens the doors for a myriad of fashion items. Shoes, handbags, and accessories – all can be made by utilizing the incredibly adaptable material. And let’s not forget that, being compostable, these items won’t end up in a landfill once they’re worn out.

The adoption of mycelium material in fashion has taken a promising and innovative step forward. Guided by passionate designers, scientists, and eco-friendly advocates, fungal fashion has the potential to transform not only the industry itself but also the way we think about fashion consumption.

While mycelium-based textiles have yet to go mainstream, early adopters in the fashion world are expressing optimism about the future of fungal fashion. In conclusion, this emerging field represents a promising opportunity for fashion enthusiasts, brands, and the environment to symbiotically flourish, highlighting the remarkable power of nature to provide sustainable alternatives for our consumptive habits. So, let’s watch this space — the fascinating fungal fashion revolution is just getting started.
