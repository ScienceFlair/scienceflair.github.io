The Effects of Plastic Pollution on Marine Animals


Plastic pollution has become a significant environmental issue, with its impact felt worldwide. The debris has reached even the most remote and untouched areas of the world, affecting the ocean’s inhabitants, their habitats, and the overall ecosystem.

Marine animals, especially those at the top of the food chain, are the most vulnerable to the harms caused by plastic pollution. Turtles, fish, seabirds, and mammals as diverse as whales and seals – all have fallen victim to the dangers posed by plastics in the ocean. The ingestion of plastic debris leads to various health problems, reduced lifespan, and even death among marine life.

Physical Harm and Entanglement

The first and most apparent harm of plastic pollution is the physical harm it causes to marine animals. Plastic products such as pellets, bottle caps, and fishing gear entangle marine creatures like turtles, seals, and fish, often leading to drowning or being unable to escape. Ingesting plastic also causes physical injury or blockage in their digestive system.

Plastic ingestion has a significant impact on the digestive tract of marine animals. When ingested, plastic particles can cause stomach blockages or lesions as the body tries to break down or dismiss the foreign material. Plastic buildup in the stomach can lead to uncomfortable bloating, malnutrition, and eventual death.

Bioaccumulation and Biomagnification

One of the most startling aspects of plastic pollution in the ocean is its potential for bioaccumulation and biomagnification. Ingested plastic particles accumulate in an animal’s body and can be transferred within the food chain. Marine animals occupying the highest trophic levels, such as tuna and large predatory fish, are at particular risk, accumulating plastic particles in their fatty tissues. This accumulation in their bodies can lead to severe health complications, ultimately affecting the entire food chain.

Disruption of Essential Behaviors

Plastic pollution can interfere with essential behaviors of marine animals, leading to significant consequences. For example, sea turtles use sand to lay their eggs, which can now be contaminated with plastic debris that authorities are trying to remove from the sand. Plastic can also disrupt foraging patterns, making it challenging for animals to find edible prey and limit the availability of suitable habitats.

Moreover, sounds emitted by discarded plastic cans, bottles, and balloons can disrupt the communication methods of marine animals, making it difficult for them to navigate and communicate. It affects the already fragile situation of marine life, often time-sensitive and temperature-sensitive, making the conservation mission even more urgent.

Conclusion

Plastic pollution is a multifaceted problem. It harms not just the environment and marine life but several industries and the human population. As global consumers and inhabitants of this planet, it is crucial for us to take note of these effects.

By making small changes in our use of plastic – recycling, disposing of it properly, and supporting eco-friendly solutions, we can help mitigate the effects of plastic pollution on marine animals. Together, we can make a change that positively impacts both marine and human life, providing a sustainable future for generations to come.
