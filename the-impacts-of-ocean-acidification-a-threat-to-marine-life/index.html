The Impacts of Ocean Acidification: A Threat to Marine Life


The world’s oceans, covering more than 70% of the Earth’s surface, are essential for sustaining life on our planet. However, the oceans are facing a significant threat known as ocean acidification. This process, driven by the increased absorption of carbon dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere, is causing a decrease in the pH levels of seawater. The consequences of this phenomenon are far-reaching and pose a serious threat to marine life and the overall health of ocean ecosystems.

Understanding Ocean Acidification

The industrial revolution, which began over 200 years ago, has led to a substantial increase in atmospheric CO2 levels due to human activities. As a result, the pH of surface ocean waters has fallen by approximately 0.1 pH units, representing a 30% increase in acidity. While this change may seem small, the logarithmic nature of the pH scale magnifies its impact significantly [1].

The rapid alteration in ocean chemistry, occurring over a relatively short timespan, has not allowed marine life, which evolved over millions of years in a relatively stable ocean pH, sufficient time to adapt. Consequently, the shells of some marine animals are already dissolving in the more acidic seawater. This is just one of the ways in which ocean acidification is expected to have dramatic and mostly negative impacts on ocean ecosystems. However, some species, especially those living in estuaries, are showing signs of adaptation to the changing conditions [2].

The Threat to Marine Life

Ocean acidification presents a two-fold challenge to marine life: decreased carbonate availability and increased acidity. Organisms that rely on carbonate-based shells and skeletons, as well as those sensitive to acidity, are particularly at risk. Furthermore, organisms higher up the food chain that feed on these sensitive organisms are also likely to be affected. The potential harm extends to a wide range of marine species, from phytoplankton to animals, and is projected to impact all areas of the ocean, from the deep sea to coastal estuaries [3] [4].

The impacts of ocean acidification are not uniform across all species. While some algae and seagrass may benefit from higher CO2 concentrations in the ocean, as they may increase their photosynthetic and growth rates, other marine species such as mollusks, corals, and certain varieties of plankton are at risk. The shells and skeletons of these animals may become less dense or strong, and their growth, development, abundance, and survival could be significantly affected [5].

Ecological and Human Health Implications

The effects of ocean acidification extend beyond marine ecosystems and have implications for human health. The chemical changes resulting from ocean acidification can directly impact the physiology of marine species consumed by humans, altering their survival and availability. This has the potential to affect the nutritional composition of food and, consequently, human health [6].

Furthermore, the threat of ocean acidification adds to the existing challenges faced by coastal and marine ecosystems, including warming waters, deoxygenation, melting ice, and coastal erosion. The combined impact of these stressors poses real threats to the survival of many marine species, especially those that build their skeletons and shells from calcium carbonate, such as clams, mussels, crabs, phytoplankton, and corals [7].

Conclusion

In conclusion, ocean acidification is a significant and growing threat to marine life and the health of ocean ecosystems. The rapid pace of acidification, coupled with the sustained nature of the change, poses challenges for the adaptation of marine organisms. It is imperative to strengthen scientific understanding and take urgent action to mitigate the impacts of ocean acidification. Addressing the root cause by acting on climate change is crucial to limit the progression of ocean acidification and safeguard the health and biodiversity of our oceans for future generations.
