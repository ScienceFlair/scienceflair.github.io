Astrobiology and Its Connection to Cosmology


Astrobiology, the interdisciplinary study of the origin, evolution, distribution, and future of life in the universe, is often perceived as a relatively modern field of scientific inquiry. Yet, its foundation lies in some of the most fundamental questions and theories of ancient cosmology, which aimed to explain the structure and behavior of the universe. A Look into Ancient Cosmologies In ancient Greek philosophy, one of the primary concerns was to understand the fabric of the cosmos. Cosmologists such as Aristotle and Anaximenes proposed theories to explain the natural world, some of which resonate with contemporary models of astrobiological research. Aristotle's Concept of Purposes In his "On the Heavens," Aristotle discussed the "purpose" (telos) of the different celestial bodies and their relationship with the universe's overall purpose. This line of thinking aligns with the biological concept of emergence, where individual components come together to form an entity that can exhibit behaviors the components cannot themselves exhibit. Emergence Theory This property of complex systems is pivotal to astrobiology as it can potentially explain how chemical reactions within ancient environments gave rise to life as we know it. Astrobiology seeks to decipher the process that led to these chemical reactions giving rise to structures capable of sustenance and information processing, laying the groundwork for the concept of life. Anaximenes' Views on Cosmogenesis Another ancient philosopher, Anaximenes, viewed the universe as a cosmic pyramid, where he believed air is the primary fundamental substance that takes on various characteristics to form solid, liquid, and gas bodies. His cosmic model of cyclical generation provides a precedent to the cyclic understanding of evolution we see in biology today, often referred to in the context of the panspermia hypothesis—where life or its seeds form in one stage of the cosmic cycle and might be preserved across different cycles through various forms and environments. The Harmony and Balance Early cosmologists found harmony and balance in their conceptualizations of the universe, attributes that echo today in discussions surrounding the potential distribution of life among the planets we observe in the universe. Does life need planets with atmospheres similar to ours, or does it adapt readily to environments starkly different? Contemporary Synthesis of Cosmology and Astrobiology Fast forward through the ages of scientific discovery. The universe no longer remains confined to a model of creation derived from myths of gods. What we observe are galaxies beyond galaxies, their motions governed by cosmological theories refined over the decades. It’s within these models that the seed of the notion of cosmic scales of life emergence is rooted, a subject being explored under various astrobiological disciplines today. Origins Astrobiologists examine hypotheses of abiogenic origins, theories that the compounds necessary for organic life arose before the appearance of life. On Earth, one of the strongest evidence of ancient life, besides fossil evidence and the chemical nature of some environments, comes from the observation that the presence of certain carbon dioxide isotopes significantly decreased as photosynthesis progressed during the past millions of years of Earth’s past. Geological Record Ancient chemical data from sediments and volcanic records provide insight into the potential diversity of the atmosphere in Earth's past, contributing to discussions in astrobiology, where understanding an Earth’s present and past allows for informed considerations of conditions we might encounter, or could imagine, elsewhere. The conditions could be far too different to replicate life in those environments as observed on Earth but could lead scientists to think innovatively about biological processes, expanding the possible understanding of what the universe holds within its celestial frameworks. Extrapolating Human Understanding of Evolution Considering contemporary astrobiological research through an ancient lens leads to questions surrounding the relationship of life within vast cosmic contexts: How universal, or limited in scope, does the existence and evolution of living organisms really hold? By what mechanisms and constraints do biological elements interact, in the process affecting the larger evolutionary trajectory? Furthermore, are potential extraterrestrial life forms confined to scenarios very similar to, or radically diverse from, ours? Linking Cosmic Origin and the Biosphere Connecting the vast spans of time as described by models of the origins of the cosmos to our place within it illuminates why Earth's biological world is studied using the language and tools of cosmo- science. Earth might be one celestial body with potentially unique chemical balances, an intriguing case among those we might investigate in our interstellar exploration that could teach scientists about diverse aspects of biological structures and behaviors far beyond Earth.
