The Anatomy of the Vocal Cords: How We Produce Sound


The human voice is a remarkable instrument, capable of producing a wide range of sounds, from the softest whisper to the loudest shout. At the heart of this incredible ability lies the vocal cords, also known as vocal folds. Understanding the anatomy of the vocal cords and the mechanics of sound production can provide valuable insights into how we communicate and express ourselves. In this post, we will explore the structure of the vocal cords, how they function, and the various factors that influence sound production.

Understanding the Vocal Cords

The vocal cords are two bands of smooth muscle tissue located in the larynx, or voice box, which is situated at the top of the trachea. They are covered by a thin layer of epithelial tissue, which helps protect them and allows for smooth vibration. The vocal cords are not actually cords in the traditional sense; rather, they are folds that can open and close to regulate airflow and sound production.

Structure of the Vocal Cords

The vocal cords consist of several layers, each playing a crucial role in sound production:

  1. Epithelium: The outermost layer, which provides a protective barrier and helps maintain hydration.
  2. Lamina Propria: This layer is divided into three sub-layers:
    • Superficial Layer: Often referred to as the “Reinke’s space,” this gel-like layer allows for maximum flexibility and vibration.
    • Intermediate Layer: Composed of elastic fibers, this layer provides support while still allowing for movement.
    • Deep Layer: Made up of collagen fibers, this layer offers strength and stability to the vocal cords.
  3. Vocalis Muscle: The innermost layer, which is a muscle that adjusts the tension and length of the vocal cords, influencing pitch and volume.

The Larynx: The Voice Box

The larynx plays a vital role in sound production. It houses the vocal cords and is responsible for regulating airflow from the lungs. The larynx is made up of several cartilages, including the thyroid, cricoid, and arytenoid cartilages, which work together to control the position and tension of the vocal cords.

How Sound is Produced

Sound production begins with the process of phonation, which occurs when air from the lungs is pushed through the closed vocal cords. This airflow causes the vocal cords to vibrate, producing sound waves. The frequency of these vibrations determines the pitch of the sound, while the amplitude of the vibrations affects the volume.

The Phonation Process

  1. Breath Support: The process starts with the diaphragm and intercostal muscles contracting to create negative pressure in the thoracic cavity, allowing air to be drawn into the lungs.
  2. Subglottic Pressure: As the lungs fill with air, pressure builds up beneath the closed vocal cords. This pressure is known as subglottic pressure.
  3. Vocal Cord Adduction: The vocal cords come together (adduct) to form a barrier against the airflow. This is controlled by the intrinsic laryngeal muscles.
  4. Vocal Cord Vibration: Once the subglottic pressure exceeds the pressure above the vocal cords, the cords are forced apart, allowing air to escape. As the air passes through, it causes the vocal cords to vibrate, producing sound.
  5. Resonance and Articulation: The sound produced by the vibrating vocal cords is then modified by the resonating chambers of the throat, mouth, and nasal passages. The shape and position of the tongue, lips, and soft palate further articulate the sound, allowing for the production of speech.

Factors Influencing Sound Production

Several factors can influence the quality and characteristics of the sound produced by the vocal cords:

1. Tension and Length of the Vocal Cords

The tension and length of the vocal cords are crucial in determining pitch. When the vocal cords are stretched and tightened, they vibrate at a higher frequency, producing a higher pitch. Conversely, when they are relaxed and shortened, they vibrate at a lower frequency, resulting in a lower pitch. The vocalis muscle plays a key role in adjusting the tension and length of the vocal cords.

2. Airflow and Subglottic Pressure

The amount of airflow and the pressure generated beneath the vocal cords significantly impact sound production. Increased airflow can lead to louder sounds, while controlled airflow can produce softer sounds. The balance between airflow and vocal cord tension is essential for producing clear and controlled speech.

3. Resonance

The shape and size of the vocal tract, including the throat, mouth, and nasal passages, influence the resonance of the sound produced. Different configurations can enhance or dampen certain frequencies, contributing to the unique quality of an individual’s voice.

4. Health and Hydration

The health of the vocal cords is vital for optimal sound production.
