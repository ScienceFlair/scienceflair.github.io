How Do Plants Respond to Biotic and Abiotic Stressors? The Role of Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS)


Plants are remarkable creatures capable of surviving in diverse environments, both existing (biotic) and non-living (abiotic). This remarkable resilience to changing circumstances is thanks to their innate ability to respond to stressors, such as drought, salinity, extreme temperatures, and pathogen assaults. The complexity of these responses is relacionado to reactive oxygen species (ROS).

ROS are molecule fragments created during various metabolic processes or under conditions of oxidative stress. They consist of molecules of Oxygen linked with other chemical elements, like hydrogen, superoxide, or hydroxyl radicals, all with one common feature: their high reactivity. Despite the negative connotation of the word “reactive,” ROS play a crucial roll in plant defense against stressors.

For starters, abiotic stress such as drought or extreme temperature triggers the production of ROS. They can cross-link proteins, causing modifications that can either activate or inhibit their function depending on the plant’s needs. For instance, cross-linking of proteins during vernalization or stratification required for seed germination has critical implications on the plants’ development process.

Similarly, ROS also catalyze the responses of biotic stress. This often works in association with the plants’ immune defense strategy. Upon pathogen invasion, ROS production increases. This increase can formulate a chemical barrier that neutralizes the infection.

Furthermore, plants can detect biotic or abiotic stressors and respond accordingly by inducing the production of ROS. Some plants exhibit hormesis under stress. It’s a phenomenon where exposure to low levels of a chemical triggers an improvement in the overall organism response to that stressor, much like how minor exposure to cold helps build the plant’s cold tolerance.

Briefly, ROS is complex and multi-faceted in their function for plants. They can damage cells or promote healing. Similarly, plants navigate complex biochemical scenarios triggered by ROS either by mitigating it to counteract stress, or channeling it as a weapon against pathogens, or allowing it to incite adaptation hormesis. ROS simply help in the survival of plants, an endeavor that could be considered paramount for over 400,000 known species of plants.

In a nutshell, understanding plant responses to stressors and the mechanisms behind them is vital for crop improvement efforts. ROS and their influence on plants could indeed be the key to unlocking a future of crops that can thrive in increasingly unpredictable environments, and adaptable to an overwhelming and ever-changing natural and unnatural pressures.
