Aquaculture Trade Policies and Agreements


Aquaculture, the farming of aquatic plants and animals, has become an increasingly significant source of food and livelihood globally. In recent years, international trade in aquaculture products has boomed, reaching $176.1 billion in 2018. However, unpredictable trade policies and agreements have challenged the growth and sustainability of this vital industry.

Trade Barriers and Their Impact

Trade policies and agreements can both foster growth and erect barriers in the aquaculture industry. One key challenge has been the imposition of tariffs on imports and exports. Tariffs raise the cost of traded goods, creating trade barriers that disadvantage producers and consumers alike. For aquaculture, this has occurred most notably in the United States-China trade wars, where tariffs on shrimp, salmon, and other products have disrupted the global market. Additionally, non-tariff barriers, such as overly strict sanitary and phytosanitary (SPS) measures, can impede trade flow.

Consequences of trade barriers include:

  • Higher prices for consumers as goods become more expensive due to tariffs and restricted trade.
  • Loss of revenue for producers, impacting their livelihoods and potentially jeopardizing the sustainable nature of their farms.
  • Reduced market access for some countries, potentially harming their economic growth.
  • Increased competition for a share in the already saturated global seafood market.

Progress Through International Agreements

Amid these challenges, international agreements have provided opportunities for cooperation and consistent growth. One such agreement is the World Trade Organization (WTO) Agreement on Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures (SPS Agreement), which aims to ensure that SPS measures are applied in a transparent and scientific manner. This agreement helps preclude trade barriers of an irrational nature.

Another essential development has been the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 14, which calls for the conservation and sustainable use of oceans, seas, and marine resources. Specifically, Target 14.7 seeks to enhance the sustainable management of our oceans by 2020. As aquaculture plays a critical role in feeding a growing global population and providing an alternative to over-fishing, its responsible cultivation and management is a key component of achieving this target.

Recent efforts have seen the establishment of organizations such as the International Seabass and Barramundi Growers Association (ISBG) and the Global Aquaculture Alliance (GAA), which contribute to shaping policy and encouraging responsible farming practices.

The Future of Aquaculture Trade Policies

To improve the global aquaculture trade, cooperation at both national and international levels is necessary. This includes reviewing and adjusting policies, streamlining bureaucratic processes, and establishing practical measures to guarantee the welfare of farmed aquatic species.

It is essential to develop better monitoring systems to track the environmental impact of farming and to guarantee compliance with established standards and guidelines. Technology can play a crucial role in solving some of these challenges, by improving production techniques, facilitating tracking, and fostering new markets.

In conclusion, overcoming trade challenges in the aquaculture industry is critical for its sustainable growth and to support the millions of people whose livelihoods depend on it. Investing in global cooperation, enhanced policies, and technological innovation will light up the future of the seafood market. The question is: Are we ready to seize this opportunity for a more prosperous and sustainable future?
