The Geomorphology of Planets: Planetary Science Perspectives


The bulk of our knowledge about planetary landscapes comes from studying our terrestrial neighbors, Earth and the Moon. However, in recent years, our understanding of geomorphology on celestial bodies has expanded exponentially, as advances in technology have allowed us to explore and better understand the diverse surfaces of Mars, Venus, and beyond. This essay will endeavor to discuss the geomorphology of planets from both the perspective of Earth-bound scientists and those who study extraterrestrial environments.

When discussing the Martian landscape, the first feature that comes to mind is the extensive network of canyons and valleys that stretch across the red planet. Curiously, some of these formations rival the sheer size of their Earth-bound counterparts, such as the Grand Canyon. The origin of these features bears a striking resemblance to those of the terrestrial variety: they are primarily the result of the action of water, albeit in the form of snow and brines, rather than liquid water. These small differences highlight the importance of considering a planet’s unique environment when studying its geomorphology.

Equally intriguing are the geological features on Venus, a planet often touted as an environment of extremes. As the second planet from the sun, Venus experiences a tremendous amount of solar radiation and is blanketed by a thick atmosphere, causing temperatures that can reach up to 900 degrees Fahrenheit. Given these harsh conditions, one might assume that Venus would have no liquid surface features. Quite the contrary—Venus hosts an enigmatic structure known as “tectonic rose petals,” reshaped from Earth-like domes and wrinkles. Despite the unforgiving environment, Venus still exhibits significant geological activity, revealing the complexities of the planets that remain to be explored.

Contrary to the classical image of a serene, icy moon, the Moon—including Earth’s nearest celestial neighbor—features a diverse range of geological environments. As the only natural satellite observed in high-resolution detail, the Moon has provided insight into the myriad effects of a gravitationally locked world. The polar regions of the Moon, in particular, contain vast reservoirs of ice situated in permanent shadows, challenging our understanding of what we consider a “non-habitable” environment. These findings display the necessity of looking beyond initial assumptions when contemplating geomorphology in planetary science.

The study of planetary geomorphology is a powerful tool, not only for understanding the surface features of other worlds but also for furthering our knowledge of Earth itself. By comparing our home planet with other celestial bodies, we may uncover the common threads that link them all. This line of inquiry allows scientists to delve deeper into the mysteries of our universe and, in some cases, may even push the boundaries of research for sustainable future colonization.

Ultimately, it is critical to emphasize that the study of planetary geomorphology is a collaborative, interdisciplinary effort rather than the realm of any one scientific discipline. Composited under this broad term of geomorphology, we find a wide array of fields, including geology, planetary science, and planetary exploration. Continued collaboration between these disciplines will ensure our collective understanding of planetary surfaces and all their fascinating intricacies continues to expand and evolve.
