Exploring Existentialism: What It Means to Exist


Existence is one of the most profound and enduring questions in human thought. Though it has puzzled humanity for centuries, existentialist philosophy offers a deeply personal and evocative framework for exploring what it means to exist. Rooted in the idea of individual freedom, choice, and the search for meaning, existentialism delves into the complexities of human life without shying away from the inevitable confrontation with absurdity, anxiety, and mortality.

At its heart, existentialism is not merely a philosophical doctrine, but an invitation to introspection—a mirror held up to the self, prompting us to question the very fabric of our being. As we embark on this exploration, we’ll uncover the origins of existentialist thought, its key themes and concepts, and how it resonates in everyday life.

The Emergence of Existentialism

While existentialist ideas can be traced back to ancient philosophy, existentialism as a formal movement emerged in the 19th and 20th centuries. Key figures like Søren Kierkegaard, Friedrich Nietzsche, Jean-Paul Sartre, Simone de Beauvoir, Martin Heidegger, and Albert Camus articulated the struggles and freedoms inherent in existence, offering a lens through which individuals could navigate the complexities of life.

Søren Kierkegaard is often regarded as the father of existentialism, emphasizing the importance of subjective experience and personal faith. He challenged traditional norms, proposing that each person must confront their existence individually, deciding what truth means to them and cultivating authenticity through choice.

Later, Friedrich Nietzsche’s declaration that “God is dead” symbolized the collapse of traditional moral and religious structures. His works explored the consequences of living in a world without inherent meaning and urged individuals to create their own values, embracing the concept of the “Übermensch”—a figure who rises above societal norms to live authentically.

The movement gained momentum in the 20th century, with Jean-Paul Sartre’s assertion that “existence precedes essence” capturing the crux of existentialist belief. Sartre contended that humans are born without predefined purpose; it is through our actions and choices that we define ourselves.

The Core Themes of Existentialism

Existentialism is characterized by a set of interwoven themes that guide its examination of human existence. Each theme reflects an aspect of the human experience, pushing us to confront life’s inherent uncertainties and recognize the potential for meaning within ourselves.

1. Freedom and Responsibility

At the center of existentialist thought lies the concept of freedom. Existentialism asserts that humans have the capacity for free will, enabling us to make decisions and chart our paths. However, this freedom is accompanied by immense responsibility. With no predetermined guidelines or external forces dictating our choices, we are solely accountable for the consequences of our actions.

This duality of freedom and responsibility can be both liberating and overwhelming. Jean-Paul Sartre referred to this as “anguish”—the awareness of our absolute freedom and the weight of making meaningful choices. Instead of evading this responsibility, existentialism encourages us to embrace it and take ownership of our lives.

2. The Absurdity of Life

One of the most challenging aspects of existentialism is its confrontation with the absurd—the conflict between humans’ desire for meaning and the universe’s apparent indifference. Albert Camus famously explored this theme in his essay The Myth of Sisyphus, drawing parallels between human life and the mythological figure condemned to roll a boulder up a hill, only to watch it roll down again for eternity.

While the absurd highlights life’s lack of inherent meaning, it also opens the door to creating personal meaning. Camus advocated for a “revolt” against the absurd—rejecting despair and embracing life fully despite its contradictions. This act of defiance affirms our humanity and transforms the absurd into a source of empowerment.

3. Authenticity

Existentialism champions the pursuit of authenticity, urging individuals to live in accordance with their true selves rather than conforming to societal expectations. To be authentic is to take responsibility for defining one’s identity and values, aligning actions with personal convictions rather than succumbing to external pressures.

Simone de Beauvoir expanded on the idea of authenticity, particularly in the context of gender and societal roles. Her groundbreaking work The Second Sex challenged the limitations imposed on women, arguing that liberation requires transcending imposed identities and embracing true autonomy.

4. Anxiety and Mortality

Human existence is inextricably linked to our awareness of mortality. Existentialists argue that coming to terms with the inevitability of death forces us to confront life’s fragility and urgency. This awareness can provoke existential anxiety, or a sense of unease that arises when we face our finitude and the uncertainty of our purpose.

Rather than viewing anxiety as negative, existentialists see it as a catalyst for growth. It compels us to examine our lives, prioritize what truly matters, and live more intentionally. Far from being paralyzing, this anxiety can serve as a wake-up call, driving us to seize the present moment.

Existentialism in Everyday Life

While existentialism may appear abstract or philosophical, its teachings permeate everyday life. The journey to find personal meaning and authenticity is one many of us navigate, often without consciously identifying it as existentialist thought.

For instance, career choices, relationships, and creative pursuits all reflect the existentialist tension between freedom and responsibility. Whether we seek a job that aligns with our passions or maintain a relationship that prioritizes mutual growth, we engage with these ideas through the decisions we make.

Similarly, grappling with uncertainty—whether facing life-altering events or contemplating the future—echoes the existential confrontation with the absurd. By embracing uncertainty instead of resisting it, we become active participants in shaping our own narratives.

The influence of existentialist themes also extends to culture, literature, and art. Novels like Albert Camus’s The Stranger or existentialist characters in films invite audiences to reflect on the struggles and paradoxes of existence, fostering empathy and introspection.

Challenges of Embracing Existentialism

Although existentialism offers powerful insights, it is not without its challenges. The invitation to take full responsibility for one’s life can be daunting, particularly in moments of doubt or hardship. Living authentically may require breaking away from societal norms or confronting uncomfortable truths about oneself. For some, the absence of predetermined meaning may provoke feelings of aimlessness or despair.

However, existentialism is not about providing definitive answers; rather, it is about embracing the uncertainty and multiplicity of existence. By engaging with these challenges head-on, we develop resilience and a deeper understanding of ourselves.

The Continuing Relevance of Existentialism

In an age of rapid technological change, global challenges, and cultural shifts, existentialism remains profoundly relevant. Its emphasis on individual freedom, self-awareness, and personal meaning resonates in a world where external pressures often try to dictate what it means to succeed or thrive.

By adopting existentialist principles, we can navigate the complexities of modern life with a renewed sense of purpose. Whether confronting existential crises, reevaluating priorities, or simply seeking greater authenticity, existentialism serves as a timeless guide to understanding what it means to exist.

In the end, existentialism does not demand that we solve the mysteries of existence—it simply reminds us to live fully, take responsibility for our choices, and embrace the infinite potential of our humanity. Through reflection and action, we can forge our own paths and create meaning in the face of life’s inherent ambiguity. It is in this act of creation, this constant becoming, that we truly exist.
