How to Conduct Health Policy Research


Health policy research is a critical component of public health practice, essential for informing decisions that impact the lives of millions. As healthcare systems evolve, policymakers need evidence-based insights to create policies that improve health outcomes, increase access to care, and reduce healthcare costs. Conversely, researchers seeking to influence policy decisions must navigate complex systems, balance competing priorities, and collaborate with stakeholders to mobilize effective policy changes. Setting Research Objectives Before embarking on a health policy research project, researchers must define a clear research question or objective. This entails identifying a pressing issue in healthcare, such as the impact of government-sanctioned Medicaid expansions on individual behavior, the effects of mental health services on quality of life, or the decreasing rate of opioid relapse. Once the question is defined, the researcher must:

Identify relevant context, e.g., policy and regulatory developments, emerging trends, and influences on the target population. Select a research approach that will best address the question. Justify the research design by explaining why it is the most reasonable choice.

Gathering Information When researching health policy issues, there are various ways to gather information.

Academic and Government Databases: Databases such as PubMed, ScienceDirect, and health.gov provide an array of literature, policies, and data. Surveys and Focus Groups: Surveys and focus groups are valuable tools for gathering input on health care priorities, health care access and use, and individual perspectives on health care related policies. Document Reviews: Document reviews help experts understand current policies, debates, and insights. Credible Sources and Tools: Various credible sources and tools help experts navigate data, evidence and related programs, including The Health Services Research Collaboratory, County Health Rankings & Roadmaps, and others.
