Induced Earthquakes: How Human Activities Trigger Them


Earthquakes, often perceived as natural phenomena tied solely to the movement of tectonic plates, are increasingly being influenced by human actions. While the vast majority of seismic activity originates deep within the Earth’s crust, a growing body of research reveals that certain human activities can disrupt the delicate balance of subsurface pressures, leading to what are known as “induced earthquakes.” These events shed light on the complex interaction between human development and geological processes—and understanding them is more important than ever.

What Are Induced Earthquakes?

Induced earthquakes are seismic events caused or triggered by human activity, as opposed to those arising from natural tectonic movements. They occur when activities such as resource extraction, waste disposal, or infrastructure development alter stresses in the Earth’s crust. Even minor changes in subsurface conditions can destabilize existing fault lines or create new pathways for seismic movement, resulting in tremors or significant earthquakes.

Although induced earthquakes have been documented for decades, advancements in geophysics and seismic monitoring have illuminated the scope and frequency of these human-triggered quakes, particularly in the context of modern industrial practices. These insights are crucial for understanding how certain activities can amplify seismic risk and what can be done to mitigate it.

Key Activities That Trigger Induced Earthquakes

1. Oil and Gas Extraction

One of the most well-documented sources of induced seismicity is oil and gas extraction. The very act of removing large quantities of hydrocarbons from deep underground reservoirs alters the balance of pressure within the surrounding rock formations. When fluids are withdrawn, the decrease in pressure can allow previously constrained fault lines to slip, leading to earthquakes.

In addition, methods such as enhanced oil recovery (EOR), which involves injecting fluids into the ground to displace more oil, exacerbate stress changes in the subsurface. Similarly, the extraction of natural gas, particularly through methods such as fracking, has been linked to changes in subsurface conditions. Although fracking-related tremors are usually of low magnitude, the cumulative impacts on fault lines can contribute to larger seismic events over time.

2. Wastewater Injection

Perhaps one of the most significant contributors to induced seismicity is the disposal of wastewater by deep injection into underground wells. This process, commonly used in the oil and gas industry to manage byproducts from extraction, introduces fluids at high pressures into deep reservoirs. These injected fluids can migrate into fault zones, lubricating them and reducing the friction that holds the faults in place. This increases the likelihood of slippage, triggering earthquakes.

Notably, some of the most prominent induced earthquakes in recent years—especially in areas like Oklahoma in the United States—have been linked to wastewater injection. The intensity and frequency of these earthquakes have demonstrated how seemingly localized activities can have larger-scale implications.

3. Reservoir-Induced Seismicity

Constructing large dams and filling reservoirs can also lead to seismic activity, a phenomenon known as reservoir-induced seismicity (RIS). When massive volumes of water accumulate in a reservoir, the weight exerts pressure on the crust below. Additionally, water may seep into faults and fractures within the rock, altering subsurface stress and potentially triggering earthquakes.

Some of the earliest cases of induced seismicity, such as tremors associated with the construction of the Hoover Dam in the United States, highlighted this phenomenon. RIS remains a concern today, especially with the construction of large reservoirs in seismically active regions.

4. Mining Activities

Mining operations, whether for coal, metals, or other resources, can also induce seismic events. By removing vast amounts of material from the subsurface, mining creates voids, alters stress fields, and weakens surrounding rock formations. In some cases, mining-induced seismicity can result in small earthquakes, while in others, significant tremors have been observed near major mining areas.

Underground coal mines and open-pit mines are particularly susceptible to inducing seismicity, as the ongoing excavation process continuously alters the local geological environment.

5. Geothermal Energy Production

Tapping into Earth’s geothermal energy—while a critical step toward transitioning to renewable energy—has also been linked to induced seismicity. The process of extracting geothermal energy often requires injecting water into hot rocks deep below the surface. This water changes the temperature and pressure within the rock, potentially triggering earthquakes. Known as Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS), this technology holds great promise but must be carefully monitored to avoid increasing seismic risks.

6. Carbon Sequestration

With the growing urgency to combat climate change, carbon sequestration is emerging as a critical technology to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. However, injecting carbon dioxide into underground geological formations also carries the risk of triggering seismic events. These injections, like those of wastewater, alter pressures in the subsurface and can destabilize fault zones.

The Global Perspective: Regions and Cases

Induced earthquakes have been recorded across the globe, with some countries experiencing more intense and frequent activity due to their geological predisposition and industrial practices. For example:

  • Oklahoma, USA: Once considered seismically quiet, Oklahoma experienced a dramatic rise in earthquake activity attributed to wastewater injection. Hundreds of earthquakes, including some of magnitudes over 5.0, rocked the region, raising public and scientific concern.
  • Groningen, Netherlands: Decades of natural gas extraction in the Groningen field led to induced earthquakes, culminating in significant damages to structures and growing public backlash, prompting the government to begin phasing out gas production.
  • China: The construction of the massive Three Gorges Dam reservoir is thought to be linked to increased seismic activity in the surrounding region.
  • South Korea: A geothermal energy project near Pohang triggered a 5.5-magnitude earthquake, one of the largest attributed to geothermal activity.

These examples underscore the global relevance of induced seismicity and the need for widespread monitoring and regulation.

Mitigating Induced Earthquakes: Strategies and Challenges

As the understanding of induced seismicity grows, so does the focus on mitigating its impact. Strategies include:

  1. Improved Monitoring and Risk Assessment
    Advanced seismic monitoring networks can help detect early warning signs of induced earthquakes, enabling industries to modify operations before significant events occur. Risk assessment frameworks tailored to specific activities can also help identify vulnerable areas.

  2. Regulatory Measures
    Governmental regulations play a crucial role in managing activities that contribute to seismicity. For instance, regulating fluid injection rates, controlling reservoir levels, and enforcing seismic monitoring for energy production projects can substantially reduce risks.

  3. Technological Advances
    Innovations in engineering and extraction techniques can help minimize the environmental and geological impacts of human activities. For example, optimizing the placement and pressure levels of fluid injections can prevent injection-induced tremors.

  4. Public Awareness and Communication
    Transparent communication between industry leaders, governments, and local communities is essential. Educating the public about induced seismicity fosters informed decision-making and encourages proactive engagement.

The Balance Between Development and Safety

As human activities continue to intersect with Earth’s geological processes, understanding and addressing induced seismicity remains paramount. From energy production to resource extraction, industrial practices must coexist harmoniously with the Earth’s natural systems. Striking this balance is challenging but necessary to mitigate risks to both infrastructure and communities.

By fostering collaboration between scientists, policymakers, and industries, it is possible to develop practices that minimize the likelihood of induced seismicity while enabling sustainable growth. As research continues to shed light on the mechanisms behind these human-triggered events, we take another step toward ensuring both the safety of communities and the long-term health of our planet.
