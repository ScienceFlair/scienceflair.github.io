Mental Health Parity: Chasing Equitable Access


Mental health parity, long a pipe dream for millions of individuals worldwide, is slowly coming into focus. Though significant strides have been made over the past two decades toward equitable access to mental healthcare, much work remains. For far too long, the stigma surrounding mental illness has impeded progress in mental healthcare reform. The good news? Those with mental illness are no longer alone in their fight for parity.

Thinking about mental health in terms of parity means considering equal access to care for those suffering from mental illness. This concept builds on the well-established principle of equal treatment under the law. Today, mental healthcare advocates and patients around the world persist in a multifaceted campaign for affordable, non-discriminatory mental health services. And while mental health parity may never be fully achieved, the pursuit of parity has brought many positive changes to mental healthcare systems worldwide.

One of the most promising developments in mental healthcare has been the introduction of technology-based platforms for the delivery of mental health services. Teletherapy, telepsychiatry, and telecounseling have made mental healthcare more accessible and convenient than ever before. As these technologies continue to develop, they hold the promise of even greater strides for mental health parity.

In the United States, the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act of 2008 has been the most significant legislative undertaking toward achieving mental health parity. Mental health advocates hailed this legislation as a watershed moment, but the path toward true mental health parity has been slow and uneven.

The law mandates that group health plans, group and individual health insurance coverage, and employee welfare benefit plans cannot impose more stringent financial requirements or treatment limitations for mental health and substance use disorder benefits than those imposed for medical/surgical benefits. Mental health and substance use disorder benefits must be provided based on the same cochlear plan and coverage parameters.

Despite these measures, however, several obstacles persist in the quest for mental health parity. The stigma of mental illness remains an intractable problem, causing many individuals to white. As a result, many remain untreated, unaware of the mental healthcare resources available to them. Additionally, barriers to access within the medical community itself prevent some healthcare providers from advocating for change.

As we push forward to attain mental health parity, it’s essential to develop the right kind of legislation, policies, and services to meet the needs of those with mental illness. Ongoing advocacy and education are indispensable. Recognition of the intersections between mental health and other aspects of human life, such as income inequality, is also vital.

Remember that progress has been made, but much more is left to do. Together, we can continue to advocate for and create mental health parity. Change may not always be swift, but the pursuit of equitable mental healthcare for all is the right fight to wage.
