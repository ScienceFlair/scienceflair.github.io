Aquaculture and Climate Change: Adapting to a New Normal


The global aquaculture industry has become a cornerstone of food security, livelihoods, and economic growth in many regions worldwide. It accounts for over half of the world’s seafood supply, providing a sustainable alternative to wild-caught fisheries and meeting the growing demand for protein. However, climate change is reshaping the ecosystem in which aquaculture thrives, presenting both challenges and opportunities.

The interplay between aquaculture and climate change is multifaceted, influencing everything from water temperature to ocean acidity and extreme weather events. These changing conditions demand innovative solutions to safeguard the resilience of aquaculture systems and ensure their long-term productivity.

The Twin Impact of Climate and Aquaculture

Rising Temperatures and Species Vulnerability

As global temperatures climb, freshwater and marine ecosystems face undeniable strain. Warmer water temperatures can significantly affect the physiology, growth, and reproduction of aquaculture species. For instance, many finfish and shellfish species are sensitive to specific temperature ranges, and even minor deviations can disrupt their metabolic processes.

Temperature shocks are particularly harmful during developmental phases such as larval and juvenile stages. Prolonged heatwaves can lead to mass mortality events, threatening species like salmon and shrimp, which are foundational to the global aquaculture market. In tropical regions, the challenges intensify as baseline temperatures in aquaculture systems are already high, leaving little margin for further warming.

Ocean Acidification

Increased carbon dioxide (CO2) absorption by the world’s oceans, a consequence of excessive emissions, has led to a phenomenon known as ocean acidification. This chemical shift in seawater poses a grave risk to calcifying organisms such as oysters, clams, and mussels, whose shells and skeletons depend on carbonate ions that become scarcer as pH levels drop.

Shellfish farming, a significant subsector of aquaculture, has already experienced the impacts of acidified waters. For example, significant larval die-offs in oyster hatcheries have been linked to reduced pH levels, causing economic losses to farmers and ripple effects through supply chains.

Increased Frequency of Extreme Weather Events

The rise in extreme weather events, including hurricanes, floods, and prolonged droughts, is disrupting aquaculture operations across the globe. In coastal regions, storm surges can destroy aquaculture infrastructure, wash away farmed stock, and reduce water quality by introducing pollutants and sediments into aquatic systems. In extreme cases, entire communities reliant on aquaculture are left vulnerable to economic collapse.

Conversely, prolonged droughts can diminish freshwater availability, disrupting inland aquaculture systems and competing with other critical freshwater usage, such as agriculture and drinking water. Planning for water use efficiency has become as essential as adapting to climate variability.

Adapting Aquaculture to a Changing World

Despite these mounting challenges, the aquaculture sector is uniquely positioned to be a leader in climate adaptation and sustainability. With proactive strategies, aquaculture operations can both mitigate risks and seize opportunities in this evolving landscape.

Selective Breeding and Resilient Species

One promising approach to addressing the challenges of climate change is the development of more resilient aquaculture species through selective breeding programs. Advances in genetics research have enabled the production of fish and shellfish strains that exhibit greater heat tolerance, faster growth rates, and resistance to disease.

For example, tilapia farming—one of the most widespread aquaculture practices globally—has benefited from the selective breeding of strains that thrive in higher temperatures and low-oxygen environments. Crisp synergies between science and practical aquaculture management can ensure sustainability while meeting production targets.

Additionally, diversifying species cultivated in aquaculture offers another adaptive measure. Seaweed and microalgae farming are gaining attention for their minimal environmental impact, quick growth cycles, and ability to sequester carbon. These aquatic crops offer a versatile, low-cost protein source while contributing to climate mitigation strategies.

Embracing Technology and Innovation

The use of technology is proving transformative in adapting aquaculture systems to the “new normal.” Smart sensors and Internet of Things (IoT) devices are becoming valuable tools for real-time monitoring of water quality, temperature, and oxygen levels. Early warnings of stress conditions allow farmers to make timely decisions, such as increasing aeration or adjusting feeding regimes.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) and machine learning tools are being used to analyze historical climate data and predict aquaculture yields under varying scenarios. Armed with better forecasting, farmers can optimize resource allocation and reduce financial risks tied to climate fluctuations.

Another innovation gaining ground is the use of Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS). Unlike traditional open-water systems, RAS allows farmers to control water quality and temperature more precisely. This approach reduces vulnerability to external weather events and is particularly beneficial for inland aquaculture operations.

Sustainable Feed Practices

One crucial element of climate-resilient aquaculture lies in improving feed formulations and reducing reliance on wild fish stocks. Traditional feed often incorporates fishmeal and fish oil, which can contribute to overfishing in wild stocks and exacerbate carbon emissions from feed production and transport.

Sustainable alternatives like plant-based and insect-based feeds are not only reducing aquaculture’s environmental footprint but also offering cost-effective solutions to farmers. Algae-based feed, in particular, is gaining popularity for its rich nutrient content and carbon footprint mitigation potential.

Policy Support and Community Engagement

Beyond technological advancements, proactive policies and community engagement play a pivotal role in fostering climate resilience within aquaculture. Governments and international organizations must prioritize climate-smart aquaculture policies that promote sustainable resource use and environmental stewardship.

Engaging aquaculture communities in climate adaptation strategies ensures that solutions are practical and tailored to local conditions. Farmers and stakeholders are often the first to observe climate impacts, making their input invaluable for designing adaptive measures. Cooperative models, knowledge exchange programs, and financial incentives can empower farmers to implement best practices.

Opportunities in the Midst of Challenges

While the realities of climate change present significant challenges, the aquaculture sector is uniquely positioned to contribute to climate-positive solutions. Sustainable aquaculture practices, when scaled appropriately, can play an active role in global climate mitigation efforts.

For example, seaweed farming is emerging as a potential climate solution due to its ability to absorb large quantities of CO2 while requiring minimal freshwater and inputs. Seaweed also serves as a raw material for biofuels, biodegradable packaging, and even livestock feed, creating potential revenue streams for farmers embracing diversification.

Additionally, integrating aquaculture with other agricultural systems, such as rice farming, creates a symbiotic relationship where ecosystems support each other. This approach not only maximizes land use but also enhances food security and improves economic resilience for rural communities.

The Road Ahead

As we face a future shaped by climate change, aquaculture will require a collective effort across industries, researchers, policymakers, and farmers to adapt effectively and sustainably. Equipping the aquaculture sector with the tools, technologies, and knowledge to weather future storms is essential for the food security and livelihoods of millions worldwide.

The “new normal” created by climate change demands a paradigm shift in how the aquaculture industry operates. Embracing sustainable practices, investing in resilient infrastructure, and fostering innovation will ensure that aquaculture thrives in the decades to come.

By navigating these challenges thoughtfully, aquaculture can serve not only as a source of food and income but also as a solution to some of the most pressing environmental issues facing our planet. The future of aquaculture rests on its ability to adapt to these changing tides while maintaining its critical role in feeding a growing global population.
