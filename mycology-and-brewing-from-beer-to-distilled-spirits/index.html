Mycology and Brewing: From Beer to Distilled Spirits


Mycology, the study of fungi, has long been intertwined with the art of brewing. From the early days of beer production to the modern craft of distilled spirits, fungi have played a crucial role in shaping the flavors and aromas we associate with our favorite libations. In this blog post, we will explore the fascinating relationship between mycology and brewing, and uncover the hidden secrets behind the magic that happens in our glasses.

The Yeast Connection

At the heart of the mycology and brewing relationship lies the humble yeast. This microorganism, specifically the species Saccharomyces cerevisiae, is responsible for the fermentation process that turns simple sugars into alcohol. Yeast is a type of fungus that has been domesticated for thousands of years to produce beer, wine, and other fermented beverages.

Yeast performs its magic by consuming the sugars present in the brewing ingredients, converting them into alcohol and carbon dioxide. This process, known as fermentation, not only creates the alcohol content of the beverage but also imparts unique flavors and aromas. Different strains of yeast can produce a wide variety of flavors, ranging from fruity and estery to spicy and phenolic.

The Role of Fungi in Beer

Beyond yeast, other fungi can also influence the brewing process. One such example is the use of molds, such as Aspergillus, in the production of smoked beers. These molds are responsible for creating the distinct smoky aroma and flavor that characterizes this style.

Additionally, certain fungi, like Brettanomyces, can introduce desirable funkiness and complexity to beers. These wild yeast strains are often intentionally added to barrel-aged beers, where they interact with the wood and contribute unique flavors over time.

Expanding Horizons: From Beer to Distilled Spirits

While mycology has traditionally been associated with beer production, its influence extends to the world of distilled spirits as well. In recent years, craft distillers have embraced the use of fungi to create innovative spirits that push the boundaries of flavor.

One notable example is the use of koji, a type of fungus, in the production of traditional Japanese sake and Shochu. Koji is responsible for breaking down the starches in rice, making them accessible for fermentation. This process not only increases the yield of fermentable sugars but also adds complexity and depth to the final product.

Another exciting development in the realm of distilled spirits is the use of mushroom-infused spirits. By macerating mushrooms in alcohol, distillers can extract their unique flavors and aromas, resulting in distinctive and memorable spirits.

The Future of Mycology and Brewing

As our understanding of mycology and brewing continues to evolve, so too does the potential for innovation in the world of fermented beverages. With the rise of craft brewing and distilling, there is an increasing focus on experimentation and the exploration of new flavors.

Scientists and brewers alike are delving deeper into the world of fungi, discovering new strains of yeast and exploring their potential in creating unique and exciting beverages. From sour beers to mushroom-infused spirits, the possibilities are endless.

In conclusion, mycology and brewing have a long and intertwined history. From the yeast that ferments our beers to the molds and fungi that add complexity and character, fungi play a crucial role in shaping the flavors and aromas we enjoy in our drinks. As we continue to push the boundaries of fermentation, the future of mycology and brewing looks bright, promising even more intriguing and flavorful libations to come.

So, the next time you raise a glass, take a moment to appreciate the hidden world of fungi that made it all possible. Cheers!
