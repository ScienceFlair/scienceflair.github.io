Foods of the Past: Investigating Ancient Diets and Cooking Techniques


Long before the creation of modern convenience foods and processed snacks, humans were relying on locally sourced ingredients to survive. In investigating ancient civilizations’ diets, we can learn much about their societies, lifestyles, and even their expansions and declines. In this post, we’ll explore what people ate in bygone eras and how cooking techniques have evolved over millennia.

The diets of ancient societies varied widely based on their geographic locations, climates, and resources. For example, the societies that inhabited the Mediterranean basin had access to a plethora of fresh fruits and vegetables, olives, fish, and small game. In contrast, many Early Human and Neanderthal populations were largely carnivorous, as evidenced by their consumption of mammoth, oxen, and ample amounts of wild game.

As people settled in more stable communities, they began to rely more heavily on agriculture. This shift had profound effects on their diets and overall health. In ancient Mesopotamia, residents consumed barley and wheat cereals, legumes, vegetables, and occasional meat or fish. Meanwhile, the ancient Egyptians enjoyed a diverse diet that included figs, melons, dates, leeks, onions, and an array of vegetables, as well as the occasional poultry or fish.

These agricultural societies also developed early cooking techniques that are still in use today. The concept of fermentation, for instance, can be traced back to these early civilizations that lacked refrigeration. People to this day consume fermented foods such as yogurt, sauerkraut, and kimchi – a testament to their ancient roots.

The ancient Romans were known for viewing food as an art, with elaborate meals and recipes passed down through generations. They employed advanced cooking techniques such as using the “potager” or stew pot to cook multiple ingredients simultaneously, and the “platter basket” to facilitate easy serving. They also devised methods for preserving food through drying, pickling, and different forms of salting.

Food and cooking methods continued to evolve through time, as trade opened doors to new ingredients and techniques. During the Age of Discovery, Europeans were introduced to spices and exotic foods from their global explorations, such as vanilla, chocolate, and even pineapple.

Fast-forward to the present day, and our understanding of nutrition has expanded exponentially. While modern diets benefit from our widened knowledge of foods and ingredients from around the world, it’s crucial not to overlook the valuable lessons we can glean from the diets and cooking techniques of ancient civilizations. We can learn much about nutrition, sustainability, and even gastronomy from their rich and varied culinary traditions.

In conclusion, investigating ancient diets and cooking techniques can offer valuable insights into our own culinary practices and habits. As we continue to evolve as a society, let us not forget the wisdom of the past and carry forward their lessons for healthy, sustainable, and delicious eating.
