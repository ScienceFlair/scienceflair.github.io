The Psychology of Virtual Reality: Understanding Immersion


Virtual reality, or VR for short, has been increasingly present in our lives over the past decade. From gaming to education, training to tourism, the field is constantly expanding. As technology advances, so does our interaction with it. There’s something about VR that resonates deeply with human emotional and cognitive interaction.

At its core, VR allows us to immerse ourselves in another environment, often referred to as a ‘digital environment." For a psychologist, what exactly constitutes immersion in this virtual world and the impact of immersion on cognition and emotions is intriguing. Let’s delve deep into the ‘psychology of VR - immersion.’

The term ‘immersion’ is derived from the Latin ‘immersus’ for soaking, literally, ‘drowning’ in an experience. Same concept is used for VR; you are ‘drowning’ in the digital experience. Immersion is the degree to which a person can forget their real life while experiencing the virtual one.

The ability to decipher the depth of this immersion is vital on several levels, including sensorimotor contingency, presence, flow state, adaptation, spatial perception, and cognition.

Firstly, sensorimotor contingency: Since VR stimulates sensory systems without bodily actions, this discrepancy creates a mental disconnect. Cognitive psychologists call this ’the rubber hand illusion.’ VR has a way of amplifying this illusion, turning our senses and body perception against us.

Presence, a central aspect of virtual reality, refers to the user’s subjective experience feeling of being in the virtual environment, like it is reality. “Excessive presence” sometimes occurs, blurring the line between the person and the environment so intensely that it can lead to dissociation or uneasiness.

Next, the flow state: a mental state where a person is completely absorbed in what they are doing; a happiness expert’s dream. In VR, it’s possible to manipulate this feeling by tweaking visual and auditory cues.

Adaptation is the brain’s ability to adjust sensory organs to changes in the environment. VR can push the boundaries of adaptation, challenging our depth perception, for example, for the sake of improving technology.

Cognitive abilities are also affected. VR often requires intensive manipulation of working memory, problem-solving, and fine motor skills, therefore influencing cognition.

Regardless of context, immersion in VR engages users and can create a form of environmental flow state we experience in nature. This may open up new fields in understanding not just our response to immersive virtual environments but the value it holds in general psychology and human sensory-motor feedback loop.

At the end of the day, whether we use VR for gaming, therapy, architectural viewing, or scientific research, it’s the capacity for immersion that drives the true potential of this technology - for better or worse. As with any psychological topic, understanding the capacity and limitations of our brain is key.

As with any technological revolution we are just scratching the surface on what immersive psychology can offer. The opportunities, understanding and potential solutions VR may provide are endless, but so are questions we need to answer about the effects on human mind.

Overwhelm and hypnosis aside, it’s incredibly fascinating to immerse oneself in virtual reality, proving psychology and technology can, and do, mix to capture the essence of our curiosity. It’s remarkable how such technology has opened doors to answers about our cognitive and emotional selves we never had before.

This ever-evolving technology offers us profound opportunities to understand psychology and human experience in fresh new ways. Be ready to be surprised and humbled by the magnitude virtual reality can offer, for it’s not only the future but also the ‘here and now’ of our psychological understanding. We should embrace it, question it, study it. After all, aren’t we living in an Age of Immersive Psychology?
