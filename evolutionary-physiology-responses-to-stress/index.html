Evolutionary Physiology: Responses to Stress


Stress - a biological, psychological, and behavioral response to an imbalance that we often try to avoid. But why? And how did our ancestors manage it? Through the concept of evolutionary physiology, we can better understand how our bodies have evolved over time to respond to stress and survive in diverse and challenging environments.

We can trace the roots of human stress responses back to early human ancestors, whose lives were filled with more immediate, tangible threats. Danger was everywhere, and a quick response was crucial. If someone spotted a predator or simply needed to find food, the body needed to be primed to act. This rapid response has shaped our physiological development over thousands of years.

The human body has been shaped by natural selection to favor those who can effectively cope under stress. Physiological responses to stress primarily consist of two systems - the autonomic nervous system and the hormonal response, including the famous “fight or flight” mechanism famously studied by Hans Selye. These physiological changes serve to increase our capacity for self-preservation in times of distress.

The Autonomic Nervous System is responsible for the most immediate and drastic response to stress. Known as the “bellengermy” response, it consists of increased heart rate, blood flow to vital organs, and sharp cognitive focus. This response prepares the body for action, whether it be to fight or flee from danger.

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis, our body’s primary hormonal response system, starts with the release of Cortin Releasing Factor (CRF) by the hypothalamus. This release prompts the pituitary gland to release Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH), which instructs the adrenal glands to discharge hormones including cortisol.

The release of cortisol is what helps to prepare our bodies for the “fight or flight” response, influencing how we perceive and prepare for stress. It increases blood flow to the brain and skin, enhances blood sugar levels, and increases blood clotting. It also suppresses the immune system and digestion, diverting resources toward vital, life-saving tasks.

Stress responses vary according to the source of stress. Physical stress, such as hunger or thirst, can trigger the hormonal responses more immediately. However, non-physical stress, such as social pressure, can also trigger responses –in particular in the HPA. The latter can occur over a longer period, making us more vulnerable to diseases like obesity, hypertension, and depression.

However, understanding our evolved responses to stress can help uncover how we can better live with and cope with modern-day stressors. It underlines the importance of exercise and a healthy lifestyle, as well as mindfulness practices, to reduce chronic stress levels. Our bodies evolved under very different conditions than the ones we face now, and a clear understanding of evolutionary physiology could begin to shape a new path toward healthier ways of living.

Stress is a complex and nuanced concept, and further evolution will only make it more intricate. Our understanding of our bodies’ physiological responses to stress can inform us about when stress is good for us and when it is detrimental. Effectively managing stress is a crucial part of modern life, with implications for physical and mental well-being. Understanding its roots can give us valuable insight into how best to navigate these complex experiences. The evolution of our responses to stress is a tale of survival – and one we should respect in order to optimize our lives and health.
