Top 5 Bird Species to Spot During Spring Migration


Spring migration is a magical time for bird enthusiasts and nature lovers. As the cold grip of winter loosens, the skies come alive with the vibrant sights and sounds of birds traveling thousands of miles to reach their breeding grounds. For many, this annual phenomenon is not only a chance to witness astounding feats of endurance but also an opportunity to observe some of the most dazzling and beloved bird species. Let’s explore the top five bird species you should look out for during spring migration!

1. Ruby-throated Hummingbird

The Ruby-throated Hummingbird is the epitome of spring vibrance. Measuring just 3-3.5 inches, these tiny acrobats are a testament to nature’s incredible engineering. Males are particularly striking, with their iridescent ruby-red throats that shimmer in the sunlight. Each spring, these diminutive birds undertake an awe-inspiring migration, traveling thousands of miles from Central America back to breeding areas in the United States and Canada.

One of the most astonishing feats of the Ruby-throated Hummingbird is its non-stop, 500-mile journey across the Gulf of Mexico. This herculean effort is even more impressive considering the bird’s small size and delicate constitution.

If you’re hoping to spot a Ruby-throated Hummingbird this spring, put out hummingbird feeders filled with a mix of four parts water to one part sugar. They’ll also be drawn to flowering plants, especially those with tubular blossoms rich in nectar. Observing their rapid wingbeats and agile aerial maneuvers is sure to leave you captivated.

2. Baltimore Oriole

The Baltimore Oriole is a favorite among birdwatchers, thanks to its dazzling orange-and-black plumage and sweet, whistling songs. As these vividly colored songbirds return from their wintering grounds in Central and South America, their arrival signals that spring has fully arrived in many parts of North America.

Orioles are often found in open woodlands, along forest edges, and in parks or gardens. They are particularly drawn to fruit-bearing trees and shrubs. If you want to attract Baltimore Orioles to your yard, consider offering sliced oranges, grape jelly, or nectar feeders designed specifically for orioles.

Aside from their striking appearance, male Baltimore Orioles are known for their melodic songs, which they use to establish and defend territories. Spotting these birds among blooming trees or hearing their cheerful tunes wafting through the spring air is a quintessential migration experience.

3. American Redstart

The American Redstart is an elegant warbler with a flair for the dramatic. Its black and orange plumage (on males) and striking white wing patches make it instantly recognizable as it flits through trees and shrubs. Females and immature males sport subtle but still beautiful gray and yellow hues. Known for their active foraging behavior, these birds are often seen fanning their tails to flush insects from foliage.

As part of the spring migratory wave, American Redstarts move from Central and South America to North America, where they establish territories in forests and woodlands. They are particularly drawn to areas with dense understory vegetation, which provides ample cover and food sources. Birders may spot these lively songbirds in local parks, nature reserves, or even suburban areas with good tree coverage.

To lure an American Redstart to your backyard, consider planting native shrubs and trees, which encourage the insects they love to prey on. Many bird enthusiasts find that the dynamic appearance and charming behavior of the American Redstart make it one of their favorite species to observe during migration.

4. Sandhill Crane

Few sights in spring are as majestic as the migration of the Sandhill Crane. With their long, graceful necks, big wingspans, and iconic red crowns, these birds inspire admiration wherever they go. Unlike smaller songbirds, the Sandhill Crane travels in massive, noisy flocks, their resonant trills echoing through the air during their long journeys between wintering grounds in the southern U.S. and breeding grounds in northern North America.

One of the most famous gathering points for Sandhill Cranes during migration is along the Platte River in Nebraska. Here, thousands of cranes rest and feed in the shallow waters and surrounding fields, creating an impressive spectacle that draws birdwatchers from across the country.

These cranes prefer open wetland areas or agricultural fields, where they can forage for grains, invertebrates, and other small creatures. Their interactions, ranging from graceful courtship dances to shared feeding behavior, further add to their allure during the spring migration season.

5. Black-and-White Warbler

The final bird on this list may not boast the vibrant colors of some of its migratory companions, but the Black-and-White Warbler more than makes up for it with charm and unique behavior. True to its name, this small warbler sports a bold black-and-white-striped appearance and has an interesting foraging style—it moves along tree trunks and large branches like a nuthatch, gleaning insects from the bark.

The Black-and-White Warbler begins its journey in Central and South America before making its way to wooded areas throughout North America. Unlike most warblers, this species favors mature forests, including deciduous and mixed woods. Its high-pitched song is a familiar sound during spring for those wandering through wooded trails.

If you wish to attract Black-and-White Warblers to your backyard, providing native plants and trees helps promote a healthy habitat for the insects they seek. Their bold patterns and enthusiastic feeding activity make them an exciting bird to spot during their annual migration.

How to Prepare for Spring Migration Birding

Spring migration can seem fleeting, with many species passing through your area for only a few weeks. To make the most of this incredible phenomenon, preparation is key. Here are a few tips to help you catch the spring migration wave:

  1. Know the Timing: Each species follows its own migratory schedule. Research when specific birds are expected to pass through your region. Local birding guides, apps, and online databases can help track migration progress.
  2. Scout Hotspots: Spring often concentrates birds around food-rich habitats such as wetlands, forests, or coastal areas. Identify birding locations near you and plan visits during migration peak.
  3. Equip Yourself: Binoculars and a field guide are invaluable tools for identifying migratory birds. Bring a notebook—or use an app—to record your sightings.
  4. Enhance Your Yard: Provide bird-friendly habitats by planting native vegetation, offering feeders, and ensuring water sources are available. A welcoming yard can attract a surprising variety of migratory visitors.

Spring migration offers a unique and fleeting chance to connect with the natural world. Whether you’re marveling at the endurance of a Ruby-throated Hummingbird, reveling in the melodic calls of a Baltimore Oriole, or witnessing the grandeur of a Sandhill Crane flock in flight, the experience promises to leave a lasting impression. By heading outdoors and taking in the splendor of migrating birds, you’ll not only deepen your appreciation for wildlife but also become part of an age-old tradition that celebrates the rhythms of nature.
