The Intersection of Cultural Norms and Global Health


Global health is an ongoing challenge that requires the collaboration of nations, organizations, and individuals worldwide. To tackle this challenge effectively, it is essential to consider and understand cultural norms. Cultural norms can significantly impact global health efforts, either positively or negatively, depending on how well they are understood and integrated into health interventions and policies. In this article, we will explore the intersection of cultural norms and global health.

CULTURAL VARIABLES IN GLOBAL HEALTH

Cultural variables play a substantial role in the formulation of health-related behaviors and practices. These variables include:

a) Beliefs and Values: Cultural beliefs and values are central to people’s lives, dictating what they consider healthful behavior. For example, some cultures value exposing their skin to sunlight, which increases their risk of skin cancer. Other cultures prioritize privacy and modesty, making it challenging to discuss sensitive health issues openly, leading to delayed medical treatment.

b) Social Structure: Cultural social structures shape the distribution of resources, power, and responsibilities. In some cultures, there are remnants of hierarchical structures where individuals in positions of power may enjoy better health care access than the rest of the population. Conversely, some cultures emphasize collectivism, where people prioritize the group’s wellbeing over their individual health.

c) Health Care System: Cultural norms influence how nations structure their health care systems. Some cultures prefer traditional medicine, while others rely on modern technology. The availability, affordability, and accessibility of healthcare in nations also vary due to cultural considerations.

d) Disease Perception: Cultural perceptions of diseases shape people’s behaviors in the face of illness. Some cultures view diseases as solely physical, while others acknowledge psychosocial or spiritual dimensions. Perceptions influence vaccination rates, disease prevention, and treatment-seeking behaviors.

THE CHALLENGE OF CULTURAL DIVERSITY IN GLOBAL HEALTH

The complexities of cultural variables present considerable challenges to global health initiatives. Health policies and practices must be aware of these cultural nuances to be effective. It is essential to incorporate cultural understandings in the following ways:

  1. Contextual Awareness: It is vital to understand cultural variables within a specific context. For instance, the effect of the “fente” (twelve-hour traditional childbirth practice) on maternal health varies between Togo and Burkina Faso, necessitating context-specific solutions.

  2. Knowledge Translation: Communication and knowledge translation must be culturally relevant, respecting cultural beliefs on the use of languages, symbols, and modes of communication.

  3. Program Development: Global health interventions should design programs in collaboration with local communities, integrating cultural understandings into development, implementation, and evaluation.

  4. Capacity Building: Investment in local human and institutional capacities is necessary for long-term sustainable solutions. It is crucial to develop local expertise with cultural sensitivity, understanding, and respect.

  5. Policy Implementation: Cultural understanding must inform public health policies, ensuring that they are contextually relevant and sensitive to local needs, beliefs, and values.

CONCLUSION

The intersection of cultural norms and global health is a critical area of focus for the effective implementation of health interventions and policies. Understanding cultural variables can enhance healthcare access, prevention, and treatment strategies, ultimately contributing to global health progress. Conversely, the absence of cultural understanding can hinder progress and result in missed opportunities and potential harm.

In the fight against global health challenges, enhancing cultural literacy is a crucial step towards positive change. As such, global health actors must prioritize cultural understanding, begin with the end in mind, and implement culturally appropriate strategies for the benefit of all.
