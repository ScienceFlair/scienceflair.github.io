What Happens to a Star When It Becomes a Red Giant?


When a star reaches the end of its life cycle in its current state, it begins to transform dramatically. For stars similar in mass to our Sun, this transformation marks the transition to a red giant—one of the most breathtaking yet destructive phases in a star’s evolution. The journey of a star from its tranquil main sequence phase to the fiery existence of a red giant is a story of change driven by physics, fusion, and stellar balance.

The End of Hydrogen Fusion in the Core

For the bulk of a star’s life, it exists in a state of equilibrium, a harmonious balance between the inward pull of gravity and the outward pressure created by nuclear fusion in the core. During this phase, hydrogen fusion powers the star, converting hydrogen atoms into helium via nuclear reactions. However, no fuel source lasts forever. Over time, the hydrogen in the core of the star becomes depleted.

Once the core exhausts its supply of hydrogen, the outward pressure that once countered gravity diminishes. Gravity takes over, causing the core to contract. This contraction has profound effects on the star as a whole and sets the stage for its eventual transformation into a red giant.

Core Contraction and Hydrogen Shell Burning

The core’s contraction raises its temperature and pressure. However, while the core continues to shrink, hydrogen fusion isn’t entirely over. Around the contracting helium core, there’s still a layer, or “shell,” where hydrogen can continue fusing into helium. This process is known as hydrogen shell burning. The increased temperature and energy output caused by this shell burning generate an enormous amount of heat, which pushes the outer layers of the star outward.

As the star’s outer layers expand, it grows in size and becomes a red giant. The expansion also cools the outer layers, giving the star its characteristic reddish hue. It’s during this phase, when hydrogen shell burning dominates, that the star becomes the luminous, swollen celestial body we associate with red giants.

The Transformation Into a Red Giant

The most dramatic change in this stage of a star’s life is its size. A medium-sized star, such as our Sun, can reach sizes hundreds of times its original radius as it transforms into a red giant. For comparison, if the Sun were to become a red giant, its outer layers might expand as far as the orbit of Earth—or even beyond.

As the star swells, its brightness increases dramatically. This increase in luminosity occurs even as the star’s outer layers cool. The cooling explains the reddish coloration associated with red giants, while the inner layers that remain hot and dense continue to sustain the fusion occurring in the shell.

Helium Fusion in the Core

As the hydrogen shell continues burning outside the core, the core itself becomes dominated by helium that was formed earlier in the star’s life. The contracting core heats up further, eventually reaching a critically high temperature and pressure where helium can begin to fuse into heavier elements like carbon and oxygen. This process, termed helium flash, occurs abruptly in some stars. Once helium fusion begins, a new source of energy stabilizes the core temporarily, creating an internal balance akin to what the star experienced during its earlier main sequence phase.

However, the helium core fusion process is relatively short-lived in cosmic terms. Helium stores are depleted more rapidly than hydrogen, bringing the star closer to the end stages of its life.

Changes in the Star’s Stability

The red giant phase is inherently unstable. As helium fusion starts and stops in cycles, the star may go through pulsations, shedding its outer layers in the process. During this time, stellar winds grow stronger, and the material blown off contributes to the enrichment of the surrounding interstellar medium. These winds can spread heavier elements, like carbon and oxygen, across the cosmos—materials that play a crucial role in forming planetary systems, life, and other stars.

The Endgame for a Red Giant

The ultimate fate of a star once it enters the red giant phase depends on its mass:

  1. Stars with Low to Medium Mass (Up to 8 Times the Mass of the Sun): For stars like our Sun, the red giant phase culminates in the ejection of their outer layers, leaving behind a dense, hot core. This exposed core becomes a white dwarf, a faint, Earth-sized remnant of the once-mighty star. The outer layers that were ejected form a planetary nebula, a stunning, short-lived spectacle of gas and dust expanding into space.

  2. Stars with High Mass (Greater than 8 Times the Mass of the Sun): Massive stars experience a vastly different fate. After the red giant phase, they continue fusing heavier elements until they reach iron. Since iron cannot undergo energy-generating fusion, the core collapses under gravity, potentially producing a violent supernova. What remains after such an event may be a neutron star or even a black hole, depending on the star’s original mass.

The Sun’s Future as a Red Giant

As we look at our own Sun, it’s humbling to consider that it, too, will undergo this transformation billions of years from now. The Sun’s brightness will increase during its red giant phase, potentially swallowing the inner planets, including Mercury, Venus, and possibly Earth. While life on Earth will no longer be possible due to extreme heat long before this stage, the Sun’s transformation offers a fascinating glimpse into the life cycle of stars and their role in shaping the universe.

The Cosmic Legacy of Red Giants

The red giant phase is more than just an endpoint in a star’s life. It represents a crucial link in the cycle of stellar evolution. During this phase, matter that was once part of the star is returned to the cosmos, becoming the building blocks of new stars, planets, and ultimately life itself. In a poetic sense, red giants are contributors to the recycling of the universe, dispersing heavy elements that can form into entirely new worlds.

In the study of stars, the red giant phase reveals the profound interconnectedness of celestial events. Even as a star sees its end, it provides the raw materials for cosmic beginnings elsewhere. The life and death of stars are vital to the fabric of the universe, and red giants play a central role in this grand process.

Understanding what happens when a star becomes a red giant not only embraces the immense beauty of the cosmos but also sparks reflection on our own place within it. The life cycles of stars, including the red giant phase, remind us that transformation—however disruptive—ushers in fresh opportunities for creation and evolution in the endless expanse of space.
