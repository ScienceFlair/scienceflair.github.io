The Philosophy of Morality: Ethical Relativism vs. Ethical Absolutism


In the realm of ethics and morality, two contrasting viewpoints have emerged: ethical relativism and ethical absolutism. These philosophical theories offer different perspectives on how we determine what is morally right or wrong. In this blog post, we will delve into the intricacies of these two approaches, exploring their fundamental principles, implications, and potential criticisms.

Ethical Relativism: Embracing Cultural Diversity

Ethical relativism posits that morality is subjective and varies across different cultures, societies, or individuals. According to this perspective, there are no universal moral truths or principles that apply to all people, at all times, and in all situations. Instead, ethical relativism suggests that moral judgments are relative to one’s cultural, societal, or personal beliefs and values.

Cultural Relativism

Cultural relativism is a specific branch of ethical relativism that argues that moral standards are culturally determined. It asserts that each culture has its own set of moral norms, and individuals should refrain from imposing their values on others. From a cultural relativist standpoint, what is considered morally right or wrong is contingent upon the cultural context.

For instance, practices like polygamy, which may be condemned in some cultures, are embraced in others. Cultural relativism emphasizes the importance of understanding diverse perspectives and appreciating the richness of different cultural practices without imposing a single moral framework.

Subjective Relativism

Another form of ethical relativism is subjective relativism. This perspective suggests that morality is determined by an individual’s personal beliefs and preferences. According to subjective relativism, what is morally right or wrong is based solely on an individual’s subjective opinion. In this view, there are no objective moral standards that transcend personal perspectives.

Ethical Absolutism: The Search for Universal Truths

In contrast to ethical relativism, ethical absolutism argues that there are objective and universal moral principles that apply to all individuals, regardless of cultural or personal differences. Ethical absolutists believe that certain actions are inherently right or wrong, regardless of the context in which they occur.

Divine Command Theory

One prominent form of ethical absolutism is the Divine Command Theory. According to this theory, moral principles are derived from a higher power, such as God or a divine entity. Moral obligations are seen as inherent in the commands given by this higher authority. For adherents of the Divine Command Theory, morality is grounded in religious beliefs and teachings.

Natural Law Theory

Another form of ethical absolutism is the Natural Law Theory. This theory posits that moral principles are based on an objective understanding of human nature and the natural order of the world. Proponents of the Natural Law Theory argue that certain actions are inherently good or bad based on their conformity to or violation of this natural order.

Criticisms and Challenges

Both ethical relativism and ethical absolutism have faced criticisms and challenges from various perspectives. Ethical relativism, for example, has been criticized for potentially justifying morally reprehensible actions under the guise of cultural tolerance. Critics argue that this approach undermines the possibility of moral progress and universal human rights.

On the other hand, ethical absolutism has been challenged for its potential rigidity and lack of flexibility in accommodating cultural diversity. Critics argue that this approach fails to acknowledge the complexities of moral decision-making and oversimplifies the dynamic nature of ethics.

Conclusion

The debate between ethical relativism and ethical absolutism continues to shape our understanding of morality and guide our ethical decision-making. While ethical relativism emphasizes cultural diversity and subjective perspectives, ethical absolutism seeks to establish universal moral principles. Both approaches have their strengths and weaknesses and offer valuable insights into the complexities of morality. As individuals, we can engage in this ongoing dialogue, striving to develop a nuanced understanding of ethics and our responsibilities towards one another.
