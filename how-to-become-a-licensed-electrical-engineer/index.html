How to Become a Licensed Electrical Engineer


Becoming a licensed electrical engineer is a rewarding career choice that combines technical expertise, problem-solving skills, and a deep understanding of electrical systems. Whether you’re drawn to the field by a passion for technology, a desire to work on cutting-edge projects, or the prospect of a stable and lucrative career, the path to becoming a licensed electrical engineer involves several crucial steps. This guide will walk you through the process, from education and examinations to gaining experience and obtaining licensure. Before delving into the steps to become a licensed electrical engineer, it’s essential to understand what the role entails. Electrical engineers design, develop, test, and supervise the manufacturing of electrical equipment, such as electric motors, radar and navigation systems, communications systems, and power generation equipment. They also work on projects related to lighting and wiring in buildings, automobiles, and aircraft.

Electrical engineers often work in teams with other engineers and scientists. They may also be involved in project management, which requires leadership skills and the ability to communicate complex technical information effectively.

Step 1: Obtain a Bachelor’s Degree in Electrical Engineering

The first step toward becoming a licensed electrical engineer is to obtain a Bachelor’s degree in Electrical Engineering (BSEE) or a related field. This degree provides the foundational knowledge needed to understand electrical systems and the principles that govern them.

Choosing an Accredited Program

When selecting a program, it’s crucial to choose one accredited by the Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology (ABET). ABET accreditation ensures that the program meets the quality standards that prepare graduates to enter the critical fields of STEM education. Graduating from an ABET-accredited program is often a requirement for licensure.

Coursework and Practical Experience

Electrical engineering programs typically include coursework in mathematics, physics, and computer science, as well as specialized courses in circuit analysis, electromagnetics, control systems, and power systems. Most programs also incorporate hands-on laboratory work and project-based learning, which are essential for developing practical skills.

Internship Opportunities

While completing your degree, seek out internship opportunities. Internships provide valuable real-world experience and can enhance your resume. They also offer a chance to network with professionals in the field, which can be beneficial when seeking employment after graduation.

Step 2: Pass the Fundamentals of Engineering (FE) Exam

After earning your bachelor’s degree, the next step is to pass the Fundamentals of Engineering (FE) exam. The FE exam, also known as the Engineer-in-Training (EIT) exam, is the first of two exams required for licensure.

Preparing for the FE Exam

The FE exam covers a broad range of subjects, including mathematics, engineering sciences, and specific knowledge areas within electrical engineering. It’s a good idea to start preparing early, using resources such as review courses, study guides, and practice exams. Familiarize yourself with the exam format and focus on areas where you feel less confident.

Taking the Exam

The FE exam is computer-based and administered year-round at approved Pearson VUE test centers. Passing this exam demonstrates that you have acquired the fundamental knowledge needed for the engineering profession and qualifies you to work under the supervision of a licensed professional engineer (PE).

Step 3: Gain Relevant Work Experience

After passing the FE exam, you will need to gain relevant work experience. Most states require at least four years of work experience under the supervision of a licensed PE before you can take the Principles and Practice of Engineering (PE) exam.

Finding Employment

Seek employment in positions where you can gain experience in electrical engineering. Entry-level positions such as electrical engineer, project engineer, or systems engineer can provide the necessary experience. Look for opportunities that offer a variety of projects and challenges to broaden your skills.

Documenting Your Experience

Keep detailed records of your work experience, including the types of projects you worked on, your specific responsibilities, and the supervision you received. Many licensing boards require detailed documentation of your experience, including references from licensed PEs who can attest to your work.

Step 4: Pass the Principles and Practice of Engineering (PE) Exam

Once you have gained the required work experience, you can apply to take the Principles and Practice of Engineering (PE) exam. This exam tests your competency in your specific engineering discipline and is the final step to becoming a licensed professional engineer.

Preparing for the PE Exam

The PE exam is more specialized than the FE exam and focuses on the knowledge and skills relevant to electrical engineering. Review the exam specifications and study materials provided by the National Council of Examiners for Engineering and Surveying (NCEES). Consider enrolling in a review course and practicing with sample questions and practice exams.

Taking the Exam

The PE exam is administered twice a year and is offered in various disciplines, including Electrical and Computer Engineering. It’s an eight-hour exam with multiple-choice questions and requires a deep understanding of both theoretical and practical aspects of electrical engineering.

Step 5: Obtain Licensure

After passing the PE exam, you can apply for licensure in your state. Licensing requirements vary by state, but generally include the following:

Submitting an Application

Submit an application to the state licensing board, including proof of education, FE and PE exam results, and documentation of your work experience. You may also need to provide references from licensed PEs.

Meeting Additional Requirements

Some states have additional requirements, such as ethics exams or continuing education. Be sure to check the specific requirements for the state where you plan to practice.

Receiving Your License

Once your application is approved, you will receive your license and can legally use the title of Professional Engineer (PE). This licensure signifies that you have met the standards of education, experience, and examination required for the profession and are qualified to practice independently.

Continuing Education and Professional Development

Obtaining your PE license is a significant achievement, but the learning doesn’t stop there. Many states require licensed engineers to complete continuing education (CE) to maintain their license. CE ensures that engineers stay current with technological advancements, industry standards, and best practices.

Continuing Education Requirements

Requirements for continuing education vary by state but typically include a certain number of professional development hours (PDHs) or continuing education units (CEUs) over a specific period. These can be obtained through various activities such as attending workshops, conferences, webinars, and courses.

Professional Organizations

Joining professional organizations such as the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) can provide valuable resources for continuing education and professional development. These organizations offer networking opportunities, technical publications, and conferences that can help you stay current in your field.

Advancing Your Career

With a PE license, you have the credentials to advance your career in many directions. Whether you choose to specialize in a particular area of electrical engineering, take on leadership roles, or start your own engineering firm, the opportunities are vast.

Specializations

Electrical engineering is a broad field with many areas of specialization, including power systems, telecommunications, electronics, control systems, and computer engineering. Specializing in a particular area can enhance your expertise and make you more competitive in the job market.

Leadership Roles

With experience and additional training, you can move into leadership roles such as project manager, engineering manager, or director of engineering. These roles involve overseeing projects, managing teams, and ensuring that engineering work meets quality and safety standards.

Starting Your Own Firm

Some licensed engineers choose to start their own engineering firms. This path requires not only technical expertise but also business acumen. If you’re entrepreneurial and enjoy the challenges of running a business, this can be a highly rewarding career choice.

Conclusion

Becoming a licensed electrical engineer is a multi-step process that requires dedication, education, and experience. By following the steps outlined in this guide—obtaining a relevant degree, passing the FE and PE exams, gaining work experience, and meeting licensure requirements—you can achieve this prestigious credential and open up a world of career opportunities. Remember to stay committed to lifelong learning and professional development to continue growing in this dynamic and ever-evolving field.
