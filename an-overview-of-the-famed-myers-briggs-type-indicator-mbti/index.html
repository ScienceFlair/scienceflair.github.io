An Overview of the famed Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI)


The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) is a widely popular personality assessment tool that has been utilized for over 50 years to understand individual preferences and behaviors. Developed by Isabel Briggs Myers and her mother, Katharine Cook Briggs, the MBTI is based on the Swiss psychologist Carl G. Jung’s theory of psychological types. The assessment aims to identify an individual’s personality type, which then helps them understand themselves better and interact more effectively with others.

How the MBTI Works

The MBTI is a self-assessment questionnaire consisting of 64 questions, each with a set of two preferences. The individual responds according to their natural preferences, and the resulting scores categorize them into one of 16 distinct personality types. These types are determined by four dichotomies, each with two possible preferences:

  1. Extroversion (E) / Introversion (I): This scale measures the preference for focusing on the outer world or the inner world. Extroverts (E) enjoy social interaction and are energized by external stimuli, while introverts (I) are more introspective and find peace in quiet environments.
  2. Intuition (N) / Sensing (S): This dichotomy measures the preference for processing information through either intuition or observation. Intuitives (N) focus on possibilities and potential outcomes, while sensors (S) concentrate on facts and immediate experiences.
  3. Feeling (F) / Thinking (T): This dimension assesses the preference for making decisions based on emotions (F) or logic (T).
  4. Perceiving (P) / Judging (J): Lastly, this scale measures the choice between being organized and structured (J) or more adaptable and spontaneous (P). Judgers (J) prefer to make plans and follow schedules, whereas perceivers (P) opt for flexibility and improvisation.

The combination of these dichotomies generates 16 distinct personality types, all with their own unique characteristics and strengths. Although no type is inherently better than another, each personality type offers particular traits and general tendencies.

The 16 MBTI Types

Here is a brief overview of the 16 different types, including their primary traits and strengths:

  1. ISTJ (The Analyst): Responsible, reliable, and objective, ISTJs prefer a structured and organized environment with minimal upheaval or change.
  2. ISFJ (The Nurturer): Compassionate and patient, ISFJs prioritize the well-being of others and believe in working diligently to ensure the safety and comfort of those they care for.
  3. INFJ (The Counselor): Insightful and empathetic, INFJs are intuitive deep-thinkers who often find themselves drawn to creative and philosophical topics.
  4. INTJ (The Architect): Independent, logical, and strategic, INTJs prefer to unravel complex theories and concepts in a cerebral setting.
  5. ISTP (The Voyager): Adventure-seeking and objective, ISTPs take a hands-on approach to exploring the world around them.
  6. ISFP (The Artist): Creative and spontaneous, ISFPs prefer to let their feelings and intuition guide them through life.
  7. INFP (The Idealist): Passionate and imaginative, INFPs possess a strong inner world filled with dreams and ideals.
  8. INTP (The Thinker): Analytical and independent, INTPs excel at logical thinking and enjoy testing and exploring theories and ideas.
  9. ESTJ (The Director): Decisive and organized, ESTJs make excellent leaders and administrators, utilizing their pragmatic approach to achieve concrete results.
  10. ESFJ (The Caregiver): Warm and concerned, ESFJs have a natural talent for understanding others and ensuring their well-being.
  11. ENFJ (The Mentor): Charismatic and evidence-based, ENFJs possess a natural flair for inspiring and guiding others.
  12. ENTJ (The Commander): Tenacious and visionary, ENTJs excel at setting goals and using their strategic skills to turn their dreams into reality.
  13. ESTP (The Doer): Impulsive and adaptable, ESTPs thrive in dynamic environments where they can seize opportunities and engage with others.
  14. ESFP (The Performer): Outgoing and spontaneous, ESFPs excel in social situations and find fulfillment through engaging with others and sharing their experiences.
  15. ENFP (The Enthusiast): Enthusiastic and creative, ENFPs are natural connectors, drawing energy from the people and the world around them.
  16. ENTP (The Visionary): Dynamic and innovative, ENTPs are idea-driven and skilled at generating new strategies and solutions.

Understanding one’s MBTI type can be a transformative experience, as it reveals how to embrace and harness the strengths of their personality while learning to overcome potential weaknesses. The MBTI has helped individuals navigate relationships, pursue careers, and achieve personal growth by lighting the path to self-discovery and self-improvement.
