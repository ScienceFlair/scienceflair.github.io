Insects as Forensic Indicators: From Carrion to Crime Scenes


Insects are often overlooked in our daily lives; however, they can play an essential role in helping forensic experts solve crimes. Insects can reveal vital information about the time of death, environmental conditions, and even provide a physical link to the perpetrator. This blog post will explore how insects can serve as forensic indicators in various crime scenes. We will discuss the role of insects in revealing details about the death of a body and how forensic entomologists use their findings in legal proceedings.

Insect Indicators Found at Carrion Scenes

One of the crime scenes where insects are useful forensics indicators is at carrion scenes. Carrion refers to the dead body of an animal that decomposes, and it usually attracts insects, particularly flies. Forensic entomologists study the insects found at a carrion scene because they can determine the time of death by assessing the life stages of the insects present.

Flies are the most common insects found at carrion scenes. They can be categorized into two primary groups: blowflies and flesh flies. Blowflies are the most commonly found insects at a crime scene since they are attracted to the scent of the decomposing body and lay eggs quickly. Flesh flies, on the other hand, are attracted to the body due to their dead and live maggots seeking to feed on the decomposing matter. Other insects like beetles and bees may also attend carrion scenes, making it important to identify all species present.

Determining the Time of Death with Insect Life Stages

Using insect life stages can help forensic entomologists determine the time of death. Each insect goes through different stages in its life cycle, four primary stages for complete metamorphosis insects like flies. These stages consist of egg, larva (maggot), pupa, and adult. The three most crucial life stages for determining the time of death are the egg, third instar larva, and pupa.

Eggs are critical as they represent the beginning of an insect’s presence at a crime scene. By understanding the number of eggs laid at a specific time, entomologists can estimate when the blowflies initially arrived at the scene. Maggots in their third instar stage of development are essential because most insects would exhibit their third instar stage about ten days after oviposition (egg-laying). Finally, pupal cases shed light on the insects’ transition into adulthood, providing an idea of how long it took the insects to develop during this phase.

Insects in Non-Carrion Crime Scenes

Insects are not only valuable in carrion-scented crime scenes; they also leave tiny footprints on non-carrion crime scenes. The presence of insects in these types of crime scenes may suggest that a victim had been dead or decomposing elsewhere before the scene occurred. For instance, bed bugs can be found in crime scenes where the victim has been assaulting the victims, bloodsucking insects like bed bugs can be found in the vicinities leading to committing the crime. Racoon flies might also infest crime scenes where an assault has taken place indoor.

Blood-sucking insects could also be indicative of the nature of an assault or a sexual assault. The remaining blood or skin cells left by insects can be analyzed for DNA, leading to matching the culprit. For examining insects on crimes that are committed indoors, locating their pathways can determine the sequence of crime events. Dust analysis may also aid to understand circumstance and portray the scale of disarray and intruder activities.

Furthermore, insects can play an essential role in uncovering deceptively hidden crimes. For example, insect evidence can sometimes reveal that a crime was staged, therefore disproving an alibi, or show signs of tampering, allowing investigators to reconstruct events more accurately. In the agricultural and environmental investigation cases, forensic entomologists can specify effects of pesticides on insects present in the area, allowing better understanding of the history of the site to assess maximum possible exposure of a victim to pesticides.

Insect Evidence and the Law

Insect evidence plays a significant role in forensic investigations and trials, but it also has its challenges. Legal professionals often view insect evidence with skepticism due to their perception that entomology is a lesser-known and untested field. This misunderstanding often led to a dismissal of valuable insect evidence that will otherwise help in arriving accurate conclusions.

In conclusion, insects can reveal much about crimes that occurred nearby or on the body itself. Their presence and life stages not only tell us about the time of death but also about the possible presence of other offenders. With constant improvements in the field of forensic entomology, insects’ role in forensic investigations continue to grow significantly. Seeking a clearer understanding and appreciation of the vital position of insects, becomes more critical in the ongoing story of unravelling the secret clues in crimes scenes that once would have gone unnoticed.
