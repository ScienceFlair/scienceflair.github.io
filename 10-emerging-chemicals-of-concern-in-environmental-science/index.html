10 Emerging Chemicals of Concern in Environmental Science


In recent years, significant advancements in analytical chemistry and environmental science have shed light on a diverse range of contaminants that are increasingly posing threats to ecosystems and human health. These so-called “emerging chemicals of concern” represent a growing field of scientific inquiry as researchers work to understand their occurrence, behavior, and impacts. From pharmaceutical residues to industrial byproducts, these substances often slip through conventional regulatory frameworks or treatment processes, creating potential risks that demand attention. Below, we explore ten of the most pressing emerging chemicals of concern and their implications for environmental science.

1. Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances (PFAS)

Known colloquially as “forever chemicals,” PFAS are synthetic compounds used in a wide variety of products, including non-stick cookware, firefighting foams, and water-repellent fabrics. Their persistent nature means they resist degradation in the environment, often accumulating in water supplies and living organisms. Research suggests associations between PFAS exposure and adverse health effects, such as cancer, thyroid dysfunction, and immune system suppression. Due to their widespread use and resistance to conventional treatment methods, PFAS have become a critical focus of environmental monitoring and remediation initiatives.

2. Microplastics and Nanoplastics

While plastic pollution has been a longstanding environmental concern, the issue of smaller plastic fragments—microplastics and nanoplastics—has come into sharper focus. These tiny particles, often less than 5 millimeters in size, form through the degradation of larger plastic items or are intentionally added to products like cosmetics. They are pervasive in marine environments, freshwater systems, and even the air. Recent studies show that microplastics can enter the food chain and potentially disrupt aquatic ecosystems and human health. Emerging research highlights their potential to act as vectors for toxic chemicals and pathogens, compounding their impact.

3. Pharmaceutical Residues

Pharmaceutical compounds, such as antibiotics, antidepressants, and painkillers, are an increasingly worrisome category of pollutants. These substances often enter the environment through domestic sewage, agricultural runoff, and improper disposal. Wastewater treatment plants are not always effective at removing pharmaceuticals, allowing these chemicals to accumulate in rivers, lakes, and groundwater. They pose risks to aquatic organisms, with effects ranging from behavioral changes to hormone disruption. Additionally, antibiotics in the water may contribute to the global issue of antimicrobial resistance, which presents a grave challenge to public health.

4. Neonicotinoid Insecticides

Introduced as a safer alternative to traditional pesticides, neonicotinoids have gained popularity in global agriculture. However, their environmental persistence and potential toxicity to non-target species, particularly pollinators such as bees, have raised alarms. Neonicotinoids can contaminate soil and water through agricultural runoff, impacting ecosystems far beyond the intended application areas. Their role in the decline of bee populations has spurred regulatory actions in some regions, but their widespread use remains a critical issue.

5. Brominated Flame Retardants (BFRs)

Used extensively in furniture, electronics, and building materials to prevent fires, brominated flame retardants (BFRs) are another class of emerging contaminants. These chemicals can leach into the environment during production, use, or disposal. Due to their persistence and fat-solubility, they often accumulate in living organisms, including humans. Emerging research links BFR exposure to neurological effects, endocrine disruption, and developmental issues, leading some countries to impose restrictions. However, many variants of these chemicals are still widely used and pose ongoing challenges for environmental management.

6. Bisphenols and Phthalates

Bisphenol A (BPA) and its chemical cousins, along with phthalates, are commonly found in plastics and personal care products. These substances are considered endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) because they can interfere with hormone systems. Even low-level exposure has been associated with adverse health effects such as reproductive abnormalities and increased risks of metabolic disorders. Despite widespread public awareness, alternative bisphenols (like BPS and BPF) and phthalates are increasingly being used as replacements, posing similar concerns and underscoring the need for continued vigilance.

7. Synthetic Hormones

Synthetic hormones, such as those used in oral contraceptives and hormone replacement therapies, represent a unique environmental challenge. These compounds often enter water systems via wastewater and resist conventional treatment processes. Even trace amounts can have profound effects on aquatic species, such as fish and amphibians. For example, they may feminize male fish populations or disrupt reproduction, leading to imbalances in aquatic ecosystems. As society continues to rely on synthetic hormones, their environmental impacts demand better management and treatment solutions.

8. Alkylphenols and Nonylphenols

Alkylphenols, particularly nonylphenols, are widely used in detergents, industrial processes, and personal care products. These chemicals are highly toxic to aquatic organisms and exhibit estrogenic activity, which can disrupt endocrine systems. Alkylphenols are resistant to degradation and often persist in the environment, particularly in aquatic sediments. Efforts to phase out these compounds in some countries have shown promise, but legacy contamination remains a concern for environmental scientists.

9. Halogenated Solvents

Halogenated solvents, such as trichloroethylene and perchloroethylene, are industrial chemicals used in degreasing, dry cleaning, and chemical manufacturing. While they have long been recognized as hazardous, recent attention has shifted to their persistence in groundwater and complex degradation pathways. Many of these solvents degrade into more toxic intermediates, such as vinyl chloride, which poses significant risks to human health. Understanding and mitigating the spread of halogenated solvents in the environment remains a critical challenge, particularly for groundwater management.

10. Ionic Liquids

As a relatively new class of synthetic chemicals, ionic liquids have been praised for their role in green chemistry due to their low volatility and wide range of applications. However, emerging evidence suggests these substances may not be as environmentally benign as once thought. Ionic liquids can be toxic to aquatic life and may persist in ecosystems, raising questions about their long-term impacts. With their increasing use in industrial and chemical processes, careful evaluation of their environmental footprint is urgently needed.

Addressing the Challenge

The emergence of these chemicals as environmental concerns highlights the dynamic nature of pollution science. As new products and technologies are developed, the potential for unintended consequences grows, creating a need for proactive and adaptive regulatory approaches. Advances in chemical detection and monitoring are essential for identifying and managing these substances before they cause irreversible harm.

Effective mitigation strategies also require collaboration among scientists, policymakers, industries, and communities. Efforts to improve wastewater treatment technologies, reduce reliance on hazardous chemicals, and promote sustainable alternatives are essential steps toward addressing the challenges posed by these emerging contaminants.

As public awareness of these issues grows, so too does the opportunity for meaningful action. Ultimately, addressing emerging chemicals of concern in environmental science is not simply about preserving ecosystems; it is about safeguarding public health, supporting biodiversity, and ensuring a sustainable future for all.
