How to Recognize and Treat Cushing's Disease in Pets


Cushing’s disease, also known as hyperadrenocorticism, is a common endocrine disorder that affects dogs more frequently than cats. It occurs when the body produces excess cortisol, a hormone vital for regulating metabolism, stress response, and immune function. While cortisol plays a crucial role in maintaining overall health, too much of it can lead to a variety of health issues. Understanding how to identify the signs and knowing the treatment options is essential for ensuring the well-being of pets diagnosed with this condition.

What is Cushing’s Disease in Pets?

Cushing’s disease occurs when the adrenal glands, located near the kidneys, produce too much cortisol. This can happen for several reasons, the most frequent being a problem in the pituitary gland, which controls hormone production. The pituitary gland may develop a benign tumor that causes it to secrete excess adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), stimulating the adrenal glands to overproduce cortisol. This form is called pituitary-dependent Cushing’s.

Less commonly, tumors in the adrenal glands themselves can cause excess cortisol release, termed adrenal-dependent Cushing’s. There is also an iatrogenic type caused by prolonged use of corticosteroid medications but we’ll focus mainly on naturally occurring disease here.

Excess cortisol has wide-ranging effects on the body—from altering metabolism and suppressing the immune system to affecting skin and muscle health.

Signs and Symptoms in Pets

Often, diagnosis is delayed because the signs of Cushing’s disease can be subtle and develop slowly over weeks or months. Pet owners may notice changes but attribute them to aging or other minor issues. Here are the common symptoms to watch for:

Increased Thirst and Urination

One of the earliest and most common signs is a marked increase in water consumption (polydipsia) and more frequent urination (polyuria). Because cortisol affects kidney function, pets drink more and urinate excessively.

Increased Appetite

Excess cortisol can stimulate appetite dramatically, causing pets to eat more than usual, sometimes leading to rapid weight gain.

Abdominal Enlargement

Also called a “pot-bellied” appearance, this results from fat redistribution and weakening of abdominal muscles.

Hair Loss and Skin Changes

Pets may develop thinning fur or areas of hair loss, known as alopecia. The skin might appear thin, fragile, and prone to bruising or infections because cortisol interferes with skin renewal and defense.

Lethargy and Muscle Weakness

Affected pets often exhibit decreased energy and muscle weakness due to muscle protein breakdown and metabolic alterations.

Panting

Dogs with Cushing’s disease may pant excessively, which is related to cortisol’s impact on respiratory drive and metabolism.

Recurrent Infections or Poor Wound Healing

The immune suppressive effects of cortisol result in increased susceptibility to infections and delayed healing.

Behavioral Changes

Some pets may become more irritable or anxious, although this is less specific.

Diagnosing Cushing’s Disease

If Cushing’s is suspected based on the clinical signs and history, a veterinarian will undertake a combination of physical examination, blood tests, and specific endocrine function tests.

Physical Exam

The vet will look for physical clues such as symmetrical hair loss, skin changes, abdominal enlargement, and evidence of muscle weakness.

Blood and Urine Tests

Routine blood tests can reveal elevated liver enzymes, high alkaline phosphatase, increased cholesterol, and changes in blood glucose levels. A urinalysis might show dilute urine and presence of infections.

Confirmatory Endocrine Testing

The diagnosis hinges on specialized tests like:

  • Low-Dose Dexamethasone Suppression Test (LDDS): This assesses the feedback control of cortisol production. Normally, dexamethasone suppresses cortisol release, but in Cushing’s disease, suppression fails.

  • ACTH Stimulation Test: Measures adrenal response to ACTH injection. Elevated cortisol after ACTH administration supports the diagnosis.

  • Urine Cortisol-to-Creatinine Ratio: Used as a screening test but less definitive.

Once confirmed, other imaging techniques such as abdominal ultrasound or MRI may be employed to determine if adrenal or pituitary cause predominates.

Differential Diagnosis: What Else Could It Be?

Several conditions mimic Cushing’s disease symptoms, so ruling out other illnesses is critical. Conditions causing increased drinking and urination, such as diabetes mellitus or kidney disease, must be considered. Skin problems could be due to allergies or infections. Always ensure a thorough vet evaluation.

Treatment Options for Cushing’s Disease in Pets

While Cushing’s disease is a serious condition, with proper management, many pets live a good quality of life. Treatment depends largely on the cause.

Medical Management

For pituitary-dependent Cushing’s disease, the primary approach involves medication to control cortisol production:

  • Trilostane: The most commonly used drug, trilostane inhibits cortisol synthesis. Administered orally, it requires careful dose titration and monitoring due to potential side effects such as adrenal insufficiency.

  • Mitotane: Destroys part of the adrenal cortex. Less used nowadays due to toxicity concerns.

Medication management requires routine follow-ups with the vet for blood work and dose adjustment.

Surgical Intervention

If an adrenal tumor is diagnosed and causing excess cortisol, surgical removal of the problematic adrenal gland may be an option. Surgery can be curative but carries risks and is more invasive.

Radiation Therapy

For large pituitary tumors not amenable to surgery or with neurological signs, radiation treatment may help shrink the tumor and control hormone secretion. This modality is less commonly available.

Supportive Care

In addition to direct treatment, ensuring your pet maintains a healthy diet and appropriate exercise contributes to their overall well-being. Managing secondary infections or skin issues with antibiotics or topical treatments is also part of comprehensive care.

Monitoring and Prognosis

Cushing’s disease requires lifelong management and periodic re-evaluation. With medication, many pets respond well, showing improvement in symptoms such as drinking, urination, appetite, and energy levels.

However, untreated Cushing’s can cause complications including diabetes mellitus, high blood pressure, urinary tract infections, and blood clots. Early diagnosis and ongoing treatment drastically improve outcomes.

When to See a Vet

If you notice increased thirst, urination, panting, hair loss, or abdominal swelling in your dog, consult your veterinarian promptly. Early veterinary involvement can prevent severe complications.

Summary

Cushing’s disease in pets is a complex hormonal disorder that manifests with a range of systemic symptoms. Recognizing signs like increased drinking and urination, appetite changes, hair loss, and abdominal enlargement are critical steps toward diagnosis. With modern diagnostic tools and treatments such as trilostane, many dogs can maintain a comfortable and active life despite the disease. Close partnership with your veterinarian ensures optimal management and monitoring to catch any side effects early.

Awareness and education about this condition empower pet owners to act decisively, enhancing quality and longevity of their beloved companions.
