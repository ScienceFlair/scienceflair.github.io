Non-Equilibrium Plasmas: From Atmospheric Pressure to Low Pressure


Plasma is often referred to as the fourth state of matter, alongside solids, liquids, and gases. It is a highly ionized gas that consists of free electrons and positively charged ions. Non-equilibrium plasmas, in particular, have gained significant attention in recent years due to their unique properties and wide-ranging applications in various fields.

What are Non-Equilibrium Plasmas?

Non-equilibrium plasmas are characterized by their departure from thermal equilibrium, where the electron temperature is significantly higher than the gas temperature. This discrepancy arises due to the presence of energetic electrons that collide with neutral gas molecules, leading to ionization and excitation processes.

Atmospheric Pressure Plasmas

Atmospheric pressure plasmas (APPs) are non-equilibrium plasmas that operate at or near atmospheric pressure. These plasmas have gained immense popularity due to their potential for various applications, including surface modification, sterilization, and medical treatments.

One of the key advantages of APPs is their ability to operate at ambient conditions without the need for vacuum chambers. This makes them highly accessible and cost-effective compared to low-pressure plasmas. Moreover, the reactive species generated in APPs can interact directly with the surrounding environment, making them ideal for surface treatments.

Low-Pressure Plasmas

On the other hand, low-pressure plasmas operate at reduced gas pressures, typically in the range of millibars to a few pascals. These plasmas are commonly used in fields such as semiconductor manufacturing, plasma etching, and plasma deposition.

Low-pressure plasmas offer precise control over the plasma parameters, including gas composition, pressure, and power input. This level of control allows for tailored plasma chemistry and enables the deposition of thin films with specific properties. Additionally, the low gas pressure reduces the likelihood of unwanted reactions with the surrounding environment.

Transition between Atmospheric Pressure and Low Pressure

The transition from atmospheric pressure plasmas to low-pressure plasmas involves several challenges. One of the primary concerns is the transition from a collision-dominated regime to a regime where the mean free path of particles is much larger than the plasma dimensions.

To overcome this challenge, various techniques have been developed, such as the use of different gas flow configurations, magnetic fields, and plasma confinement strategies. These techniques aim to extend the plasma lifetime and maintain its stability during the transition process.

Applications of Non-Equilibrium Plasmas

Non-equilibrium plasmas find applications in a wide range of fields, including materials science, environmental remediation, energy, and healthcare. Some notable applications include:

  • Surface modification and functionalization of materials
  • Plasma-assisted chemical synthesis
  • Plasma medicine for wound healing and cancer treatment
  • Plasma-based pollution control and air purification
  • Plasma propulsion for space exploration

The unique properties of non-equilibrium plasmas make them versatile and attractive for innovative and sustainable technological advancements.

Conclusion

Non-equilibrium plasmas, from atmospheric pressure to low pressure, offer immense potential for various applications. Their ability to operate under different conditions and interact with the surrounding environment makes them highly versatile. As researchers continue to explore and understand the intricacies of non-equilibrium plasmas, we can expect to witness further advancements and breakthroughs in this fascinating field.
