The Syntax of Questions in Different Languages


When studying languages, one of the most fascinating aspects is the syntax of questions. The way people ask questions varies greatly from language to language, often reflecting a unique combination of phonological, syntactic, and pragmatic factors. In this blog post, we will take a journey through the land of linguistics to explore how different languages form questions.

Firstly, let’s examine some common question-forming techniques used in English. As in other Germanic languages, English uses word order to distinguish between statements and questions. With just one exception, which we will address later, questions in English always begin with an auxiliary verb (e.g., is, are, does, etc.), followed by the subject and finally the main verb. This structure typically takes the form of an Auxiliary-Subject-Verb (ASV) pattern. For example, “Do you like apples?” follows this structure: do-you-like-apples. Swapping the position of the auxiliary and the subject would render a statement instead: “You like apples.”

However, things become more intriguing when we compare English to languages like Spanish or French. In these Romance languages, auxiliary verbs are considerably less necessary for forming questions. Instead, the syntax employs different question words and intonation to signal a question.

Take the popular Spanish question “¿Cómo estás?” (How are you?) as an example. In this sentence, an intonation rise emphasizes the interrogative adverb “cómo” (how), which corresponds to the English auxiliary in function, albeit not form. Another way to craft a question in Spanish, albeit more formally, is by placing the verb está (are) between the subject “tú” and the question word: “¿Tú estás cómo?” (“You are how?”)

In the case of French, the use of question words distinguishes the question from a statement. Consider “Comment vas-tu?” (How are you?), which employs the question word “comment” for one type of question and the pronoun “tu” for another type. The trick here is knowing which question word to use. In French, “Information Questions” like “What time is the movie?” are formed with “quelle heure,” while “Yes/No Questions” such as “Is he coming?” are formed with “ça” as in “Est-ce qu’il vient?”

In further contrasts, some languages may reverse word order or omit certain elements altogether to create questions. For instance, in Japanese or Korean, the word order mirrors that of a statement but relies on a particular pitch rise called a “question intonation” to flag a question. In Japanese, “Ano hito wa elektronikaru o shiru ka?” (Do you know electronics?) demonstrates this phenomenon. The sentence maintains the Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) structure, but using a rising intonation transforms it into a question.

Lastly, some languages even dispense with word order as a primary determinant of sentence type. In these languages, a canonical question structure may not exist, and other syntactic rules and elements have to be considered for identifying questions. For example, in Mandarin Chinese, the structure of a question can appear more statement-like compared to English. Consider this Mandarin example: “[你] [吃] [饭]?” (nǐ chī fàn) (You eat rice?). This sentence looks like a statement, but the use of brackets highlights the separate, equally important components of the question.

In conclusion, the way languages form questions provides a fascinating look into their unique rule systems and linguistic intricacies. From English’s reliance on auxiliary verbs to Spanish and French’s use of question words, and from the pitch intonation in Japanese and Korean to the more flexible questions in Mandarin, the world of questions is as diverse as the languages themselves. While some languages may seem challenging to learn or understand, getting a grip on their respective question-forming mechanisms is an essential step in mastering the language and appreciating its beauty.
