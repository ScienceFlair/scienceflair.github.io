The Write Myco: Fungi as Inspiring Figures in Literature & Writing


Fungi have been present since the dawn of time; silently nurturing our planet, intertwining with life, and shaping art in countless forms. In the world of literature and creative writing, fungi have often been portrayed as intriguing and alluring phenomena, capturing the imagination of countless authors over centuries. Intrigued by the cultivation of fungi within the world of literature and creative myco-diaries, we decide to take a careful look at how this fascinating organism has inspired writers to craft some of the most captivating stories and characters we have come to cherish.

Tapping into the Realm of Magic

For centuries, fungi have been perceived as mystical organisms with medicinal and spiritual properties. Their bizarre and distinct forms have prompted writers to explore the realms of magic and fantasy, invoking an aura of mystery and awe. From ambling through mushroom-capped forests with every step feeling like it was impregnated with folklore to consuming hallucinogenic Amanita muscaria and experiencing a psilocybin-induced voyage of thoughts, fungi have always had a magnetic relationship with the human imagination.

Take J.R.R. Tolkien’s mystical Middle-Earth, for instance, where fungi have been converted into enchanted and ethereal beings. The Ents, the walking, talking trees of Tolkien’s world, have all the features of a giant fungal network known as mycelium. These characters embody the slow-growing, resilient, and deep-rooted nature of fungi, presenting a powerful statement of environmental stewardship. The Ents provide Tolkien’s legendarium with another layer of world-building that emphasizes the importance of respecting the earth, balance, and the interconnectedness of life.

Literary Fungal Landscapes

The British Romantic poets like Wordsworth and Coleridge were entranced with mushroom-infused fantasies. Samuel Taylor Coleridge’s “Kubla Khan” is rich in images of fungal paradise, with a “Cavernous vast and fetid” resembling a network of underground mycelium. The famous line – “And from this chasm, with a piacular dance / The Dervish visage, spreading, whirled and sailed / A dusky vigilant Snake” – represents the unseen fungal realm that thrives beneath our feet.

More recently, science fiction authors have tapped into the imaginative realm of fungi in different ways. In Robert A. Heinlein’s Stranger in a Strange Land, the very foundation of the society is built upon the mythical mushroom: eden drug, Sandoz. Similarly, the beautifully crafted “Mykel-Advancing” series by American author Andrew Beery showcases a far-reaching and complex future of human-fungal symbiosis.

Roots of Human-Fungal Relationships

Fungi have always played a vital role in human culture, with culinary delights like the humble mushroom to religious and spiritual connections stemming from ancient shamanic practices. Their portrayal in literature reveals the potential of fungi as catalysts for human development, prompting us to rethink our relationship with this crucial component of the earth’s ecosystem.

Understanding the fungi-infused literary heritage allows us to glimpse the underappreciated facets of these organisms and open a door to a world beyond just culinary and medicinal uses. These inspiring figures from literature undeniably represent the awe-inspiring and enduring nature of fungi. As our connection to the natural world weakens in our increasingly digitized and simplified world, it may very well be the fungi-infused artistry and mystique that reminds us to reconsider the composition of our existence – the combination of our “roots” and our mental landscape, shaped by the ineffable yet potent forces of imagination and dreams.

To embrace the power and beauty of fungi, authors have metaphorically unearthed the microscopic avenues where life buds from decay. They have embraced the myco-world and elevated it to a level of supernatural majesty, inviting us to walk through this giant, fantastical universe alongside them. And in these literary journeys, a new appreciation for fungi as sources of inspiration, nourishment, and the catalyst for understanding the deep-rooted connections that bind us all is uncovered.
