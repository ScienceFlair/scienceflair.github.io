Fly Species and Their Succession: A Window to the Time Since Death


Imagine you stumbled upon a dead body in the woods. As you walk away from the scene, thoughts start to stir in your mind. One of the most intriguing questions could be, “How long ago did this person pass away?” In a fortunate turn of events, forensic entomologists have the perfect tool to assist them in answering precisely that question: fly species and their succession.

Since its inception, the study of insect colonization on decomposing remains has significantly contributed to the field of forensic science. The dynamic world of entomology assists in estimating the time since death, known as the post-mortem interval (PMI). This assistance results from the unique life cycles and behaviors of various fly species found on a corpse. This blog post will guide you through this fascinating journey, exploring the world of flies, their succession, and what they reveal about the time since death.

At first instance, it may seem counterintuitive to use fly species for determining a deceased person’s time frame. However, the extraordinary sequence of life stages these insects experience upon encountering a decomposing body provides unique insights into the realm of forensic science. Forensic entomologists focus mainly on the activities and development stages of blowflies, as they are the first insects attracted to a corpse. The life cycle of these insects, composed of egg, larva, pupa, and adult stages, offers a reliable frame of reference for determining PMI.

To delve into the fascinating world of fly species succession, let’s start by exploring the different stages of the life cycle:

  1. Egg: The first step in the life cycle of blowflies is laying eggs. Upon discovering a corpse, the adult blowflies examine the body’s condition and assess the level of decay. If they find the conditions satisfying, female flies will lay their eggs on the decomposing tissue. The number of eggs laid can be a vital piece of information to forensic experts.

  2. Larva: Following the egg stage, tiny maggots emerge. These larvae feed voraciously on the corpse’s decomposing tissue. Maggot growth rate is consistent and heavily reliant on temperature. This information, combined with the larva’s size, aids in estimating the time since death. Several factors influence maggot development, including species, environmental conditions, and the time the larvae had to feed.

  3. Pupa: After consuming their fill, maggots enter the pupal stage. The pupa is encased in a protective cocoon, during which they undergo metamorphosis. Determining the presence of pupae and their extent of development can provide forensic entomologists with a more accurate estimation of the time since death.

  4. Adult: The final stage of the blowfly life cycle is the adult fly. These insects eventually leave the corpse to continue their life cycle. The emergence of adult flies can help set a lower limit estimate of the time since death.

Succession occurs when different fly species arrive at the corpse based on their preferred environment and type of decomposition. The initial stage is called the larval colonization period. During this time, only blowflies predominate. Over a few days, other fly species like houseflies and flesh-flies arrive. These later arrivals contribute to the ecological succession process since their feeding and egg-laying habits influence the decomposition stages ongoing on the corpse.

As time passes by, the corpse goes through various decomposition stages. These stages are categorized as fresh, bloat, active decay, postmortem livor, and dry or skeletal stage. In correlation to these stages, forensic entomologists can identify and analyze data from fly life stages on a corpse, ultimately helping to determine the time since death.

Forensic experts apply their findings of fly species succession from the corpse scene by using the Maggot Rearing and Analysis for Time Range Estimations (MRAT) method. The entomologist collects a series of samples and examines the life stages from eggs, larvae, and pupae. By taking these samples from a corpse and implementing MRAT, forensic experts can create a numerical graph delineating the insect life cycle. This data becomes a significant factor in calculating the elusive time since death.

In a typical entomological investigation, the scope of the research consists of the abovementioned stages. Collecting evidence in the form of maggots, pupae, and adult flies, along with environmental factors such as temperature and humidity, play a crucial role in estimating the PMI. Considering all this data collectively, forensic scientists integrate this information using mathematical models to reconstruct the life cycle of blowflies.

To make this process more effective and efficient, they employ technological advances such as the Computerized Insect Life Expectancy and Development System (CIL-EDS). This tool allows the calculation of the PMI within a greater precision, taking into account the variables influencing the successional stages of fly species in a decomposition scene.

In conclusion, fly species and their succession offer an incredible insight into the time since death. Forensic entomology plays a crucial role in the world of forensic sciences, helping solve and answer many questions based on PMI estimations. By understanding and studying the complicated life cycle of flies and their behaviors, we are able to transform such seemingly insignificant insects into powerful allies in criminal investigations. Nonetheless, future research and advancements in technology will improve and refine our understanding of these fascinating creatures and the insights they provide into the mysteries of the human timeline.
