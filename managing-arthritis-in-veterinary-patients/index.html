Managing Arthritis in Veterinary Patients


Living with arthritis can be a painful and trying experience for any creature, big or small. Although arthritis in humans is a common and often dreaded ailment, it’s not something that is limited to our species. Veterinary patients can experience the same discomfort as we do, and it’s the responsibility of their caregivers to help alleviate that suffering. Just as there are various types of arthritis affecting humans, the same holds true for our animal companions. The most common forms include osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, and juvenile idiopathic arthritis.

For pets like dogs and cats, it can sometimes be challenging to determine if your beloved companion is experiencing arthritis. Often, owners might mistake the symptoms as a natural part of aging, fur loss, or weight gain as signs of an underlying issue. However, the sooner it is identified, the sooner treatment can begin to improve your pet’s quality of life.

Identifying Arthritis in Our Pets

To start, we should keep an eye out for particular signs and symptoms that may indicate hidden arthritis. Common indicators of arthritis in pets include:

  1. Changes in walking, running, or playing: Your once spry and energetic dog or cat may begin to reduce activity or even limp when walking or running.
  2. Difficulty in grooming: Older animals may have difficulty keeping themselves clean and well-groomed, as arthritis may make it painful to reach certain parts of their body.
  3. Changes in sleep patterns: Pets with arthritis may sleep more or have irregular sleep schedules due to joint discomfort.
  4. Changes in appetite: Pain from arthritis can cause a decline in appetite for pets, leading to unintentional weight loss.
  5. Changes in mood or behavior: Anxiety, aggression, or depression - all are possible responses to living with chronic discomfort.

If you notice any signs of arthritis in your pet, consult your veterinarian. They can provide a thorough examination and provide the best guidance in determining whether your pet is suffering from arthritis.

Managing Arthritis in Veterinary Patients

Once your pet is diagnosed with arthritis, it’s crucial to create a comprehensive approach to managing the condition. This approach should typically include several strategies, such as:

  1. Weight Management: Ensuring your pet is a healthy weight can significantly decrease the amount of stress and pain being placed on their joints. By working with your veterinarian to create an effective weight loss plan, your pet can experience improved mobility and reduced discomfort.

  2. Physical Activity: While it can often be challenging for arthritic pets to exercise, a gentle and appropriate exercise regimen will improve their physical health. Swims, short walks on a leash, and gentle play can help strengthen their muscles, maintain flexibility, and alleviate stiffness.

  3. Pain Management: Similar to humans, pets may need medications to keep arthritis-related pain under control. Work closely with your veterinarian to determine the most appropriate treatment plan, which may involve NSAIDs, joint supplements, or even prescription pain medications.

  4. Nutritional Support: Some specialized pet foods are specifically designed to help alleviate joint pain by addressing conditions exacerbated by arthritis. Working with your veterinarian to select the most appropriate food for your pet can make a considerable difference.

  5. Assistive Devices: In some cases, architects like ramps, and orthopedic beds can make daily tasks and even getting into a favorite lounging spot more comfortable for your pet.

  6. Alternative Therapies: Acupuncture, massage, and physical rehabilitation have been proven to help manage pain and improve the overall wellbeing of pets with arthritis. Always consult with your veterinarian before initiating any alternative therapies.

  7. Veterinary Visits: Keeping regular appointments with your veterinarian will ensure your pet receives proper care and treatment throughout their life. Regular veterinary monitoring and screens will help your pet with arthritis stay as comfortable as possible.

Final Thoughts

Arthritis doesn’t have to mean the end to a happy, comfortable life for your animal companion. With a combination of proper nutrition, pain management, exercise, and, if necessary, alternative therapies, your pet can lead a healthy, happy life full of love and enjoyment. Remember, partnering with your veterinarian is the key to ensuring the best possible outcome for your arthritic pet.
