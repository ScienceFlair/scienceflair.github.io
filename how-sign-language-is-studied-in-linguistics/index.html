How Sign Language is Studied in Linguistics


Sign language has long been a subject of fascination for linguists, as it challenges traditional views of language structure. While spoken languages rely on vocal cords and auditory perception, sign languages use visual and gestural communication through the hands, face, and body. In recent decades, the study of sign language within the field of linguistics has grown exponentially, reshaping our understanding of what constitutes a language. This blog post explores how sign language is studied in linguistics, highlighting the methods, theories, and implications of sign language research in the context of linguistic science.

The Role of Linguistics in Sign Language Research

Linguistics is the scientific study of language, encompassing a range of subfields that focus on different aspects of language, such as syntax, phonology, morphology, semantics, and pragmatics. When it comes to sign language, linguists apply the same tools and methods to study it as they would with spoken languages. The primary goal is to analyze how sign languages function as full-fledged languages with their own complex grammar, syntax, and vocabulary. Sign language linguistics seeks to uncover the unique features that distinguish sign languages from spoken languages while identifying universal principles shared by all human languages.

The Unique Features of Sign Language

One of the key motivations for studying sign language is its unique properties. Sign languages do not rely on sound or speech in the conventional sense. Instead, they use a combination of hand shapes, movement, facial expressions, and body posture to convey meaning. These components of sign language can be analyzed just as spoken words, tones, or intonations are analyzed in traditional linguistic studies.

1. Phonology in Sign Language

Phonology traditionally refers to the study of sounds in spoken languages. In sign languages, however, the concept of “phonology” refers to the building blocks of signs. Sign linguists have identified that, similar to spoken languages, sign languages have a “phonetic” system composed of distinct features. These features include:

  • Handshape: The configuration of the hand when forming a sign.
  • Location: The place in the signing space where the sign is made, such as near the signer’s head or chest.
  • Movement: The direction, speed, and type of motion used to produce the sign.
  • Palm orientation: The direction in which the palm is facing.
  • Facial expression and non-manual signals: Facial expressions and body posture that accompany signs to provide additional grammatical or emotional context.

These phonological features combine in specific patterns to create distinct signs, just as phonemes combine in spoken languages to form words. Sign language phonology is studied to understand the phonological processes and how signs interact within the larger linguistic system.

2. Morphology and Syntax in Sign Language

Morphology deals with the structure of words and how they are formed. In spoken languages, this often involves the combination of roots, prefixes, suffixes, and inflections. Sign languages have their own system of morphological processes, which may include adding movement or changing handshapes to modify the meaning of a sign. For example, a sign for “house” can be modified to mean “apartment” by changing the movement or direction of the hands.

Syntax, on the other hand, refers to the arrangement of words or signs to create grammatical sentences. Just like spoken languages, sign languages have their own syntactic rules that govern word order, negation, questions, and other grammatical structures. For instance, American Sign Language (ASL) generally follows a topic-comment sentence structure, where the topic is established first, followed by the comment.

Linguists studying sign language morphology and syntax focus on understanding how these elements work together to form coherent communication in sign languages. The challenge lies in comparing how different sign languages handle similar grammatical structures and whether there are universal principles at play across all sign languages.

3. Semantics and Pragmatics of Sign Language

Semantics involves the study of meaning, while pragmatics explores how language is used in social contexts. Sign language, like spoken languages, has an intricate system of meaning at the word, phrase, and sentence levels. Each sign carries its own meaning, and this meaning can be influenced by factors such as context, facial expressions, and body language.

For instance, the sign for “food” in ASL can change meaning depending on the facial expression used. If the face is smiling, it might indicate something tasty, while a frown could suggest that the food is unpleasant. This interplay between signs and non-manual signals (facial expressions, body movements) is a critical area of research for linguists studying sign language semantics and pragmatics.

Researchers also study how sign languages handle politeness, indirectness, and other social norms through linguistic choices. In certain social settings, signers might use specific sign language structures or tones that convey respect or deference, much like how spoken languages use tone of voice, formality, and politeness markers.

The Methods of Studying Sign Language

The study of sign language in linguistics involves a variety of methods, each designed to investigate different aspects of the language. These methods include fieldwork, corpus linguistics, experimental studies, and computational modeling. Below are some of the primary techniques used by linguists to study sign languages.

Fieldwork and Participant Observation

Fieldwork is a foundational method in linguistics, and it is equally important in sign language research. Linguists who study sign languages often conduct fieldwork by immersing themselves in deaf communities, observing how sign languages are used in everyday communication. This approach helps linguists collect naturalistic data and document how signs evolve in different social contexts.

Fieldwork also involves interviewing native sign language speakers, participating in conversations, and gathering a broad range of data on how sign languages function across various regions and communities. This data provides insight into dialectical variations, social influences on language use, and the ways in which sign languages adapt to new technological and cultural changes.

Corpus Linguistics

Corpus linguistics is the study of language through large collections of texts or recorded data. In the case of sign language, linguists use video recordings of sign language conversations, lectures, storytelling, and other types of communication. These recordings are transcribed and analyzed for patterns, usage, and grammatical structures.

Sign language corpora are increasingly being developed around the world, offering researchers valuable resources for understanding the linguistic features of different sign languages. These corpora can be used to identify regional variations, analyze language change, and study aspects of grammar, such as sentence structure and word order.

Experimental Studies

Experimental research plays an essential role in understanding how sign languages are processed cognitively. Linguists design controlled experiments to examine how sign language users produce and comprehend signs, compare processing speeds, and explore how different regions of the brain are activated when engaging in sign language.

One area of interest is the mental representation of signs and how they are stored in memory. Experimental methods can help linguists explore whether sign language processing relies on visual-spatial cognition or if there are shared cognitive mechanisms between sign language and spoken language.

Computational Modeling

With advances in technology, computational modeling has become an increasingly important tool in linguistics. In the study of sign languages, researchers use computational models to simulate and analyze the structure of sign languages, examining how different linguistic features interact. These models help linguists test hypotheses about sign language phonology, morphology, syntax, and other aspects of language structure.

Computational approaches can also assist in creating tools for automatic sign language recognition and translation, which could improve accessibility for deaf individuals in various contexts, such as healthcare, education, and online communication.

Theoretical Approaches to Sign Language

Several theoretical frameworks inform the study of sign languages in linguistics. These approaches offer different perspectives on the nature of sign languages and their relationship to spoken languages.

Universal Grammar Theory

Universal grammar is a theory proposed by Noam Chomsky, suggesting that all human languages share a common structural foundation. According to this theory, all languages, whether spoken or signed, are governed by a set of universal principles. This idea has been extended to sign languages, with researchers investigating how sign languages follow these universal principles.

While sign languages differ in form from spoken languages, they appear to adhere to similar grammatical structures. For example, both spoken and sign languages use syntax, morphology, and phonological rules to generate meaning. The concept of universal grammar allows linguists to explore the underlying commonalities between signed and spoken languages, supporting the idea that both forms are legitimate human languages.

Cognitive Linguistics

Cognitive linguistics focuses on the relationship between language and thought, emphasizing how language reflects cognitive processes. Researchers in this field study how the spatial nature of sign languages influences cognition and how sign language users mentally organize and conceptualize the world around them.

Cognitive linguistics has provided valuable insights into how sign languages represent concepts such as time, space, and motion. For instance, sign language speakers often use space around them to represent the past, present, and future, which challenges traditional theories of linguistic representation in spoken languages.

Sociolinguistics and Variation

Sociolinguistics examines the social aspects of language use, including how language varies according to factors such as region, social class, and identity. The study of variation in sign languages focuses on how different communities and social groups use sign languages differently. Researchers investigate dialects, regional variations, and the role of social identity in shaping language use.

For example, just as spoken languages have regional dialects, sign languages also exhibit variations across different geographic areas and cultural contexts. American Sign Language, for instance, has different dialects across the United States, which can reflect not only geographic but also social influences.

The Impact of Studying Sign Language

Studying sign language within the framework of linguistics has profound implications, both academically and socially. On an academic level, it challenges long-held assumptions about the nature of language and reinforces the idea that all forms of communication—whether spoken, written, or signed—are equal in their complexity and structure.

From a social perspective, sign language research contributes to greater recognition and respect for the deaf community and its culture. Understanding sign language as a legitimate and fully developed linguistic system helps to reduce misconceptions and stereotypes about deaf people. It also highlights the importance of sign language preservation, especially as many sign languages are endangered, with speakers often marginalized or underrepresented.

Furthermore, the study of sign languages can improve accessibility and inclusion. By understanding how sign languages work, linguists can contribute to the development of better communication tools, translation technologies, and educational resources that support deaf and hard-of-hearing individuals worldwide.

Conclusion

Sign language is a rich and complex linguistic system that provides valuable insights into the nature of human language. Linguists study sign languages to understand how they function as full languages, exploring their phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and pragmatics. The research methods used in sign language linguistics—fieldwork, corpus linguistics, experimental studies, and computational modeling—contribute to a deeper understanding of how sign languages operate both independently and in relation to spoken languages. By continuing to explore the nuances of sign language, linguists contribute to a more inclusive, accessible, and linguistically rich world.
