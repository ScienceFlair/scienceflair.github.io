How to Start Your Own Entomology Collection: A Beginner's Guide


Entomology, the study of insects, is a fascinating and rewarding field of science that offers endless opportunities for discovery. Insects are among the most diverse and abundant organisms on the planet, making them an ideal subject for a collection. Whether you’re interested in building a hobby collection or pursuing entomology more seriously, starting your own entomology collection can be an exciting and educational journey.

In this beginner’s guide, we will walk you through the steps to create and maintain your very own entomology collection. From selecting the right equipment to identifying and preserving specimens, this guide will help you navigate the essential aspects of starting your insect collection.

Why Start an Entomology Collection?

There are many reasons to begin an entomology collection. Not only can it be an enjoyable and rewarding hobby, but it can also be educational, offering insight into the natural world and the behavior of insects. If you’re studying entomology or are interested in a related field like ecology or biology, a personal collection can serve as a valuable resource for research.

Moreover, insect collections can have historical value, especially when the specimens are rare or have significant scientific interest. Collecting insects allows you to contribute to the understanding of biodiversity and conservation efforts. Plus, it’s a great way to connect with other entomologists and naturalists who share your passion.

Getting Started with Your Entomology Collection

Step 1: Understand the Basics of Insect Classification

Before you begin collecting insects, it is essential to familiarize yourself with the basics of insect classification. Insects are typically classified into various orders, families, and species. The most common orders that entomologists study include:

  • Coleoptera (beetles)
  • Lepidoptera (butterflies and moths)
  • Diptera (flies)
  • Hymenoptera (ants, bees, and wasps)
  • Orthoptera (grasshoppers, crickets, and locusts)

Understanding the differences between these orders and their specific characteristics will help you identify and organize your specimens. You can use field guides and online resources to learn about these groups and begin to recognize different insect species.

Step 2: Choose Your Collection’s Focus

You don’t have to collect every insect you encounter. Many successful collectors choose a specific focus for their collection, whether it’s a particular family, order, or geographic area. Here are some common focuses for insect collections:

  • Geographical focus: Collecting insects from a specific region, such as a local park, forest, or backyard.
  • Taxonomic focus: Specializing in a particular order or family, such as butterflies or beetles.
  • Ecological focus: Focusing on the role insects play in ecosystems, like pollinators or decomposers.

Choosing a focus will help you streamline your collection process, as it allows you to narrow your search to a particular group of insects. However, don’t hesitate to collect a diverse range of specimens as your skills and interests develop.

Step 3: Gather the Right Equipment

An entomology collection requires the proper tools and equipment for capturing, preserving, and organizing insects. Here’s a list of essential items you’ll need:

1. Insect Collecting Gear

To collect insects, you’ll need the right tools for capturing them. Some of the most common tools used in entomology are:

  • Insect nets: Lightweight, long-handled nets are used to capture flying insects such as butterflies and moths. There are specialized nets for different types of insects, so choose one that suits your needs.
  • Aspirators: Small devices used for catching small, delicate insects like ants, beetles, and flies. Aspirators are typically battery-operated and allow you to suck insects into a container without harming them.
  • Pitfall traps: These traps are set in the ground and are designed to capture ground-dwelling insects like beetles and ants.

2. Preserving and Mounting Materials

Once you’ve captured your insects, it’s time to preserve them for your collection. The preservation process is essential to maintaining the condition and integrity of your specimens.

  • Killing jars: A killing jar is used to euthanize insects quickly and humanely. It typically contains a piece of cotton soaked in ethyl acetate, which is a common insecticide.
  • Pinning supplies: Insects like butterflies and moths are often pinned to preserve their shape and allow for easy identification. You’ll need insect pins, pinning blocks, and soft foam boards to secure your specimens.
  • Alcohol: Some insects, especially those that are too small to pin, are best preserved in alcohol (usually rubbing alcohol or ethanol). This helps to prevent decomposition and preserves the insect’s features.

3. Field Guides and Identification Tools

Field guides, identification books, and smartphone apps can help you identify the species you encounter during your collecting trips. It’s also a good idea to take notes about the environment where you collected each specimen (location, date, and habitat). This information will be invaluable when organizing and identifying your collection.

Step 4: Go Insect Hunting

Once you have your equipment and a basic understanding of insect identification, it’s time to go hunting! When collecting insects, keep in mind that different species are active at different times of the day and in different environments. Some insects prefer sunny areas, while others may be found in shady spots or near water.

Consider going to areas with a high diversity of insects, such as parks, meadows, forests, and gardens. Also, try to visit different locations at various times of the day. Early morning and late evening are often prime times for capturing certain species, especially moths and nocturnal insects.

When collecting, always be mindful of local regulations and ethical considerations. Many regions have rules that govern the collection of wildlife, especially if you’re collecting in protected areas. Avoid overcollecting in any one area to preserve local populations.

Step 5: Preserve and Mount Your Specimens

Once you have captured your specimens, the next step is to preserve and mount them for display. Proper preservation is crucial for maintaining the quality of your collection over time. Here are the general steps for preserving and mounting insects:

  1. Euthanize the insect: Use a killing jar to euthanize the insect in a humane manner.
  2. Pin the insect: Once the insect is deceased, carefully pin it through the thorax (middle section) using an entomology pin. Ensure the insect is mounted straight and properly positioned.
  3. Label the specimen: Create a label with details about the specimen, including the species name (if known), location, date, and habitat where it was collected. This information is essential for organizing your collection and can help in future research.
  4. Preserve smaller insects: For smaller insects, place them in a vial with alcohol or another preserving solution.

Ensure that your mounted specimens are placed in a dry, cool, and dark environment to prevent deterioration. Over time, insects may fade or become brittle if exposed to too much sunlight or humidity.

Step 6: Organize Your Collection

Organization is key to a well-maintained entomology collection. There are several ways you can organize your specimens, depending on your focus and preferences. Some common methods include:

  • Taxonomic organization: Organizing by insect order, family, and species.
  • Geographical organization: Grouping specimens based on where they were collected.
  • Ecological organization: Categorizing insects by their ecological roles or habitats.

Labeling your specimens is crucial for easy identification and tracking. Use clear, concise labels that include the insect’s scientific name (genus and species), the date and location of collection, and any other relevant details about the specimen.

Step 7: Maintain Your Collection

To keep your collection in top condition, regular maintenance is essential. Here are some tips for caring for your entomology collection:

  • Check for pests: Insects in your collection can attract pests like moths, beetles, or mites. Regularly inspect your specimens for any signs of damage or pest infestations.
  • Store specimens properly: Keep your collection in a safe, dry place away from sunlight and moisture. Consider using display cases, drawers, or cabinets specifically designed for storing insect specimens.
  • Update your collection: As you continue collecting, you may discover new species or want to refine your collection. Regularly update your records and add new specimens to your collection.

Step 8: Share and Learn from Your Collection

One of the most rewarding aspects of collecting insects is sharing your knowledge and discoveries with others. You can participate in online forums, social media groups, or local naturalist clubs to share your collection, ask questions, and learn from other entomologists.

You may also consider contributing your collection to a museum, university, or research organization. Many institutions welcome donations of insect specimens, especially if they are rare or scientifically significant.

Final Thoughts

Starting your own entomology collection is an exciting and educational endeavor that allows you to explore the diversity of the insect world. By following these steps and being patient, you will build a collection that provides both a personal sense of accomplishment and valuable insights into the wonders of nature. Whether you’re just starting out or looking to expand your knowledge, your collection will serve as a unique record of the incredible insects that inhabit our world. Happy collecting!
