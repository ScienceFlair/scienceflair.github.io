How to Start Your Own Entomology Collection: A Beginner's Guide


Entomology is the scientific study of insects, one of the most diverse and fascinating groups of organisms on the planet. With over a million described species and potentially millions more waiting to be discovered, insects offer endless opportunities for exploration and study. Starting your own entomology collection can be an exciting and rewarding hobby that allows you to explore the intricate world of bugs while developing your scientific skills.

Whether you’re interested in building a collection for personal enjoyment, education, or scientific study, this guide will help you get started. From choosing the right equipment to learning how to preserve specimens, we’ll walk you through the essential steps of starting your own entomology collection.

Step 1: Understanding the Basics of Entomology

Before diving into insect collecting, it’s essential to understand the basics of entomology. The term “entomology” refers to the study of insects, but it also encompasses related fields such as the study of arachnids (spiders, scorpions), myriapods (millipedes, centipedes), and other arthropods.

Insects are classified into different orders, each with its own unique characteristics. Some of the most well-known insect orders include:

  • Coleoptera (Beetles)
  • Lepidoptera (Butterflies and Moths)
  • Diptera (Flies)
  • Hymenoptera (Bees, Wasps, Ants)
  • Orthoptera (Grasshoppers, Crickets)
  • Hemiptera (True Bugs)

Having a general understanding of insect taxonomy can help you identify species and appreciate the diversity of life forms within your collection.

Step 2: Gather the Necessary Equipment

To begin collecting insects, you’ll need some basic tools. While insect collecting doesn’t require a significant investment, having the right equipment will ensure you’re able to safely collect, store, and preserve your specimens for future study.

Essential Tools for Insect Collecting

  1. Collection Container

    • A small container, jar, or box to temporarily house insects as you collect them is essential. Ideally, this should be breathable to prevent suffocation but sturdy enough to prevent escape.

  2. Insect Net

    • An insect net is useful for capturing flying insects such as butterflies, moths, and dragonflies. There are different sizes and types available depending on your specific needs.

  3. Collecting Vials or Bottles

    • Once you’ve captured an insect, you’ll need a vial or bottle to transport it. Clear plastic or glass vials are often used to house insects for observation or preservation.

  4. Field Guide or Insect Identification Book

    • Having a reference book or mobile app to help identify the insects you collect is an important tool. Many field guides are available, some focused on specific regions, and others on particular insect groups.

  5. Tweezers or Forceps

    • These tools are useful for gently handling delicate insects without harming them. They can help you place insects in vials or on pins without causing injury.

  6. Insect Pins

    • Insect pins are used to mount specimens for display or preservation. These pins allow you to insert the insect into a display box while keeping it intact and undamaged.

  7. Preservation Tools

    • To preserve your specimens for the long term, you’ll need tools such as drying boards or ethanol. You might also need chemicals like formaldehyde or a freezing method depending on your preservation approach.

  8. Magnifying Glass or Loupe

    • A magnifying glass or loupe is crucial for closely examining the finer details of your specimens. This is especially helpful when identifying specific characteristics like wing patterns or mouthparts.

Step 3: Choose the Right Location

Choosing the right location is one of the most important decisions when it comes to collecting insects. Different environments host different species, and the time of day, season, and weather conditions can all influence the types of insects you’ll encounter. Here are some of the best places to collect insects:

1. Woodlands and Forests

  • Woodlands and forests provide a rich habitat for a variety of insects. Look for beetles, moths, ants, and more. Check under tree bark, on leaves, and in the soil for hidden species.

2. Gardens and Yards

  • If you’re just starting out, your own garden or yard can provide ample opportunities to collect insects. Look for bees, butterflies, spiders, and grasshoppers in flower beds, on shrubs, or near a water source.

3. Parks and Nature Reserves

  • Parks and nature reserves are excellent places to find a wide range of insects. These areas are often home to diverse habitats and ecosystems, which can lead to discovering rare or uncommon species.

4. Wetlands

  • Wetlands, such as ponds, marshes, and swamps, are perfect for collecting aquatic insects, including dragonflies, water beetles, and various species of mosquitoes. If you’re near a wetland, bring a net to collect specimens from the water’s surface.

5. Urban Areas

  • Don’t overlook urban environments! Insects like ants, cockroaches, and flies can be found even in the heart of cities. While they may not be as exotic as those in more natural habitats, they can still make for an interesting addition to your collection.

6. Nighttime Collecting

  • Many species, particularly moths, are more active at night. Consider setting up a light trap in your backyard or a local park to attract nocturnal insects.

Step 4: Collecting Insects Safely and Humanely

When collecting insects, it’s important to respect their environment and handle them gently to avoid harm. Here are some tips to ensure your collecting practices are both safe and humane:

  1. Handle with Care

    • Insects are delicate creatures. When capturing them, avoid using excessive force. If you’re using a net, be gentle to avoid injuring the insect.

  2. Ethical Collection

    • Avoid over-collecting from a single location or species. While it can be tempting to gather as many specimens as possible, it’s important to ensure that you’re not causing harm to local insect populations. Follow ethical guidelines and avoid collecting endangered or protected species.

  3. Use Appropriate Methods

    • Depending on the species, some insects may need to be killed for preservation. This can be done humanely using a killing jar, which contains a chemical like ethyl acetate. Alternatively, you can freeze your specimens if you prefer a more eco-friendly method.

  4. Respect Wildlife Regulations

    • Be aware of any local or national regulations regarding insect collection. Some areas may have restrictions, especially for protected or endangered species. Always make sure your collection practices comply with these rules.

Step 5: Preserving Your Insects

Preserving insects is essential to maintaining their quality and ensuring they remain a valuable part of your collection. Different insects require different preservation methods, but here are some general guidelines:

1. Drying

  • For many insects, the best preservation method is drying. You can use drying boards or boxes to allow insects to dry naturally. Place the insect on the board, pin it in place, and leave it to dry for several days to a few weeks, depending on the size.

2. Ethanol Preservation

  • For aquatic insects or those you don’t plan to display, preserving them in ethanol (alcohol) is an excellent option. Simply submerge the insect in a small vial of ethanol and store it in a cool, dark place.

3. Mounting

  • Once your insect is dry, it can be mounted onto an insect pin. Gently insert the pin through the thorax of the insect and position its legs and wings for a natural look. You can display your mounted specimens in a glass-fronted box for easy viewing.

4. Labeling

  • A crucial part of preserving your collection is labeling your specimens. This allows you to record important information, such as the species, date of collection, and location. Proper labeling ensures the scientific value of your collection and helps you keep track of your specimens.

Step 6: Organizing Your Collection

Once you’ve started building your collection, you’ll need a systematic way to organize it. There are many ways to arrange insects, but here are some common methods:

1. By Taxonomy

  • One of the most common methods is organizing insects by their taxonomic classification, starting with the highest level (order) and working your way down to species. This method allows you to easily compare related species.

2. By Habitat or Environment

  • Alternatively, you might choose to organize your collection based on the habitats from which the insects were collected. This could involve grouping insects from woodlands, gardens, wetlands, and so on.

3. By Geographic Location

  • Another popular organizational method is grouping insects by the area where they were found. This approach is especially useful if you collect from multiple regions.

4. By Color or Size

  • Some collectors prefer to arrange insects based on their physical characteristics, such as size, color, or wing patterns. This can create visually striking displays.

Step 7: Expanding Your Collection

As your collection grows, you may want to expand it by targeting specific insect groups or focusing on particular regions. You could also experiment with different collecting techniques, such as setting up light traps to attract nocturnal insects or using pitfall traps for ground-dwelling species.

Additionally, networking with other entomologists and collectors can open doors to new collecting opportunities. Many entomologists are part of online communities, where you can exchange information, trade specimens, or get advice from more experienced collectors.

Conclusion

Starting your own entomology collection is a rewarding journey that can provide you with a deeper appreciation for the natural world. By following these steps—understanding the basics, gathering the right equipment, choosing the right collection sites, collecting ethically, preserving your specimens, and organizing your collection—you can create a fascinating and scientifically valuable archive of the insect world.

Whether you’re interested in studying the behavior of insects, contributing to citizen science, or simply enjoying the beauty of these tiny creatures, your entomology collection will grow over time, reflecting your increasing knowledge and passion for the study of insects. Happy collecting!
