Eco Language: Unraveling the Jargon of Sustainability Practices


In the sphere of environmental conservation, the term ‘sustainability’ holds a dominion. But, just as in every powerful realm, this one too has its jargon. Known commonly as Eco language, this trove of terms can often bewilder even the most curious minds. Opting for recycled paper or which degree of a renewable energy isn’t as straightforward as it seems, is it?

This blog post unearths the intricacies of these eco-terms and demystifies them one by one, ensuring you’re well-equipped in your mission for a greener planet.

  1. Renewable Energy vs Non-Renewable Energy

It’s impossible to bypass the notion of energy when we talk about sustainability. The split between renewable and non-renewable energy is quite straightforward.

Renewable energy, fondly known as ‘clean’ energy, is obtained from natural sources that replenish at a faster rate than we deplete them - such as wind, sunshine, rain, tides, waves and geothermal heat. Solar panels, wind turbines and hydropower plants are your visual representatives of this energy.

On the flip side, non-renewable energy sources include coal, oil, natural gas, and nuclear energy. These are fossil fuels that take millions of years to form and get depleted way quicker than they can be replenished. Clearly, the choice of energy in your home, car or business needs ascertaining.

  1. Recycled Paper vs Virgin Paper

Heading over to paper, ‘recycled paper’ doesn’t refer exclusively to newspaper clippings. It’s a material that has been recovered and processed for reuse.

Virgin paper, unlike its recycled comrade, is paper produced from newly processed raw materials, specifically wood fibers extracted from our trees. For those who pen down their thoughts on paper, opting for recycled paper becomes a sustainable option.

  1. Carbon Footprint vs Water Footprint

With ‘footprint’ in every environmentalist’s vocabulary, the carbon footprint, or how much CO2 and greenhouse gases our daily activities emit, comes to the forefront. But what is that cousin of it – the water footprint?

The water footprint is essentially the total volume of fresh water used to produce the goods and services we consume - including everything from containerized produce to your favorite shirt. Both carbon and water footprints play crucial roles in the larger picture of sustainable living and conservation.

  1. Net Zero Emissions vs Carbon Neutral

‘Net zero emissions’ is often interchangeably used with ‘carbon neutral’. But there’s a fine line. Net zero emissions refer to a scenario where the amount of greenhouse gas emissions produced is equal to the amount removed from the atmosphere over a specific period.

Carbon neutrality, however, signifies that carbon emissions are balanced with an equal measure of carbon offset, for example, through planting trees, thus leading to a net zero emission effect.

  1. Permaculture & Intensive Farming

To the appeal of urban farming, permaculture lets your garden be your grocer. It’s a harmonious design of ecosystems that mimic natural arrangements, emphasizing on symbiotic relationships and interconnectedness. This style of farming spawns less environmental stress and boosts biodiversity.

Meanwhile, intensive farming, despite being the crux of large-scale agriculture, doesn’t focus on the environmental health. The practice involves closely and continuously stocking the land with livestock, increasing the organic growth of crops, and eliminating fallow periods to maximize yields.

  1. Composting vs Bokashi

Are you squirming at the thought of composting? Don’t let the delay intimidate or overwhelm you. Composting is the natural process of recycling organic matter, reducing the amount that goes to landfill.

Bokashi, a lesser-known composting method, is effective and convenient. It involves placing scraps in a container and covering it with bran. The process accelerates the decomposition process without the typical smell. An underground pit or a worm farm are also lesser-known alternatives that mesh well with small spaces.

  1. Biodegradable vs Eco-Friendly

When we’re on the cusp of choosing eco-friendly products, we often look for biodegradable or compostable items. But ain’t these two synonymous? Far from it.

Biodegradable items can be broken down by bacteria and other microorganisms, yes, but the process might still produce toxic substances and take years. Eco-friendly products, however, are those manufactured without harming the environment, considering resources and energy consumption during production, and minimizing waste and pollution.

Conclusion

Sustainability is your fuel for the future. Understanding these eco terms will pave the way for more informed decisions and stand you in good stead for a better planet. Happy eco-travelling!

The road to eco-friendliness isn’t just about trading in your non-renewable fuel for solar energy. It’s a conscious effort to be mindful of your carbon footprint and embrace sustainable practices, step by step. As iron sharpens iron, this journey hand in hand motivates us all, towards a sustainable existence.

Now that you’re enriched with the wealth of eco language, it’s time we put our knowledge to use and make sustainable practices the new norm. After all, the caring for this planet starts with educating ourselves and contemplating every action we take. The time to act is now, and the power lies in your hands.
