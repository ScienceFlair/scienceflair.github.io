Ethology in the Media: Bringing Science to the Public Eye


As a burgeoning field, ethology often faces an uphill battle when it comes to public awareness and understanding. Discipline that studies animal behavior, ethology has long struggled to escape the realm of academic jargon and enter the mainstream discourse. Fortunately, with the ascent of media and technology, ethologist researchers and communicators now have an unprecedented array of tools at their disposal for bridging the gap between science and society. In this post, I delve into several vital ways in which the intrinsic study of human and non-human animal behaviors is being brought to the public’s eye.

Firstly, documentaries have played an essential role in popularizing ethology. Documentaries like BBC’s ‘Planet Earth’ and ‘Diana: 7 Up,’ or National Geographic’s ‘Dr. Kiki’s Feline Nutrition and Health,’ unravel the mysteries of animal consciousness and behaviors through captivating visuals and captivating storytelling. By experiencing animals in action, viewers become more than passive observers—they empathize with these creatures, potentially provoking a profound sense of kinship that spurs further interest in the scientific study of ethology.

In addition, the dawn of the internet has pushed ethology into the lap of the masses. Sequoia Capital estimates that nearly half the world’s population now watches online video. Platforms like YouTube, Facebook, and TikTok have created space for a plethora of ethology-related content. From the popular wildlife photographers who make a living capturing incredible moments of animal behavior, like Sir David Attenborough, or smaller content creators seeking to share knowledge about psychedelic frogs or the raw energy of cheetah cubs, digital media underscores the diverse and ever-changing world of ethological study. This expanse of content allows for curious individuals to indulge in questions about how and why animals behave the way they do from the comfort of their own homes.

A third arena in which ethology is being shared is through engaging lectures and seminars. According to US News & World Report, online education is predicted to replace traditional on-campus education by 2025. As the landscape of education pivots toward the digital realm, so too can the delivery of informative and engaging content about ethology. The exponential growth of virtual conference platforms and software has allowed for lectures on animal behavior to reach beyond the confines of academic walls and into the homes of those with an interest in ethology.

In conclusion, while ethology has long struggled to break free from its academic cage, it’s an exciting time for the discipline as new media and technology envelopes the study in its ever-expanding tentacles. From documentaries with jaw-dropping visuals, to online platforms that bring wildlife action into our living rooms, to innovative means of delivering academic lectures and info-tainment, ethology is being presented in myriad ways that are accessible and attractive to a wide and eager audience.

It remains to be seen how fully the melding of ethology and media will democratize scientific knowledge and deepen our connection to our shared Earth. What is clear, however, is that the future of ethology will be coaxed into the light through the kaleidoscopic palette of media. Then and now, ethology has the potential to delight, educate, and ennoble the human audience as it casts light into the shadowy corners of animal existence. The merging of high technology with an ancient desire for enlightenment is sure to provoke a phenomenal surge of curiosity and understanding. It’s among these exciting and ever-evolving means that ethology breathes life and readers unequivocally witness the precious dance of molecules, thoughts, and behaviors that mark and sustain our planet.
