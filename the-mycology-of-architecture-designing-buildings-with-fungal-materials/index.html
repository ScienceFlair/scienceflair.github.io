The Mycology of Architecture: Designing Buildings with Fungal Materials


Hello, fellow design enthusiasts and curious minds! Today, we’ll embark on a fascinating journey into the realm of architecture and its unexpected marriage with fungi. As we dive deep into the world of mycology, we’ll explore how these seemingly simple organisms possess the potential to revolutionize the entire concept of building structures. Welcome to the innovative world of fungal architecture.

Our adventure begins with the introduction to mycelium, the hidden and extensive underground network of threads that constitute fungi. Mycelium is capable of forming strong and durable connections, often referred to as “wood wide web,” by interlinking with plant roots and creating a complex symbiotic relationship. Recognizing its robust structure, architects and designers have started experimenting with mycelium, demonstrating that our relationship with fungi can perhaps lead to a more sustainable and environmentally harmonious built environment.

Architect David Hanses explores the potential of mycelium in his project “Mycobuilt” where he uses mycelium to create building materials that have the ability to decompose after their life span, thus reducing the burden on landfills and offering a greener alternative to traditional construction materials. These bio-construction blocks are moulded into shapes resembling bricks and offer a highly stable and sturdy framework for buildings.

But that’s not all! The use of mycelium in architectural designs also offers opportunities for bioluminescent fungi. These fungi emit a soft, ethereal glow, which could be capitalized upon by incorporating them into modern building designs. This unique feature would not only add a touch of ecological elegance to the buildings, but it could also save electrical energy, thereby reducing the carbon footprints of the buildings.

There is another fascinating aspect of fungal materials that make them rather alluring for architects, and that is their capacity to clean pollutants and toxins present in the air. The air-filtering ability of various species of fungi can be harnessed to create a sustainable ventilation system for buildings.

In our pursuit of creating eco-friendly buildings, fungi-based biocomposites have gained importance due to their ability to imitate the behavior of living tissues. Hence, researchers and architects are incorporating them in façades to recycle CO2 released from enclosures, thus highlighting the fact that smart materials of biological origin have a promising future in the field of architecture.

Talking about sustainability, it’s essential to mention the mushroom-based materials created by MycoWorks. Their patented process, called Mycotech, employs mycelium to grow a material called " fruiting bodies" which is practically indistinguishable from leather. These mushroom leather alternatives comprise 20 times less CO2 and water than the cowhide leather, and the process requires just five days to produce instead of several years for conventional leather.

While we are moving towards designing eco-friendly habitats, we should also consider the part we play in the growth of the fungi. Hence, some architects like Jae Seung Lee of SMLA have made use of fungus grown on discarded cereal straw in their designs, enabling the process to take place on construction sites itself. This approach ensures that local materials are used and contributes to reducing transportation costs and dependency on imports.

In conclusion, mycology is offering unique opportunities to the field of architecture and design. By cultivating a symbiotic relationship between design and fungi, we can showcase the incredible potential fungi possess and push the boundaries of our built environments. This will not only contribute to the aesthetic quality of buildings but also ensure they are sustainable, resource-efficient, and harmonious with our planet.

The fusion between architecture and mycology engenders a meaningful environmental shift while creating structures that defy traditional building materials and shapes. As we continue to explore this boundless relationship between man and fungi, let’s ensure we tread responsibly and uphold the principles of ecology and sustainability in our architectural designs.

So, what do you say? Shall we embrace the fungi-frenzy in architecture and etch a greener, smarter imprint on Mother Earth? The choice is ours, but one thing’s for sure - The Mycology of Architecture is only blooming greater as time goes by.
