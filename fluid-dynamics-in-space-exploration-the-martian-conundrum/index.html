Fluid Dynamics in Space Exploration: The Martian Conundrum


As humanity embarks upon the next great leap in space exploration, with eyes firmly affixed on the Red Planet, Mars, a fascinating puzzle emerges from the rocks and sands: Fluid dynamics. While Newtonian physics brilliantly explains the behavior of fluids on Earth, our new extraterrestrial playground poses entirely different questions and situations on a cosmic stage. Before we can consider Mars capable of harboring life or as a future human colony, we need to gain a better understanding of how fluids behave on its surface.

Mars is less forgiving towards fluid dynamics than Earth. We experience 1g, the force pulling ourselves towards our home planet’s center, which makes the study of fluids relatively simple. Mars, however, offers much less than 1g, averaging about 62.6% of Earth’s gravity. What this means is, fluids behave differently on Mars as gases, liquids, and even aerosols on Mars behave as they might not on Earth.

Let us consider the simplest instance, dropping a water droplet on the Martian soil. While the droplet would behave similarly to on Earth regarding splashing and spreading over the ground, it would evaporate faster due to the difference in atmospheric pressure. The atmosphere on Mars is much thinner compared to Earth, Mars’ atmospheric pressure is about 0.6% of Earth’s. This leads to evaporation rates of water on Mars being roughly six times faster.

The behavior of water and Earth’s fluids are, in general, vastly different on Mars, due to low gravity, thin atmosphere, and cold weather, permeable soils, and thin atmosphere. The lack of pressure and gravitation doesn’t just imply faster evaporation; it also affects the erosion of soil by liquid water. Water in motion on Earth shapes our landscape, but not on Mars, at least not as the way we know it. Wind on Mars is stronger due to the thin atmosphere and low pressure, yet it wouldn’t have the same erosional power.

Let’s discuss some real examples of fluid dynamics in action on Mars. Beaming down on the Martian surface, we see that surface dust and sand storms are common occurrences. These create a fascinating fluid dynamic, as the dusty, low-density atmosphere behaves almost as a fluid itself. The dust devils are whirlwinds consisting of red Martian dust that spin out of the sand. Unlike their terrestrial counterparts, these sand devils are characterized by high concentrations of swirls and vortices.

Another phenomenon on Mars: the persistence of water features. For example, certain “dry” river beds seem to indicate water flowing like on Earth, albeit a lot slower due to lower gravity. Despite the water having long dried up, the cohesiveness of the soil can cause it to hold “stream” patterns. In Mars’ weak gravity, water could puddle, as low pressure allows low viscosity, more like honey than liquid on Earth.

The study of fluid dynamics on Mars is thus paramount not only for understanding the availability and behavior of water and other fluids but also for future colonization. It informs our understanding of how best to provide habitats and resources for humans on Mars, should we decide to make reality of science fiction.

In conclusion, studying fluid dynamics in space exploration, and particularly on Mars, provides fascinating insights. It tells us about fluids in unnatural states, from faster evaporation to slowed erosion. From swirling dust devils to dried riverbeds, each phenomenon unveils secrets of the Red Planet and paves the way for future missions. Fluid dynamics on Mars also implies a wealth of unrelated fields, from environmental science to human colonization readiness, which emphasizes the transformative role this area can play in the coming years.
