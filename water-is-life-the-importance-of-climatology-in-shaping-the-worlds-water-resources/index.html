Water is Life: The Importance of Climatology in Shaping the World's Water Resources


The sun rises over the horizon, and the first rays of light glide across the surface of the Earth. A new day begins, but this seemingly mundane process holds tremendous power over the fate of our planet’s most vital resource – water. Climate and water are inextricably linked, forming an intricate dance that plays out over thousands of years and determining the destiny of those who dwell upon the Earth. This blog post delves into the profound relationship between climatology and water resources, elucidating their impacts on ecosystems and human societies.

Climatology – the scientific study of the Earth’s climate, past, present, and future – focuses primarily on weather patterns and atmospheric conditions. However, the influence of climatology on water resources extends far beyond the atmosphere itself. As climatologists Deliang Chen and Jingyong Zhang reveal, water is not simply a body of liquid but rather a system of interconnections across the biosphere. Climate influences the availability and distribution of water, shaping the planet’s landscapes, and the plants and animals that inhabit them.

Humans have long recognized the need to live in harmony with water; ancient Egyptians, for example, worshipped the Nile River as both a lifeline and a deity. This relationship between humanity and water is no mere coincidence. Water is critical for satisfying basic human needs, including drinking, cooking, sanitation, agriculture, and public health. According to the UN, water scarcity affects more than 40% of the global population, a number set to rise as the globe warms and populations swell. Knowing this, we must understand the vital role climatology plays in shaping the world’s water resources.

Climate and precipitation go hand in hand, with precipitation the most direct form of climate’s interaction with water. There are periods where the climate delivers more precipitation than needed, causing floods, and others where its absence leads to drought. An excellent example of this is the case of El Niño and La Niña events. These periodic climactic phenomena cause widespread modification of atmospheric circulation, with the southern Oscillation index (SOI) measuring fluctuations in air pressure between Tahiti and Darwin – affecting both precipitation and temperature across the planet. Meanwhile, phenomena like the Intertropical Convergence Zone, more commonly known as the ITCZ, shifts north and south around Earth, resulting in areas receiving more rainfall while others become dry.

Climate’s impact on water is not only geographic but temporal As we’ve observed in recent years, glaciation cycles occurring over lengthy periods, which can dramatically alter the water storage on our planet, in oceans, lakes, rivers, and underground aquifers. Reiterating, climatology shapes the distribution of water resources over time and space.

Moreover, an intriguing fact often overlooked by casual observers is the role that climatology plays in determining the types and qualities of water present on the planet. While most people are familiar with surface water, the Earth’s water system includes groundwater, ice, and even atmospheric water vapor. Some parts of the world receive the majority of their water supply from precipitation, while in others, underground water networks hold most of the water resources. Climatology influences each of these systems, and we can harness this knowledge to improve the management and distribution of water resources.

In conclusion, the deep connection between climatology and water resources merits our keen attention. The relationship between climate, hydrologic cycle, and water availability affects all aspects of life, in both subtle and dramatic ways. Climate scientists, hydrologists, policy-makers, and the general public must remain vigilant, recognizing climate change’s significant potential influence on future water availability. By understanding the intricate relationship between climatology and water resources, we can nurture them more effectively in an increasingly drought-prone world and preserve water’s vital role in shaping our life as well as our civilizations.
