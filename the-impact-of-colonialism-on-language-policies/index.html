The Impact of Colonialism on Language Policies


Colonialism has left a profound impact on the history of humankind, significantly shaping the world we live in today. Language, as an integral component of cultural identity, was not immune to these changes. This essay will delve into the nuances of how colonialism influenced language policies, offering a historical perspective on the topic.

The Bijuridical Legacy: Language Policies & Cultural Supremacy

The arrival of European colonizers brought with it the doctrine of linguicism - the preferential selection and valuation of certain languages, intrinsically tied to cultural supremacy. For instance, the introduction of English and French to certain regions worldwide influenced regional vernaculars’ suppression, leading to the emergence of global lingua francas. The misconception of a ‘universal language’ facilitated cultural assimilation, instrumental in enforcing a colonizer’s language as the norm.

Language Policies & Colonizing Souls: The Missionary Angle

Cultural assimilation was further fueled by religious missionaries who often believed in converting the colonized to their faith. Teaching local languages wasn’t a priority, with missionaries prioritizing vernaculars’ extirpation to emphasize the importance of their universal faith. Decolonization entailed the recognition that languages constitute an essential part of cultural heritage, requiring protection and preservation. This realization has led to the introduction of multilingualism as a core principle in various postcolonial societies.

Multilateral Linguistic Policies: Unpacking International Organizations

The United Nations, among other international bodies, emerged as pivotal in promoting multilingualism - a move away from the monolingual policies of colonial powers. Recognition of the value of diverse languages facilitated the adoption of policies promoting multiculturalism and linguistic rights. Today, the international community acknowledges over 200 human languages, representing a profound shift from the colonial mindset that preferred a limited range of tongues.

Dialects & the Politics of Language: Relevance in the Modern World

Language policies have evolved significantly, yet the cultural complexities of dialects remain underrepresented. Recognizing these nuances is crucial in building inclusive societies. Understanding that languages are alive, growing, and changing continuously should remain significant in shaping language policies.

In Conclusion, our understanding of language policies must transcend the legacy of colonialism. Further research into this topic can help evolve our perspective on the world we inhabit, make meaningful contributions to sociolinguistics, and support the vibrant celebration of language and culture worldwide.
