Top 10 Earth Science Experiments for Kids


Exploring the wonders of Earth science can captivate young minds and spark a genuine curiosity about the natural world. Hands-on experiments provide an opportunity for kids to dive into topics like geology, meteorology, hydrology, and environmental science, all while having fun. By conducting these engaging experiments, children can think critically, ask questions, and better understand the physical processes shaping the Earth. Here are ten fascinating Earth science experiments that are perfect for kids.

1. Erupting Volcano Experiment

One of the most iconic Earth science experiments kids love is creating a model volcano. This activity is an engaging way to illustrate the process of volcanic eruptions and how magma transforms into lava.

To make your own erupting volcano:

  • Build a volcano model using clay or papier-mâché.
  • Place a small container (like a film canister) at the center of the volcano model to simulate the vent.
  • Pour baking soda into the container, then add a few drops of red and orange food coloring to mimic lava.
  • When ready, pour in vinegar and watch the “eruption” as bubbles and foam spill from the vent.

While the vinegar and baking soda reaction is a simple acid-base chemical process, it closely resembles the explosive release of gases during a real volcanic eruption.

2. Rock Cycle Crayon Experiment

How do rocks form and change over time? Teach kids about the rock cycle with a colorful and hands-on demonstration using crayons.

For this experiment:

  • Shave crayons into small pieces to symbolize rock fragments.
  • Apply gentle heat to the shavings to mimic the process of sedimentation and compaction, turning them into a “sedimentary rock.”
  • Add more heat and pressure to simulate the formation of “metamorphic rock.”
  • Finally, melt the wax completely to represent molten rock cooling into “igneous rock.”

This activity provides a powerful visual for helping kids understand how geological forces reshape rocks in nature.

3. Build a Water Cycle in a Bag

The water cycle is a foundational concept in Earth science. Kids can witness evaporation, condensation, and precipitation using a simple plastic bag experiment.

Steps to create a mini water cycle:

  • Fill a zip-lock bag with a small amount of water and add blue food coloring to make it more visible.
  • Seal the bag tightly and tape it to a sunny window.
  • Over the course of a day, observe as the water evaporates, condenses on the sides of the bag, and eventually “rains” back down.

This quick and easy activity shows how water moves through different states in the environment.

4. DIY Fossil Imprints

Fossils are a fascinating peek into ancient life on Earth. Kids can create their own fossil imprints using everyday materials.

Here’s how:

  • Roll out a ball of clay and flatten it into a disk.
  • Press objects like leaves, shells, or toy dinosaur feet into the clay to leave an imprint.
  • Bake or air-dry the clay to harden it, preserving the “fossil” impression.

This activity introduces concepts like fossilization and helps kids imagine how scientists study ancient life forms and ecosystems.

5. Ocean Currents in a Bowl

Why do currents move in the ocean? This experiment models the movement of warm and cold water, explaining how temperature differences drive ocean currents.

What you’ll need:

  • A glass bowl filled with water
  • A few ice cubes and red food coloring

Place the ice cubes on one side of the bowl to represent colder regions of the ocean. On the opposite side, carefully add warm water with red food coloring. Watch as the cold water sinks and the warm water rises, creating movement just like real ocean currents.

This visual experiment provides insight into the balance of Earth’s climate and how thermal energy circulates in the oceans.

6. Grow Your Own Crystals

Growing crystals is not only a fun experiment but also a fantastic way to show kids how minerals form in nature.

To grow crystals:

  • Dissolve salt, sugar, or borax in warm water until the solution is saturated.
  • Tie a string or pipe cleaner to a pencil, then suspend it in the solution without touching the sides of the container.
  • Leave it undisturbed for several days as the crystals begin to form on the string.

Crystals are natural patterns formed by the orderly arrangement of molecules, and this experiment helps kids understand how environmental conditions impact their growth.

7. Simulating Erosion with Sugar Cubes

Erosion is one of the most powerful forces that shapes the Earth’s surface. Using sugar cubes, kids can observe the effects of water erosion firsthand.

Steps for this experiment:

  • Build a small “mountain” out of sugar cubes on a plate or tray.
  • Use a spray bottle filled with water to mimic rainfall or flowing water.
  • Watch as pieces of the sugar cubes dissolve, simulating how rainwater and rivers erode mountains and sediment over time.

This simple and interactive activity explains how even small amounts of water can carve away landforms over millions of years.

8. Layered Sediment Jars

Sedimentary rocks tell stories about Earth’s history, and kids can recreate the process of their formation using a jar and household materials.

Here’s how to do it:

  • Fill a clear glass jar with alternating layers of sand, soil, and small pebbles.
  • Add water and shake the jar to mix everything together.
  • Let the jar sit for a few hours or overnight.

As the particles settle, kids will notice how heavier materials like pebbles sink to the bottom while finer sediments like clay settle on top. This activity provides a clear example of sedimentary rock layers and deposition.

9. Earthquake Simulation with Blocks

Earthquakes are a fascinating subject for kids to explore. Using building blocks, kids can simulate tectonic plate movements and understand how earthquakes occur.

Try this:

  • Stack a tower from building blocks.
  • Begin gently sliding the base back and forth to mimic tectonic plate movement.
  • Gradually increase intensity until the structure collapses, demonstrating how energy builds and is released during an earthquake.

This experiment introduces kids to the seismic activity that shapes our world and emphasizes the importance of structural stability in earthquake-prone areas.

10. DIY Soil Percolation Test

Why do some soils absorb water better than others? A soil percolation test helps kids understand soil composition and its effect on water movement.

Steps to explore soil properties:

  • Gather soil samples from different areas, such as sand, clay, or garden soil.
  • Fill several funnels with these samples and place them in jars to catch water.
  • Slowly pour the same amount of water into each funnel and observe how quickly it drains through the soil.

By comparing results, kids can learn about permeability and why certain soils are better suited for draining or holding water.

Earth science experiments for kids offer a tangible way to explore complex ideas about our planet. Whether they’re modeling volcanoes, simulating erosion, or growing crystals, these activities encourage curiosity, critical thinking, and a lifelong love for the natural world. Dive into these hands-on projects and watch as kids uncover the magic hidden in the Earth’s processes.
