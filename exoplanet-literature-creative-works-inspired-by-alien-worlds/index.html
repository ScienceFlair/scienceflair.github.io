Exoplanet Literature: Creative Works Inspired by Alien Worlds


The discovery of exoplanets – planets orbiting stars outside our solar system – has opened a whole new world of possibilities for science fiction authors, poets, and other creative individuals. Exoplanet literature explores the realms of imagination and fills the space with inspiring tales, poems, and myths that revolve around these celestial beings. In this blog post, let’s delve into the wonderful genre of exoplanet literature, showcasing some iconic and lesser-known works, and discuss how these alien worlds inspire creativity.

The Birth of Exoplanet Literature

The societal romanticization of space exploration and the recent influx of exoplanet discoveries have given rise to the exoplanet literature genre. The term “exoplanet” was first used in a 1961 article in Science News, and since then, the number of known exoplanets has significantly increased. With this knowledge, creative minds have started imagining and writing about these alien worlds, fostering the exoplanet literature genre.

The Seduction of Science and Imagination

Exoplanet literature embraces both scientific facts and the creativity necessary to imagine alien landscapes, cultures, and lifeforms. Authors build upon the principles of astrophysics, planetary science, and chemistry to conceive realistic and relatable alien worlds, drawing upon the public’s fascination with the unknown.

For instance, Kim Stanley Robinson’s “Red Moon” depicts a future where humans have colonized a tidally-locked exomoon orbiting a gas giant. The story incorporates the scientific constraints of tidal locking while conjuring up an engaging narrative. Similarly, in Jeff VanderMeer’s “Annabel,” exoplanets serve as a backdrop for a tale of love and survival, melding factual exoplanet details with imaginative storytelling.

Famous Works and Literary Icons

Renowned authors have made significant strides in the exoplanet literature genre, with notable works like Octavia Butler’s “Earthseed” series, Gregory Benford’s “Galactic Club,” and Becky Chambers’ “The Wayfinder” series. Further down the list, lesser-known gems like Jane Lewty’s “The Survival Paradox” and Doris Piserchia’s “Earthsparks” provide rich insight into how creative minds are inspired by these distant worlds.

Exoplanets in Poetry and Myth

Not just limited to novels and short stories, the realm of exoplanet literature extends to poetry and myths. For example, Margaret Atwood’s “Hag-Seed” relates a myth centered around an exomoon, exploring themes of repetition and transformation. In her collection “The Insect Denied,” Carol Ann Duffy masterfully crafts poems that dive into the strange and ethereal nature of exoplanets.

The Future and Influence of Exoplanet Literature

As exoplanet discoveries continue to grow, so, too, will the exoplanet literature genre. Creative minds will be further inspired to draw upon the scientific facts and feasible scenarios for their stories, poems, and myths. The intricate blend of real-world astrophysics and the freedom of imaginative worlds can only enhance the captivating nature of this genre.

In conclusion, exoplanet literature is a thriving genre that encapsulates the vast, awe-inspiring nature of our universe. From the classics to the newly established, each piece takes us on a journey through time and space. Inspired by real exoplanets, these imaginative narratives, poems, and myths allow us to ponder the questions at the core of our existence: Are we alone? What is life? How do we survive? Exoplanet literature invites us to explore these questions, providing us with answers from the corners of our universe and beyond.
