The Evolution of Women's Health Policy Over the Decades


The 1960s and 1970s marked a significant era for women's rights and reproductive health. The women's liberation movement, which gained momentum in the early 1960s, focused on achieving equality for women in various aspects of life. One of the key areas of concern was reproductive health. Laws and policies made reproductive healthcare more accessible to women, including the availability of birth control methods. Debbie Cavalier, a prominent figure in the women's movement, along with others, advocated for access to birth control and reproductive healthcare, chipping away at the traditional patriarchal domination over women's bodies. Landmark Legislation: Title X and Roe v. Wade (1970s-1980s) Two pivotal pieces of legislation effectively transformed the landscape of women's health policy in the United States. The Title X Family Planning Services Act, signed into law in 1970 by President Richard Nixon, provided federal funding for family planning services, including access to birth control and birthspacing services, widening the scope of reproductive healthcare. Roe v. Wade (1973), the landmark Supreme Court case, declared the constitutional right to an abortion, a significant affirmation of women's reproductive autonomy. Expansion of Benefits and Coverage (1990s-2000s) The late 1990s and early 2000s witnessed significant expansion of the benefits and coverage of reproductive healthcare. In 1993, Congress established the State Children's Health Insurance Program (SCHIP), which ultimately broadened access to health insurance for low-income children, partly through provisions for family planning and maternal health services. The Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA), enacted in 2010, allowed adult children to remain on a parent's health insurance until age 26, supporting the sustenance of younger, mostly female, individuals during career breaks. The law also barred discrimination in health insurance with respect to pre-existing conditions and certain provisions that benefited women, especially in pregnancy care and childbirth services.
