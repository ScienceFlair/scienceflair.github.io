Marine Extinctions: The Vanishing Species of the Oceans


The vibrant oceans that surround our planet, full of life and majesty, are an integral part of Earth’s rich biodiversity. However, hidden beneath the surface lies a growing threat to the life that inhabits them: the specter of extinction. Sadly, this threat is becoming increasingly apparent among marine species, alarming not only biologists, but all humanity. In this blog post, we delve into the world of marine extinctions, exploring the alarming evidence of vanishing species, the possible causes, and potential solutions to stem this tide of loss.

As we attempt to gain a broader understanding of marine extinctions, it’s essential to grasp the sheer scale of loss that has already occurred. A common misconception was that the oceans were too vast and resilient to face the same threats as terrestrial habitats. However, this conceptual barrier was shattered by the publication of a groundbreaking study by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List of Threatened Species in 2019. The report revealed an astonishing number of marine extinctions; a tally which far exceeds the widely publicized terrestrial extinctions. This unsettling truth sheds light on a forgotten face of biodiversity loss, highlighting the vanishing marine species beneath the waves.

While marine extinctions have been occurring long before humans roamed the Earth, the current rate of loss is astonishingly rapid. Around 90% of large fish species have disappeared since the dawn of the Industrial Revolution, while overfishing and climate change are hastening the decline of species in contemporary times. The grim reality sparked an extensive study published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) in 2016, which estimated that the global extinction rate of marine species is increasing about 17 times faster than the rate of extinctions in the pre-human era.

Despite the clear and present danger posed by marine extinctions, addressing this pressing issue begins with recognizing just how many species are at risk. Some marine species that are significantly threatened by extinction include:

  1. Vaquita (Phocoena sinus): Dubbed the ‘panda of the sea,’ the vaquita is the most endangered marine mammal in the world. Found solely in the Gulf of California, the vaquita is trapped by gillnets intended for commercially valuable fish species, like the endangered totoaba, pushing them to the brink of extinction.

  2. Northern,right_whale (Eubalaena glacialis): The northern right whale’s population has declined drastically due to ship strikes and entanglement in fishing gear. With a current population of around 400 individuals, the fight to save this species is a critical priority for conservationists.

  3. Hawksbill sea turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata): Once valued for their beautiful shells, the global trade of hawksbill sea turtles is now strictly regulated. However, climate change-induced coral bleaching, poaching, and accidental capture in fishing gear continue to threaten this vulnerable species.

  4. Frilled shark (Chlamydoselachus anguineus): Despite its ancient lineage, the future of this mysterious deep-sea predator hangs in the balance due to slow reproduction and overfishing.

The overarching threat driving marine extinctions lies in human activities, but they manifest in various forms:

  1. Overfishing: Overfishing not only drains a species’ population, depriving humans of their invaluable resources but can also create a ripple effect that destabilizes the entire marine ecosystem.

  2. Climate change: Warming oceans not only disrupt marine species’ habitats but also cause physiological challenges, coral bleaching, and complete ecosystem shifts.

  3. Pollution: Chemicals, plastics, and other marine pollutants impact marine species’ behavior, respiration, and ingestion, frequently leading to substantial declines in population sizes.

  4. Coral reef degradation: Coral reef systems, vital habitats for a multitude of species, are being eroded and fragmented due to growing ocean acidity and rising global temperatures.

To respond to this crisis, several strategies can be employed to address the root causes and impending threats to marine extinctions:

  1. Sustainable fisheries management: By implementing strict catch limits and more selective fishing gear, we can curb depletion of overfished marine species and prevent ecosystem collapse.

  2. Habitat protection: Conservationists must work to establish protected and marine reserve areas to shield vulnerable species from direct human threats.

  3. Pollution reduction: Reducing pollution requires multifaceted approaches, such as improving waste management and encouraging ocean-friendly consumption habits.

  4. Climate change mitigation: As the greatest looming threat to the future of marine life, combating climate change is paramount. Communities worldwide must embrace environmentally friendly technologies, work to reduce carbon emissions, and encourage renewable energy alternatives.

Addressing marine extinctions demands nothing less than holding ourselves accountable for the irreversible damage we have inflicted on these precious ecosystems. It requires a global and concerted effort to protect vulnerable marine species before it is too late.
