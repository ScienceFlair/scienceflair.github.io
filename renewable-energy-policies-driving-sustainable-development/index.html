Renewable Energy Policies: Driving Sustainable Development


The rise of climate change on the global agenda has urged governments to invest in renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, and hydroelectric power. In this blog post, we will discuss how renewable energy policies drive sustainable development and fuel the transition towards cleaner energy systems.

Renewable energy sources have multiple benefits. They are virtually limitless, replenished naturally, and free to access. As such, they pose a more scalable and cost-effective solution to meet increasing energy demands. Moreover, their usage leads to reduced greenhouse gas emissions and air pollutants, mitigating the effects of climate change and improving public health outcomes.

Governments worldwide have increasingly turned towards renewables for various reasons. One key factor is the global race to reach net-zero emissions by mid-century to limit average global warming to 1.5°C. Additionally, the growing global demand for affordable and reliable energy sources propels policymakers to diversify and modernize their energy mix. Here are three examples of how governments are adopting renewable energy policies:

Feed-in Tariffs (FIT) Schemes: Feed-in Tariffs are government policies that incentivize renewable energy generation through economic measures. Governments mandate utilities to buy renewable energy generated by households, businesses, and communities at a guaranteed price – the ‘feed-in tariff.’ This guarantee provides a stable and predictable revenue stream for renewable energy developers, reducing their financial risk. Germany’s “Estrich-Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz” (EEG) is an excellent example of FIT schemes. The EEG has stimulated an 80-fold increase in renewable energy production since its introduction in 2000, making Germany a global leader in renewable energy deployment.

Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS): Renewable Portfolio Standards are legislated targets that require electricity retailers to source a specific minimum percentage of their electricity from renewable energy sources. Australia, for example, has implemented a renewable energy target (RET) under which electricity retailers must increase the share of renewable energy in their portfolio to 50% by 2020. This policy stimulates competition and market innovation while reducing greenhouse gas emissions and promoting clean energy technologies.

Green Bans and Moratoria: Green Bans are restrictions on certain development activities to protect the environment or preserve public health outcomes. In New South Wales, Australia, green bans have been used effectively to protect culturally and historically significant buildings from demolition and destruction. Brazil halted all large hydroelectric projects on Indigenous land in 2016 in response to social and environmental concerns, demonstrating the importance of local communities in sustainable development.

Renewable energy policies are vital in creating the right conditions to promote investment in renewable energy projects. These policies alleviate investor anxieties, provide long-term project revenues, and enable the development of the necessary infrastructure for a clean energy future. As these policies drive sustainable development, renewable energy’s global share is steadily increasing.

As we look to the future, the importance of renewable energy policies will only grow. With growing populations and increasing demand for energy, policymakers must work to establish effective renewable energy policies to secure sustainable development and address climate change concerns adequately. By doing so, we can create a cleaner, brighter future for generations to come.
