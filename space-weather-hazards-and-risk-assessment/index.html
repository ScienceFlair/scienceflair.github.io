Space Weather Hazards and Risk Assessment


The sun, our magnificent star, has had a significant impact on Earth’s atmosphere and climate. Space weather refers to environmental conditions in space, resulting from solar activity such as solar flares, coronal mass ejections (CMEs), solar wind, and geomagnetic storms. These phenomena can have profound consequences on our technologies and even human society.

The Importance of Space Weather

Space weather has been a pertinent concern for some time, mainly due to its potential impact on satellite communication, navigation systems, and power grids. Satellites that provide critical GPS navigation for transportation and communication services can be disrupted during extreme space weather events. Solar flares and coronal mass ejections can interfere with radio waves, disrupting communication lines and interrupting connections between continents.

Additionally, the energy surges in these geomagnetic storms can induce powerful electrical currents on the ground, leading to extreme geomagnetically induced currents (GICs). This can trip out power transformers, creating power outages. This might amount to millions of dollars in economic losses and emergency response for utilities worldwide.

Moreover, space weather can impact our daily lives beyond technology. Cosmic radiation from solar storms can lead to increased radiation exposure for airline passengers and astronauts, leading to health implications. The southern and northern lights, Birkeland currents, and other beautiful effects of space weather (for those located in the polar regions) serve as a reminder that our planet is not immune to the sun’s impact.

Space Weather Risks and Assessments

Solar Radiation

Solar radiation refers to the high-energy particles and electromagnetic radiation which the sun emits. One prominent risk associated with solar radiation is radiation exposure in commercial air travel at high altitudes. At altitudes above 10 kilometers, an airplane can be exposed to an increased dose of solar radiation. However, reliable radiation protection measures are in place to protect passengers and crews.

Astronauts on long-duration space missions, such as those undertaking a mission to Mars, are exposed to higher ionizing radiation, mainly from cosmic rays. The International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) and NASA have established guidelines for the radiation exposure of astronauts.

Geomagnetically Induced Currents (GICs)

GICs arise when currents induced in the Earth’s ground from magnetospheric disturbances create prolonged disruptions to power grids. These disturbances are unwanted in any electrical power system. GICs are significant risks for power transformers, substation equipment, and even oil pipelines.

Preparedness and risk assessments for GICs and other space weather hazards are crucial, as extreme events of prolonged durations may lead to widespread power outages. A 2003 Swedish study revealed the impacts of past geomagnetic storms on Swedish power systems. Since then, further research emphasizes understanding the broader scope of these space weather hazards’ consequences and planning countermeasures.

Communication Disruptions

Communication system disruptions are perhaps the most noticeable space weather hazard effect. Perturbations of the ionosphere due to increased ionization hinder short-wave radio communication even in well-maintained communication systems. Solar flares can disturb rendering GPS data, resulting in consecutive errors. Navigation systems errors and outages can significantly impact passenger aircraft, maritime vessels, and car fleets of transportation companies.

Conclusion

While space weather generally imparts spectacular displays, it also poses an undeniable risk to various aspects of life. While the effects of space weather are sometimes difficult to measure, the truth is that the solar influence is undeniable. With proper planning and knowledge, we can enhance our understanding and prepare for the hazards that space weather throws our way. We must continue to study and monitor space weather and develop resilience measures so that we can continue to enjoy life’s comforts without interference from our magnificent, yet unpredictable, source: the Sun.
