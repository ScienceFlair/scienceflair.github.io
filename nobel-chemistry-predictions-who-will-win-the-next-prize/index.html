Nobel Chemistry Predictions: Who Will Win the Next Prize?


The Nobel Prize in Chemistry represents the pinnacle of achievement in the fields of chemistry and chemical engineering. Every year, scientists around the world await the announcement, holding their breath in anticipation of seeing their colleagues and peers lauded for their groundbreaking research and significant contributions to the subject. The field of chemistry remains dynamic, with new discoveries unveiled almost daily. As we hurtle towards 2022, we have wondered about who could be in line to receive the prestigious award. Let’s take a look at some potential candidates.

Oliver Duwe and the quest for a protein structure database

Oliver Duwe is a German scientist working at the University of Duisburg-Essen. His main focus lies in deciphering the complex configurations of proteins. Through his work, Duwe and his team have developed a comprehensive, online database that catalogues protein structures. Their database, known as PDBe (Protein Data Bank in Europe), allows researchers to study and analyze the structure of these complex molecules, thus driving further scientific inquiry. There’s no doubt that their efforts have significantly accelerated the pace of biochemical research. Should his work continue to develop, Duwe could be in contention for a long overdue chemistry Nobel Prize.

Mark Miodownik and the exploration of material science

Mark Miodownik, a British scientist and engineer, is the director of the UCL Institute of Making. His research is concentrated on material engineering, a field that delves into the creation and understanding of various materials to improve our current way of life. Miodownik’s award-winning TED Talk _ and even his textbooks _ engage and captivate audiences because of his contagious enthusiasm for chemistry and material science.

Could Miodownik win the Nobel Chemistry Prize? His contributions to our understanding of simple and composite materials, from materials for human tissue engineering to the exploration of materials in spacecraft, are profound. His innovative ideas may well earn him the recognition and accolades of the Nobel committee.

Fatemeh Tabarestani and the complexity of chemical systems

Fatemeh Tabarestani, a computational chemist at the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Lausanne, seeks to understand the complexity of chemical systems. Her work on multiscale modeling has paved the way for advanced simulations that can predict chemical reactions and their results with exceptional accuracy. As much as chemistry is about understanding molecules, it’s also about predicting how those molecules interact within a dynamic ecosystem. Such predictions are indispensable for medical research, materials science, and engineering alike. Tabarestani’s groundbreaking work on computational chemistry could catapult her to the forefront of Nobel laureates.

So, who will ultimately win the prize remains a mystery, but these contemporary scientists have made significant advancements in their respective fields. Any of them could be future Nobel Prize laureates in Chemistry, influencing the course-altering trajectory of chemistry, for decades to come. These are just three examples of a diverse range of talents working to advance the field of chemistry. Many others who are defying the boundaries of human knowledge are yet to be acknowledged.

For those who are eager to predict which scientist will take the prize, remember that the road to Stockholm is an unpredictable journey, replete with discoveries and serendipity. But one thing remains certain: the excitement never wanes as the scientific world continues to shine with the genius visionaries that propel it forward. Stay tuned for the aromas and effervescence of scientific breakthroughs, as we keep our ears to the ground for the next Nobel to unfold.
