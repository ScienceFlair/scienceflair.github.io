Evolutionary Ethics: Morality from an Evolutionary Lens


The field of biology has made significant advancements over the past two centuries through the concept of evolution and natural selection. Likewise, the study of evolutionary ethics has grown to understand the moral principles that have shaped our world. In this article, we will examine the evolutionary basis of morality and its implications on our understanding of societal norms.

So, What is Evolutionary Ethics?

Evolutionary ethics is a branch of the philosophy of biology that explores the relationship between human morality and the evolutionary processes of natural selection. It takes the idea of evolution as a guiding principle to understand the development of moral systems.

Morality and Evolution: Shared Characteristics

Before diving in, let’s first understand how morality aligns with the principles of evolution. Like all living things, humans evolved to survive and prosper in their environment. Over time, advances in communication, tool-making, cooperation, and an exponential increase in brain size supported the development of increasingly complex moral systems.

These moral systems are intimately connected to our capacity for empathy, deception, altruism, and cooperation - traits that both result from and drive our evolutionary success.

Natural Selection and Moral Development

Consider the process of natural selection. The individuals within a species best adapted to their environment are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass on their beneficial traits to future generations. This same logic applies to the morality associated with certain behaviors.

For example, the tendency to cooperate and share resources in a group fosters trust and social cohesion, which have been vital components contributing to the survival and success of human societies. This resulted not just in advantageous biological traits, but also in advantageous moral codes that supported and reinforced cooperative behaviors.

In turn, individuals who exhibit traits like honesty, fairness, and empathy are seen as trustworthy and more likely to receive social and material support. Being perceived as honest, fair, and empathetic was necessary for successful social bonds and cooperation, and so these traits grew more common within our species as generations passed.

Evolutionary Ethics: Moving beyond the foundations

While natural selection can provide a foundation for understanding fundamental moral principles, it’s only part of the entire picture. Societies continue to evolve with complex moral systems that continually need to adapt to new environmental factors and a changing world.

As humans develop new social groups, cultures, and technologies, longstanding moral systems can come under scrutiny and lead to new adaptations. Take, for example, the evolution of morality in regards to the treatment of minorities and equality; as our societies have evolved so have our moral attitudes concerning these subjects.

Closing Thoughts

The evolutionary lens has opened new doors for exploring the foundations of morality and the reasons behind different moral values within human societies. By understanding these driving forces that have shaped our moral conscience, we can begin to better comprehend our moral obligations and how moral codes might change in the future.

As we move forward as a species, it’s crucial to remember that the wellspring of our morality lies in our lineage - an intimate and complex connection between our biology and morality. So when considering the principles of right and wrong, perhaps it’s best to first take a glance at our ancestry.
