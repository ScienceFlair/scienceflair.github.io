As the world grapples with the complexities of implementing universal health coverage (UHC), a sense of trade-offs and tricky compromises looms large over the landscape of global healthcare. The pressing issue of UHC has sparked intense debates among policymakers, healthcare professionals, and stakeholders from across the globe. With good reason, as the concept of UHC promises to revolutionize the provision of healthcare, bringing life-changing benefits to millions of people who, until now, have been left behind by inadequate healthcare systems. At its core, UHC refers to a comprehensive healthcare system that ensures all citizens have access to essential health services, without suffering financial hardship as a result. In other words, UHC promises to eliminate out-of-pocket expenses, excess billing, and deductibles for healthcare services. While the appeal of UHC is undeniable, the path to achieving it is not without its pitfalls. Implementing UHC requires a granular understanding of the intricate web of social, economic, and healthcare factors that make or break a healthcare system. One significant challenge is resource mobilization. UHC requires substantial financial investment from governments, which can be a daunting task, especially for developing countries with limited fiscal capabilities. Equitable and sustainable financing mechanisms are crucial to generate the necessary revenue to fund UHC initiatives. However, identifying and allocating sufficient funds for healthcare services while simultaneously maintaining economic growth and managing existing healthcare expenditure can be a staggering task. Another critical challenge is mandating quality and availability of healthcare services. To achieve UHC, adequate infrastructure and manpower need to be established to cater to the healthcare demands of the population. Nonetheless, guaranteeing the quality of healthcare services is becoming increasingly difficult in the midst of competing priorities, expertise shortages, and infrastructure gaps in numerous parts of the world. Access and equity is yet another mammoth hurdle in UHC implementation.
