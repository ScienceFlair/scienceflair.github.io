The Role of Clouds in Earth's Climate System

Clouds are often seen as fluffy, white puffs drifting across the sky, but they play a crucial role in regulating Earth’s climate. From reflecting sunlight and trapping heat to influencing precipitation patterns, clouds have a significant impact on the planet’s energy balance and weather patterns. Understanding the complexities of cloud formation and behavior is essential for accurately predicting climate change and its impacts on both local and global scales. Clouds form when water vapor in the atmosphere condenses into tiny droplets or ice crystals around microscopic particles called cloud condensation nuclei (CCN). These particles can include dust, pollen, sea salt, and pollution from human activities. As air rises and cools, it reaches its dew point—the temperature at which it becomes saturated with water vapor—and condensation occurs, forming clouds.
