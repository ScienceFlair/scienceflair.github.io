10 Essential Chemistry Experiments You Must Try at Home


Have some fun and create a mini candle lab. With a clean and dry glass jar, cotton balls, and food coloring, you can make colorful candles in various shapes and sizes. First, fill the jar with water and add a few drops of food coloring. Next, put a cotton ball soaked in vegetable oil at the bottom of the jar. Light the cotton ball, and observe the colorful show as the colors float up through the water. The oil creates a barrier and traps the color at the top of the water.

  1. Polymer Magic Experiment Discover the mysteries of polymers with this easy and fun experiment. Start by mixing two cups of warm water and one cup of cold water. Then, add 1/2 cup of sugar to the solution. Using a cotton swab, dissolve a small amount of food coloring into the sugar solution. Finally, dip a piece of long hair (from a brush) into the solution and watch as the color appears to travel along the hair. The scientific explanation: the colored sugar solution forms a long polymer strand connected to the hair fiber.

  2. Fizzy Lemon Volcano Experiment In this experiment, learn how baking soda and vinegar react to produce carbon dioxide gas. Start by flattening a clay ball and carving a crater in the middle. Spray the crater with a vinegar-and-food-coloring solution and add a lump of baking-soda-based clay into the crater. Watch as your homemade lemon volcano erupts with foam, creating a spectacular chemical reaction.

  3. Homemade Chromatography Art Chromatography is a useful technique for separating and identifying different chemicals. Using coffee filters, water, markers, and straws, you can make beautiful chromatography art. Simply draw a colorful design with markers on the coffee filter, dampen the filter with water, and insert a straw through the drawing. Watch as the colors begin to separate along the filter as the different components of the ink travel through the paper at varying rates.

  4. Flame Test Experiment Observing the colors of the flame produced by different metals can help you understand how they affect the flame color. Using a propane torch (carefully supervise this part), hold different metal wires, like copper, sodium, or magnesium, near the flame. Note the resulting colors and learn more about their atomic structure.

  5. Magic Milk Experiment Create an otherworldly effect with this magical milk experiment. All you need is milk, food coloring, dish soap, and a straw. Draw a design on a plate with food coloring and fill in the design with milk. Blow through the straw to create bubbles in the milk. The dish soap breaks the surface tension, causing the food coloring to spread out to form a unique design.

  6. Oobleck Experiment Learn about non-Newtonian fluids with this oobleck experiment. Mix together two cups of cornstarch and one cup of water, creating a boozy-like substance. Oobleck flows like a liquid but responds like a solid when you apply pressure. It’s an example of a non-Newtonian fluid, which doesn’t exhibit traditional liquid or solid properties.

  7. Crystal Growing Experiment Discover the wonders of crystal formation with this simple experiment. Fill a jar with hot water and dissolve borax or Epsom salts in it. Once dissolved, add blue food coloring to the solution. To grow the crystals, tie a string across the mouth of the jar, and let the liquid evaporate naturally. As the water evaporates, crystals will start to form on the string, exhibiting beautiful and intricate patterns.

  8. Elephant Toothpaste Experiment Join the fun with this fizzy and foamy experiment. Pour sparkling water into a cup, add a few drops of dish soap and mix. Pour the mixture into another cup with yeast and warm water. Watch as the reaction creates a glorious foam explosive. The yeast ferments the sugars in the detergent, releasing oxygen and causing the soap bubbles to grow rapidly, eventually creating a foamy geyser.

  9. Mnemonic Major System Experiment Master your numbers with the mnemonic major system using this fun experiment. Label ten different labeled jars with the numbers 0-9. Fill each jar with a different combination of 7 to 13 pebbles, and hide them at random locations. Memorize the number corresponding to a jar to memorize the locations of the pebbles inside. This process forms a memory peg for the digits and can help you remember numbers more effectively.

These simple chemistry experiments not only teach you about fascinating chemical reactions but also encourage creativity and help build problem-solving skills. So, gather your materials and have fun exploring the wonderful world of chemistry!
