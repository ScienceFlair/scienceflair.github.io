The Best Plants for Attracting Hummingbirds to Your Garden


There’s a unique charm to watching hummingbirds flutter through your garden—their iridescent feathers shimmering in sunlight, wings beating so fast they create a gentle hum, and their seemingly magical ability to hover near blossoms. These tiny creatures are not just delightful to observe; they also play a vital role in pollination, supporting the health of your garden’s ecosystem. If you’re hoping to invite more of these delightful visitors into your outdoor space, the secret lies in the plants you choose.

Creating a hummingbird-friendly garden requires a blend of the right flowers, care, and garden design. In this guide, we’ll explore some of the best plants for attracting hummingbirds to your garden, including their unique features and how to care for them effectively.

Understanding What Attracts Hummingbirds

Before diving into the list of plants, it’s helpful to understand the general preferences of hummingbirds. These birds are primarily attracted to bright colors, especially reds and pinks, but they also respond to oranges, yellows, and purples. Flowers that produce copious amounts of nectar with tubular shapes are particularly appealing, as their slender beaks and long tongues are perfectly adapted for extracting nectar.

Hummingbirds are especially drawn to native plants, which tend to offer the nectar potency and flower shapes they evolved alongside. Additionally, a diverse garden—one that provides blooms throughout spring, summer, and fall—will encourage repeat visits.

Top Plants to Attract Hummingbirds

1. Bee Balm (Monarda didyma)

Bee balm is a classic hummingbird magnet with its bright red, coral, or pink tubular flowers and distinctive splash of purple in some varieties. Native to North America, it thrives in full sun to partial shade and prefers rich, moist soil.

  • Flower shape: Tubular clusters ideal for hummingbird feeding.
  • Bloom time: Early summer to early fall.
  • Growing tips: Regular watering and deadheading spent flowers increase blooming longevity.

2. Trumpet Vine (Campsis radicans)

With trumpet-shaped, vivid orange-red flowers, trumpet vine is practically irresistible to hummingbirds. This fast-growing climber can quickly cover fences, arbors, or trellises, creating vertical interest.

  • Flower shape: Large, tubular blooms perfect for hummingbirds.
  • Bloom time: Summer to early fall.
  • Growing tips: Requires space and support; prune annually to maintain shape.

3. Coral Honeysuckle (Lonicera sempervirens)

A native honeysuckle variety, coral honeysuckle produces long, trumpet-like flowers in reds and oranges that are bursting with nectar. Unlike invasive Japanese honeysuckles, this one is wildlife-friendly and adaptable.

  • Flower shape: Tubular, clustered flowers.
  • Bloom time: Late spring through summer.
  • Growing tips: Can grow as a vine or shrub and prefers well-drained soil and full sun.

4. Salvia (Salvia spp.)

Salvias are a diverse group with numerous species and cultivars that attract hummingbirds. Their tubular flowers come in various colors including deep reds, purples, and blues. Notable types include Scarlet Sage (Salvia coccinea) and Mexican Bush Sage (Salvia leucantha).

  • Flower shape: Tubular and spike-like.
  • Bloom time: Mid-summer through fall.
  • Growing tips: Plant in sunny spots; drought-tolerant once established.

5. Cardinal Flower (Lobelia cardinalis)

This striking native plant has fiery red spikes that stand out in any garden, making it a favorite hummingbird food source. It thrives near water and blooms in late summer when nectar is often in demand.

  • Flower shape: Long, tubular flowers with a slightly flaring mouth.
  • Bloom time: Late summer to early fall.
  • Growing tips: Prefers moist soil and partial sun.

6. Penstemon (Penstemon barbatus)

Also known as beardtongue, Penstemon offers clusters of tubular flowers in shades ranging from red to purple. Native penstemons enrich the habitat and are especially inviting to hummingbirds.

  • Flower shape: Elongated tubular flowers.
  • Bloom time: Late spring to mid-summer.
  • Growing tips: Prefers full sun and well-drained soil.

7. Butterfly Bush (Buddleja davidii)

Though it’s more commonly known for attracting butterflies, the butterfly bush also draws hummingbirds with its fragrant clusters of tubular flowers in purple, pink, white, and sometimes red.

  • Flower shape: Cone-shaped flower clusters with small tubular blossoms.
  • Bloom time: Summer to fall.
  • Growing tips: Thrives in sunny, well-drained areas; prune heavily in late winter.

8. Columbine (Aquilegia canadensis)

Columbine is a charming wildflower with delicate red and yellow flowers shaped like tiny drooping trumpets, accessible to hummingbirds.

  • Flower shape: Nodding, spurred tubular flowers.
  • Bloom time: Spring to early summer.
  • Growing tips: Prefers partial shade and well-drained soil; easy to grow and naturalize.

9. Fuchsia (Fuchsia magellanica)

Fuchsias are loved for their elegant, pendulous flowers that often come in vibrant red and purple combinations. These shade-loving plants can brighten up shady garden spots.

  • Flower shape: Hanging, tubular flowers.
  • Bloom time: Summer to fall.
  • Growing tips: Needs consistent moisture and some protection from extreme sun.

10. Penstemon Digitalis (‘Husker Red’)

This hybrid variety is admired for its deep red foliage and white tubular flowers that hummingbirds adore, providing a striking mix of color and wildlife appeal.

  • Flower shape: Tubular clusters.
  • Bloom time: Late spring to early summer.
  • Growing tips: Thrives in full sun to part shade and well-drained soil.

Caring for Your Hummingbird Garden

To maximize the chances of your garden becoming a hummingbird haven, consider these tips:

Ensure Continuous Blooming

Plant a variety of species that bloom at different times so there’s always nectar available from early spring to late fall. This provides a reliable food source that keeps hummingbirds coming back season after season.

Avoid Pesticides

Pesticides harm not only insects but also the small hummingbirds that feed on them occasionally. Maintain a chemical-free environment to keep your garden healthy and welcoming.

Provide Safe Perches and Nesting Sites

Hummingbirds appreciate small branches or thin wires where they can rest and survey their territory. Incorporate native shrubs and small trees to provide natural shelter and potential nesting spots.

Supplement with Feeders

Although the focus here is on plants, hummingbird feeders filled with sugar water can complement your garden, especially during migration seasons when natural nectar might be scarce.

Conclusion

Designing a garden that attracts hummingbirds can be a delightful and rewarding undertaking. By selecting the right mix of brightly colored, nectar-rich plants such as bee balm, trumpet vine, salvia, and cardinal flower, you set the stage for these remarkable birds to visit regularly.

Beyond their beauty, adding hummingbird-friendly plants enhances biodiversity and strengthens pollination networks in your backyard. With patience and thoughtful planting, you’ll soon find yourself immersed in the magical dance of hummingbirds, making your garden a vibrant, living tapestry of nature’s wonders.
