Climatology of the Coral Reefs: Saving Earth's Underwater Rainforests


Coral reefs are the rainforests of the sea, often referred to as the “Navy Blue of Earth,” possessing an extraordinary vibrancy and richness in biodiversity. As critical components of marine ecosystems, these underwater paradises provide several ecological services as well. Their sheer immensity is astounding, covering less than 0.1% of the ocean floor but supporting about 25% of all marine species. Unfortunately, coral reefs face an existential crisis due to climate change, resulting in devastating consequences for marine life and coastal communities. Saving these underwater rainforests is more than an environmental task; it’s a humanitarian mission.

Coral Bleaching: A Symptom of a Warming Planet

The maximum surface temperature for coral reef survival is around 32°C (90°F). When sea temperatures exceed this, the symbiotic algae called zooxanthellae within coral polyps get stressed, causing them to dissipate. This phenomenon, known as coral bleaching, leads to damaged corals, which then become susceptible to diseases. In the last 30 years, coral bleaching events have risen in frequency and intensity, with most instances linked to El Niño events and rising ocean temperatures.

Coral reefs can also experience bleaching from excessive sunlight, pollution, and ocean acidification caused by the absorption of excess atmospheric CO2. Ocean acidification impedes the ability of corals to build their calcium carbonate skeletons, making them weaker and more susceptible to breakage from waves and storms.

The Great Barrier Reef, an underwater wonder and contributor to global tourism, has recently been in the news with alarming headlines like “Great Barrier Reef Suffers Worst Coral Bleaching Event on Record.” This comes after the devastating 2016-2017 event, which impacted over 90% of the reef’s northern parts. The long-term effects of these recurrent bleaching events become evident when we consider that it can take 10-30 years for severely damaged reefs to recover, if ever.

Climatology and Coral Reef Conservation Strategies

While it’s essential to reduce greenhouse gas emissions to slow down global warming, immediate and localized efforts on the ground are equally important. Coral species show potential for acclimatization to warmer waters or the ability to adapt to higher temperatures. This facet is being researched, and the identification of ‘super corals’ is an aspect of mitigation.

Stress Testing Coral Reefs

One strategy, known as “Stress Testing,” involves rehabilitating environmentally stable coral reefs to assess and adapt to higher temperatures and other stressors. These reefs could potentially foster the survival of a broader range of coral species, providing a hedge against future change.

Coral Restoration via Human Innovation

Another innovative method involves the use of pioneering coral propagation techniques, such as the Coral Restoration Foundation’s artificial nursery system. Here, coral fragments or ‘cuttings’ are regrown in underwater nurseries for one year before being outplanted to degraded reefs. Scientists have also developed ‘genetic engineering’ or ‘assisted evolution’ techniques, aiming to boost the heat tolerance of corals.

Socio-Economic Incentives for Conservation

Marine protected areas, or MPAs, are an effective tool for safeguarding biodiversity and marine resources. They prevent the destructive practices, like overfishing and unsustainable tourism. MPAs can garner support from local communities through socio-economic benefits, such as new jobs in sustainable tourism, improved fishing stocks, and protection of coastal infrastructure.

For coral reefs to survive against the backdrop of global climate change, a multifaceted approach is necessary. The more sustainable we are with our use of natural resources, the more sustainable and resilient our ecosystems will be. The health of coral reefs doesn’t just depend on environmental initiatives. It depends on climate policies, energy choices, and even personal actions. After all, protecting the underwater rainforests of our planet is protecting the ecosystems that secure our planet’s future - and ours. The push for more sustainable practices, both immediate and long-term, is not just an environmental battle but a human one.
