Ichthyology in Popular Culture: Fish in Movies and Literature


Fish have long captured the human imagination. From their mysterious, otherworldly habitats to their striking appearances and behaviors, fish have become prominent symbols and characters in movies and literature. Whether as central figures or as creatures in the background, fish hold a special place in popular culture. This post delves into the portrayal of fish in movies and literature, examining how ichthyology— the scientific study of fish—has influenced storytelling and the broader cultural narrative.

The Fascination with Fish in Human Culture

Fish have been part of human culture for centuries, playing important roles in mythologies, religious stories, and as symbols of the unknown. The oceans and seas, where most fish live, have long been associated with mystery, and fish often embody elements of this mystery. For example, the Bible features the story of Jonah and the whale, which highlights the symbolic significance of aquatic creatures in religious and moral tales.

In modern times, the portrayal of fish has evolved, often blending scientific accuracy with imaginative, fantastical elements. The rich diversity of fish species, ranging from small, colorful tropical species to the gigantic, fearsome predators of the deep ocean, offers a wide array of possibilities for storytellers.

Fish in Cinema: From Monsters to Metaphors

Fish have appeared in many films, often taking on symbolic or metaphorical meanings. One of the most famous fish in cinema is the shark, notably in Steven Spielberg’s Jaws (1975). The film cemented the shark’s place in popular culture as a symbol of danger, fear, and the primal forces of nature. Despite its portrayal as a villainous creature, the shark in Jaws also represents humanity’s battle with the natural world—an ongoing theme in movies about the sea.

The depiction of fish in films isn’t always about fear. In Pixar’s Finding Nemo (2003), the fish serve as sympathetic characters with personalities, exploring themes of family, friendship, and perseverance. Finding Nemo presents a humorous, touching story about a clownfish named Marlin who embarks on a journey to rescue his son. The film also introduced many viewers to the diverse world of fish, showing various species in their natural ocean habitats.

Fish also play prominent roles in animated films that educate as well as entertain. For instance, The Little Mermaid (1989) tells the story of Ariel, a mermaid who longs to live on land. Ariel’s underwater world is filled with a variety of fish, each adding richness to the film’s setting. The fish in the movie also embody aspects of freedom and the untapped beauty of the ocean world.

In addition to animated films, horror and thriller genres have frequently explored the eerie potential of fish and other sea creatures. In movies like Piranha (1978) and its subsequent remakes, the piranha—small but terrifying fish—becomes the antagonist. These films lean into the fear of aquatic predators, drawing on the inherent fear people have of the unknown depths of the sea.

Fish in Literature: Metaphors and Symbolism

In literature, fish have held a symbolic and metaphoric role for centuries. In Herman Melville’s Moby-Dick (1851), the white whale, though not technically a fish, symbolizes the vast, uncontrollable forces of nature. The novel is steeped in ichthyological references, with its detailed descriptions of whale hunting and the deep, mysterious sea. The ocean itself becomes a character, a space where fish and humans clash in a battle for dominance and understanding. Moby-Dick invites readers to reflect on humanity’s obsession with control and mastery over nature, using the sea and its creatures as symbols of mystery and terror.

In The Old Man and the Sea (1952), Ernest Hemingway presents a simpler, yet equally profound, interaction between humans and fish. Santiago, the protagonist, is an old fisherman who battles with a giant marlin for three days at sea. The marlin, though not as mysterious or dangerous as the white whale, becomes a symbol of Santiago’s endurance and struggle against nature’s unforgiving forces. Hemingway’s novella is often interpreted as an allegory for human perseverance and the inevitable confrontation between human fragility and nature’s grandeur.

Fish are also often used as symbols of fertility, transformation, and spiritual renewal in literature. The ancient myth of the “fish-god,” seen in cultures such as Mesopotamian and Greek traditions, finds echoes in modern literature. In The Grapes of Wrath (1939), John Steinbeck includes fish as symbolic of resilience and survival. Fish, like the people who struggle to survive in the novel, face challenges and hardships that test their endurance.

One of the most famous examples of a fish being used symbolically is the story of Pinocchio by Carlo Collodi. Although Pinocchio is often associated with his transformation from puppet to boy, the story also features a fish—the giant fish that swallows both Pinocchio and Geppetto. The fish in this tale symbolizes a journey through danger and the hope for redemption. Pinocchio’s story is one of self-realization and growth, with the fish representing the trials one must face before achieving personal transformation.

Fish as Characters in Modern Fiction

Contemporary fiction continues to feature fish as characters in more direct and nuanced ways. In the realm of magical realism, for instance, fish appear as magical beings or as representations of deeper truths. In Life of Pi (2001) by Yann Martel, the protagonist Pi is stranded on a lifeboat in the Pacific Ocean with a Bengal tiger and a variety of fish. These fish play an important role in the survival story, providing sustenance and representing Pi’s deep connection to nature. As the story unfolds, the fish serve as a reminder of the fragility and resilience of life, just as Pi himself learns about faith and survival.

Fish have also appeared in dystopian fiction. In the novel The Sea Change (1996) by A. S. Byatt, the sea and its creatures, including fish, are linked to a woman’s personal transformation. The protagonist, a scientist, undergoes a profound emotional and psychological change, which is symbolically connected to the ocean and its living creatures. The fish in the story become representations of purity, renewal, and the cyclical nature of existence.

In The Water Dancer (2019) by Ta-Nehisi Coates, fish, specifically the act of fishing, symbolizes the act of liberation. The protagonist, who can manipulate water, learns to navigate through life and his struggles with the legacy of slavery. Fish in this story represent both the physical and metaphorical means of freedom, connecting with themes of ancestry, resilience, and survival.

Real-World Inspiration: The Influence of Ichthyology on Fiction

Many writers and filmmakers have drawn inspiration from real-world ichthyology, infusing their works with accurate depictions of fish species and their behaviors. The field of ichthyology, which involves the study of fish, has contributed a wealth of knowledge about fish that often shapes how they are represented in popular culture.

For example, Jacques Cousteau’s underwater documentaries, starting with The Silent World (1956), greatly influenced how fish and marine life are depicted in movies and documentaries. Cousteau’s groundbreaking work in ocean exploration introduced millions of people to the wonders of aquatic life, fostering a deeper understanding of the natural world. His influence can be seen in films like The Blue Planet (2001), which offer a cinematic glimpse into the diversity of fish species and their ecological roles.

Moreover, biologists and scientists continue to inspire storytellers with their discoveries of new and unusual fish species. The bizarre, deep-sea creatures that populate the deepest parts of the ocean often serve as inspiration for science fiction and horror films. Fish like the anglerfish, with its glowing lure, and the immortal jellyfish, capable of reverting to its juvenile form, spark the imagination of writers and filmmakers alike.

Conclusion

The portrayal of fish in movies and literature reflects the multifaceted relationship humans have with these creatures. Whether as symbols of mystery and power, as central characters, or as representations of transformation and survival, fish continue to capture our imagination in both subtle and profound ways. Through the lens of ichthyology, fish are no longer just creatures of the sea but rich, meaningful elements of storytelling. From classic literature to modern films, the fish remains a powerful metaphor for the struggles and triumphs of humanity, inviting us to reflect on our own journey through the oceans of life.
