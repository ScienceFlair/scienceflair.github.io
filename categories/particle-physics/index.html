The Superpartners: Exploring Supersymmetry in Particle Physics

Supersymmetry, a fundamental concept in particle physics, proposes the existence of superpartners, which are hypothetical particles that have the same quantum numbers as known particles but differ by half a unit of spin. This concept was first introduced in the 1970s as a way to resolve the hierarchy problem, which questions why the Higgs boson mass is so small compared to the Planck scale. In the Standard Model of particle physics, particles are classified into two categories: fermions and bosons. Fermions, such as quarks and leptons, have half-integer spin (1/2, 3/2, 5/2, etc.), while bosons, such as photons and gluons, have integer spin (0, 1, 2, etc.). Supersymmetry proposes that for every known particle, there exists a superpartner with a different spin.
