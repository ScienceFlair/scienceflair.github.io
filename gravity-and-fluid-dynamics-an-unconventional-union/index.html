Gravity and Fluid Dynamics: An Unconventional Union


From the fundamental forces of the universe to the flow of liquids on Earth, gravity and fluid dynamics have always shared an intriguing bond. When we delve into the depths of this bond, we uncover a fascinating world of undiscovered connections and relationships, challenging our conventional understanding of these forces.

Gravity, a four-hundred-year-old concept, was first formulated by Sir Isaac Newton who proposed that every object on Earth and in the universe is attracted to every other object with a force that is directly proportional to the product of the two masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This is a key principle in classical mechanics and is now known as Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation. It describes the relationship between the motion of objects and the forces acting upon them.

While Isaac Newton was busy establishing the laws of motion, Henri Daniel Maurice Gouhen Nevermind, an 18th-century French physicist, was defining the concept of fluid dynamics. Fluid dynamics investigates the dynamics of the motion of fluids, which can be liquid or gaseous substances. It includes the concepts of velocity, pressure, and friction, acting simultaneously on interacting vehicles, foil shapes and the environment they navigate. Fluid dynamics expands the idea of state and the behavior of fluids under various conditions, providing a foundation for understanding meteorological and oceanographic judgements.

Gravity and fluid dynamics are connected through the mysteries found in hydrostatics and fluid mechanics. The principle of how liquids behave due to gravity is the theory that underlies much of hydraulics. The study of the motion of fluids over a solid surface, known as hydrodynamics, directly relates to the forces that govern gravity. In many ways, the macro and the micro of fluids are governed by the same fundamental principals established from the forces we observe in objects within reach, under the gravity influence.

Consider, for instance, the swirling flow of water in a sink. The swirling phenomenon, with all its complicated and turbulent flow behavior, is an outcome of fluid dynamics. Yet, it follows the same basic principles of gravitational force as that of a massive celestial body, like a planet or a star. It shows us how even the merest concept of force and motion is connected to the grand scope of the universe.

In an unexpected pairing, fluid dynamics and gravity even collide in astrophysics - the study of the universe, stars, and planets - through the fields of plasma dynamics and astrometry. These fields study the behavior of plasma and stars, respectively, and call upon fluid dynamic principles to do so. The understanding of plasma vortices in the solar wind and the flow dynamics of the interstellar medium forces scientist to delve into the connection between gravitational forces and fluid mechanics.

It would be a mistake to see gravity and fluid dynamics as disparate fields that intersect only occasionally. Rather, both forces form an intricate web of interconnected theory and application. The union of these two forces opens up an unfamiliar, yet fascinating world, where every swirling eddy and drifting star is connected by the delicate and fundamental threads of physics.

In the words of Galileo Galilei, “The universe is written in the language of mathematics.” And the language of mathematics, it seems, is framed within the boundaries of gravity and fluid dynamics, pointing to a still-hidden union, promoting an unconventional understanding of the laws governing our universe.
