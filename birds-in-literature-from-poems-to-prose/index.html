Birds in Literature: From Poems to Prose


Birds have been a source of fascination for humans for as long as we have kept records. These fascinating creatures have made their way into countless pieces of literature throughout history, spanning all genres from poetry to prose. Here, we explore some notable examples of avian appearances in literature.

Owls in Poetry

One of the earliest examples of birds in literature can be traced back to ancient civilizations. In Greek mythology, Athena, the goddess of wisdom, is often depicted with an owl as her symbol. The association between wise owls and wisdom continued through time, and British poet T.S. Eliot’s “The Naming of Cats” in his play “Cats” paid homage to this tradition in the famous line, “The Naming of Cats is a Highbrow Game…”

In more modern times, Emily Dickinson’s poetry features numerous birds. In one of her most well-known poems, “Because I could not stop for Death,” Dickinson describes Death as a “gentleman” who, along with a “Horse,” takes her on a mysterious journey through life. Dickinson often references birds throughout her oeuvre, perhaps most notably in “I hear in thatvelocity,” where she writes, “Enigma of the brook in pond—/ This:message—is not confined—To bird or branch,—or bough.”

Birds in Prose – Arthurian Tales

The legendary King Arthur and his court were often surrounded by a variety of birds in the countless tales that portrayed their adventures. The Lancelyn Green version of the “Grael,” one of the graded adventures of Percival, involves the eponymous hero being chased by a falcon. This bird represents the phoenix, and the achievement of this quest demonstrates Percival’s true wisdom, aligning him with the wise Andornés of the past.

Shakespeare’s Feathers – The Bard’s Love for Birds

William Shakespeare, synonymous with English literature, frequently incorporte birds into his works, expressing their symbolism to convey themes like death, rebirth, and the fleeting nature of life. “As You Like It,” for instance, features one of the best-known references to birds in literature. Jacques, a melancholic character, contemplates the story of a shepherd who compares life and love to a “most gay and palpable rumoured falsehood.” Jaques then remarks, “And like a moody Jack-daw, pecking lustily at every feather he meets, he swallows ’till he swallows down pure love and then, like a gallant<|reserved_special_token_270|> gallant, he comes the wren.”

The variety of avian appearances in literature may seem endless, but through the lens of history, we see that these colorful creations have inspired poets, playwrights, and novelists alike. From the mystique of the phoenix in Arthurian tales to the wit of a clever mosquito in Beatrix Potter’s world, our fascination with birds has continued to evolve over time. As we continue to add stories to the canon, birds will remain prominent examples of the boundless and beautiful nature of literature.
