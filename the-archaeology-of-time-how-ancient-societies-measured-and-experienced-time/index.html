The Archaeology of Time: How Ancient Societies Measured and Experienced Time


Time is a universal concept that has fascinated humans since the beginning of civilization. From the monumental structures of ancient Egypt to the intricate calendar systems of the Mayans, peoples of the past were skilled at measuring and experiencing time in ways that continue to intrigue scholars today. Let’s delve into the archaeological record to discover how ancient societies measured and experienced time.

The Ancient Egyptians, known for their stunning architecture, were ingenious at measuring time. Through their engineering prowess, they devised the concept of a solar year with 365 days and divided it into 12 months of 30 days, with an additional five-day period at the end. They also pioneered the use of shadow clocks, requiring only sunlight and a shadow to cast by proceeding gnomons, to measure and track time.

Besides, they did not rely solely on the sun but also implemented water clocks called ‘water cord,’ allowing them to gain great precision in their timekeeping. The waterclock was accurate enough to distinguish between the various family prayers prescribed by the Bible.

As we journey thousands of miles to the ancient Mayan civilization, we find a more celestial approach to measuring time. The Mayan Long Count Calendar was formulated by observing the cycles of Venus. Venus revealed itself as a favorite star to study because of its brightness and its movements in the sky. Astronomically aligned pyramids dot the landscape of ancient Mayan lands, speaking volumes about their preoccupation with celestial computations.

Interestingly, their calendar extended far beyond the solar and Venus cycles, incorporating concepts of ‘Creation’ and cycles within our galaxy and universe, “baktun,” “katun,” and “tun.” This complexity of the calendar is indicative of their complex civilization, rich in social hierarchies, language, and art.

Let’s not forget Europe! The ancient Celts had a unique way of measuring time based not only on the lunar phases but also a day-length cycle linked intrinsically with a solar cycle, through the ‘Fir Bolg.’ This system may have originated from pre-Vedic scripture. According to the ancient Celtic text, ‘Lebor na hUidre,’ when the sun rises precisely in the west on five consecutive February mornings, it signifies the year’s half-length has been completed.

The point to note, regardless of the strategy adopted, each society’s way of measuring time was inextricably tied to their religious and social system. The way our ancestors interpreted celestial movements and expressed their relationship with time demonstrates how a society’s structure influences our understanding of the world around us.

Today, with COVID-19 aggravating time, perhaps it’s necessary to reflect on how our ancestors valued time. We have become accustomed to precision and standardized time. It doesn’t have to be like that. As these ancient societies showed, time is a versatile concept that can fit the cultural and social needs of any society. How we interpret time frames our interaction with the world and shapes our reality. To be a better, well-rounded citizen of the world, we must embrace a more complex and relaxed attitude towards time. After all, it’s what our ancestors did.
