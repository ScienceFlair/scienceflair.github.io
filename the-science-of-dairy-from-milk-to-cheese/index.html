The Science of Dairy: From Milk to Cheese


Everyone’s favorite dessert and versatile food group, dairy, is not only a tasteful delight but a scientific marvel. Let’s delve into the fascinating world of milk and cheese, their production processes, and the reasons we love them so.

Milk: The Wonder Drink

Milk is a powerhouse of nutrition. It offers vitamins like A, D, and B12, minerals such as calcium and potassium, and a nutrient called whey protein. Whey protein has remarkable properties, often used in sports supplements due to its potential to increase muscle mass and decrease exercise recovery time.

Milk is a complex liquid, composed of water, fat, proteins (casein and whey), lactose, minerals, enzymes, vitamins, and other milk solids. The balance in milk composition varies among species. Cow milk is most commonly consumed; however, sheep, goat, and buffalo milk are also utilized across the world.

Milk production involves milking animals, typically cows or goats, at regular intervals. The quality of milk mainly depends on factors such as animal feed, genetics, and the environment they live in. From Milk to Cheese: A Journey

The process of turning milk into cheese is rooted in ancient practices. Cheesemaking begins with the coagulation of milk proteins. However, modern processes involve chemical coagulants like citric acid and calcium chloride.

The variety of cheese available is vast, primarily due to differences in milk source (cow, ewe, goat, etc.), coagulant choice, and subsequent processing. These variations create distinct flavors and textures.

Once the milk has curdled, the curds are drained and pressed to remove whey, a watery liquid byproduct. The remaining solid curds are molded into the desired cheese shape and, depending on the cheese type, left to age for a variable amount of time.

The Art and Science of Cheesemaking

Cheesemaking is a blend of art and science. It requires a deep understanding of milk composition and the role of various factors during cheesemaking. Temperature, pH levels, and the choice of coagulant significantly affect the final product, creating a unique taste.

In recent years, artisan cheesemakers have gained popularity, trading standard mass-produced cheeses for small-batch, niche varieties. Artisan cheesemakers prioritize unique aged tastes and strive for the perfect balance of flavor, texture, and presentation.
