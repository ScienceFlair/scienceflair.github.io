Food Waste Heroes: A Closer Look at the Botany of Composting

To begin with, composting is primarily the process of decomposing organic material, such as food scraps, leaves, and straw, into nutrient-rich soil. When it comes to food waste, approximately 1.3 billion tons of it is generated each year, with 1.6 to 2.5 billion tons of edible crops not being harvested due to various reasons. These organic resources provide a wealth of minerals and vitamins for plants, and the process works as a symbiotic relationship that aids in the growth and flourishing of greenery, both in our gardens and forests.

The art of composting is an intricate process that requires patience, understanding, and practice. The first step starts with gathering what we have charmingly called “brown” and “green” materials. “Brown” materials refer to carbon-rich materials, such as dried leaves, branches, and paper, while “green” includes nitrogen-rich items like food scraps, grass clippings, and coffee grounds. To create an effective compost system, you need an equal ratio of these two materials, allowing them to compost harmoniously and efficiently.

Following the blending of brown and green materials, oxygen plays a vital role in facilitating the decomposition process. A good way to introduce air is by turning the compost pile every few weeks. This aeration promotes the microbial activity, speeding up the process and improving the final quality of the soil.

After several months, the compost pile will transform into a dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling material — your very own organic super soil. Rich in nutrients, it can be used to improve soil structure, promote plant growth, and enhance crop yields.
