The Link Between Fracking and Induced Seismicity


Hydraulic fracturing, commonly referred to as “fracking,” has become a pivotal part of energy production over the past few decades. This technique, which involves injecting high-pressure fluid into underground rock formations to extract oil and natural gas, revolutionized energy accessibility and reshaped global energy markets. However, as its adoption has expanded, so too have concerns about its environmental and geophysical impacts. Among these concerns, one area that has received increasing attention is its connection to induced seismicity — human-caused earthquakes.

While earthquakes are typically considered natural phenomena caused by tectonic plate movement, fracking-related processes such as fluid injection and wastewater disposal are capable of altering the stress balance deep within the earth’s crust. These alterations have, in some cases, been linked to unexpected seismic events in regions with little to no previous history of significant earthquake activity. This article delves into the science behind induced seismicity, the role of fracking-related activities, and the ongoing discussions surrounding this complex issue.

Understanding Induced Seismicity: A Primer

Before diving into the specifics of fracking, it is important to understand what induced seismicity encompasses. Induced seismicity refers to seismic events triggered by human activities, such as mining, dam construction, geothermal energy extraction, and, most notably in recent times, activities associated with oil and gas production, including hydraulic fracturing and wastewater injection.

The Earth’s crust is in a state of balance or “tectonic equilibrium,” held together by stresses that are distributed along faults and rock formations. Any human intervention that alters this stress balance has the potential to trigger movement along existing fault lines or create new fractures, leading to seismic activity. The degree of this activity can range from small, imperceptible tremors to more concerning earthquakes capable of causing structural damage.

When hydraulic fracturing emerged as a method of resource extraction, the initial belief was that the seismic risk posed by the technique itself was minimal. However, as larger datasets and more advanced geophysical monitoring became available, a clearer picture of fracking’s relationship with induced seismicity began to emerge.

How Hydraulic Fracturing Contributes to Induced Seismicity

Fracking-related seismicity is generally associated with two key processes: the fracking procedure itself and the disposal of wastewater generated during the process. Each of these mechanisms influences the subsurface environment in distinct ways.

1. Hydraulic Fracturing (Injection of Fracturing Fluids)

During a fracking operation, pressurized fluid is injected into rock formations, creating fractures that allow trapped hydrocarbons to flow to the surface. This injection process occurs over extremely short durations and is highly localized. As a result, the seismic events directly triggered by hydraulic fracturing tend to be small. These events, known as “microseismic events,” are typically measured at magnitudes below 2.0 on the Richter scale and are rarely felt at the surface.

Nonetheless, in rare cases, the fluid pressure introduced during the process can reactivate dormant faults, causing more significant seismic events. For example, several notable cases in regions such as Alberta, Canada, and the United States’ shale basins have demonstrated that hydraulic fracturing can trigger seismic activity above 4.0 in magnitude. While these events remain the exception rather than the norm, they have highlighted the potential for larger-scale disruptions in tectonically complex regions.

2. Wastewater Injection and Deep-Well Disposal

Perhaps the most significant contributor to fracking-induced seismicity is not the fracturing process itself but the disposal of the massive volumes of produced water and wastewater associated with oil and gas operations. This wastewater, often a mix of the injected fracking fluid and naturally occurring brine, is frequently disposed of in deep underground wells.

The prolonged and high-volume injection of wastewater into these disposal wells can increase pore pressure in rock formations. This pressure can reduce the friction along preexisting fault lines, making faults more likely to slip and trigger earthquakes. While wastewater injection sites are geographically distinct from fracking wells, they are an integral part of the hydraulic fracturing lifecycle and contribute significantly to seismic risk in active basins.

Historical data have shown a strong correlation between the increased frequency of medium- to large-scale earthquakes in areas like Oklahoma and expanded wastewater injection activities. In fact, Oklahoma, once known for its low seismicity, has experienced more magnitude 3.0 earthquakes in recent years than California, a state famously crisscrossed by natural fault zones.

Case Studies Highlighting Fracking-Induced Seismicity

A detailed look at specific examples of regions affected by fracking-related induced seismicity paints a complex picture of local geology, operational practices, and regulatory oversight.

Oklahoma, United States

Perhaps the most well-documented case of induced seismicity from fracking-related activity comes from Oklahoma. Between the years 2009 and 2016, the state experienced an exponential increase in earthquake activity. This surge coincided with a sharp rise in wastewater injection volumes at underground disposal wells. One of the most prominent events, a magnitude 5.8 earthquake in 2016 near Pawnee, Oklahoma, was directly linked to wastewater injection, underscoring the significant risk posed by improper disposal practices.

Alberta and British Columbia, Canada

In western Canada, studies have identified instances of moderate earthquakes directly associated with hydraulic fracturing operations, rather than wastewater disposal. The Montney Formation in British Columbia, a region with active shale gas development, has experienced several seismic events of concern. These cases emphasize the potential for direct fracking-induced earthquakes, even though these events remain relatively rare.

Netherlands

While not tied to hydraulic fracturing, the Netherlands provides an instructive example of how subsidence and pressure changes associated with natural gas extraction can lead to widespread seismicity. The Groningen gas field, Europe’s largest natural gas field, has experienced hundreds of earthquakes linked to pressure depletion from gas extraction. This case highlights the broader principle that altering subsurface pressures — whether through production or injection — carries seismic implications.

Mitigating and Managing the Risks

As evidence linking fracking and induced seismicity has grown, governments, regulators, and industry stakeholders have taken steps to understand, mitigate, and manage the associated risks. These efforts typically fall into three main areas:

  1. Monitoring and Data Collection
    Advanced seismic monitoring networks have become key tools in assessing and mitigating seismic risks. By tracking small-scale seismic activity, operators and regulators can identify patterns and adjust practices to reduce the likelihood of larger events.

  2. Regulatory Action
    Many regions have established regulatory frameworks to limit induced seismic risks. These include caps on injection volumes, restrictions on wastewater well density, and mandatory seismic risk assessments prior to commencing operations. “Traffic light” systems, which require operators to halt or modify operations in response to seismic events, have also been widely adopted in places like the United Kingdom and Canada.

  3. Technological Innovations
    Innovations aimed at reducing wastewater production, improving water recycling, and refining hydraulic fracturing procedures continue to provide opportunities to mitigate seismic risks. Alternative approaches, such as carbon capture and storage (CCS) solutions, are also being explored to address subsurface pressure management.

Charting a Path Forward

The connection between fracking and induced seismicity underscores the broader challenges of balancing energy development with environmental and public safety. While hydraulic fracturing has played a transformative role in meeting global energy demands and reducing dependency on higher-emission fuels like coal, its unintended consequences require thoughtful examination.

Scientific research, robust policy frameworks, and transparent industry practices will remain essential to mitigating seismic risks while advancing energy production. By understanding the causes of induced seismicity and committing to responsible resource development, the industry has an opportunity to align economic priorities with environmental stewardship.

As communities, scientists, and policymakers collaborate to manage the seismic risks associated with hydraulic fracturing, the ultimate goal should remain clear: to foster sustainable energy practices that minimize harm and maximize benefits for future generations. The lessons learned from ongoing studies of fracking-related seismicity will not only inform safer practices within the oil and gas sector but may also have lasting implications for how humanity interacts with the Earth’s complex geophysical systems.
