What's Brewing in Your Cup: Climatology and the Science of Coffee


The humble coffee bean has been the fuel behind countless mornings, delivering people from the grasp of sleep and into the awaiting embrace of their daily routines. But what if I told you that the very same beans that help us kick-start our day are also intrinsically linked to the complex and fascinating world of climatology? From the cultivation of coffee plants to the roasting and brewing process, climatic conditions have a profound influence on the flavor and quality of our morning cup of joe. This Blog post promises to be your guide into the riveting world of coffee, as we explore the phenomenon of climatology and its deeply intertwined relationship with the world’s most cherished beverage.

Cultivating Coffee: The Sweet Spot of Climate

At the heart of the coffee-making process lies its humble beginnings in fertile soil and a climate that strikes a delicate balance between chilly nights and warm days. Coffee plants, specifically the Coffea arabica and Coffea robusta species, thrive in regions where temperatures range between 15-24 degrees Celsius during the day and 12-17 degrees Celsius at night. Additionally, an abundance of rainfall is crucial, with around 1,000-2,000 millimeters of precipitation annually, evenly spread throughout the year.

The world’s premier coffee-growing countries, including countries like Brazil, Colombia, Ethiopia, and Indonesia, boast climatic conditions that fulfill these exacting requirements. From the volcanic terrain of Chiang Mai in Northern Thailand to the lush, verdant slopes of Java in Indonesia, these regions excel in producing coffee cherries with the right balance of sugar, acidity, and bitterness – the very elements that micro-roasters delicately coax into your steaming cup.

The Impact of Climate Change on Coffee Production

As the world grapples with the impending threats posed by climate change, coffee farmers are experiencing the harsh realities of this planet-wide challenge firsthand. Coffee plants are exceptionally sensitive to fluctuations in temperature and rainfall patterns. Consequently, a higher risk of frost, droughts, and prolonged periods of intense heat are causing significant issues for coffee farmers worldwide.

Consider the significantly influential work of Dr. Aaron Davis, a coffee researcher at the Royal Botanic Gardens in Kew, United Kingdom. In his study, Davis examined the effects of global warming in coffee-growing regions and found a disturbing trend: for every degree Celsius above 24 degrees Celsius, coffee yield is projected to drop by approximately 3.7%. To put that into perspective, a 3-degree Celsius rise in temperature (which was the upper limit agreed upon by the Paris Climate Accord) would inevitably lead to lower coffee production rates, increasing the demand for less fertile land and exacerbating the already existing problem of deforestation in these regions.

Climatology and Coffee Roasting: The Hotter, the Sweeter?

Arguably, the final frontier of the coffee-climatology relationship lies within the realm of roasting. While the uninitiated may not immediately comprehend the extent to which temperature affects coffee’s flavor profile, roasters understand that roasting coffee in too high of a heat can lead to “scorching,” which imparts a bitter flavor and subsequently diminishes the product’s overall quality.

In recent years, coffee roasters have begun leveraging this understanding to intentionally explore and promote the creation of different flavor profiles while always keeping the environmental impact under consideration. The art of roasting has become a fascinating dance, balancing the nuances of temperature and time in an effort to highlight and preserve the beans’ inherent flavors.

The interplay between climatology and coffee is both complex and fascinating - an intricate symphony, blending science and tradition, artistry and sustainability, and the very quest for the perfect cup. With each brew, let’s appreciate and embrace the unique, moonlit nights and cascading rainfalls that gave birth to our beloved morning cup – a small, yet significant reminder of the incredible impact climatology has on the world we inhabit, the beans that fuel us, and the ripples they create.

To sum it up, an equally crucial lesson derived from our journey through the world of coffee and climatology is to never underestimate the importance of our planet’s immeasurable balance and the delicate threads that connect every element within it. Each sip of coffee is a testament to the extraordinary symphony of nature and the work of our tireless coffee-loving kith and kin around the world.
