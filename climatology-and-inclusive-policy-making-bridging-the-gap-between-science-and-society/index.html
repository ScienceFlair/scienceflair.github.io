Climatology and Inclusive Policy-making: Bridging the Gap Between Science and Society


The climate crisis is not a debate that can be sparked on by mere social media interactions or one that could wait for years to be acknowledged. It is an immediate and pressing threat to the planet and all its denizens. And yet, often, the policies surrounding climate change do not align with the current scientific consensus, leaving us pondering the question: How can we bridge the gap between climatology – the scientific study of climate – and policy-making, enabling inclusive participation and paving the way for meaningful dialogue between science, policy, and society?

In recent times, inclusiveness has become a buzzword. But its role in climatology extends far beyond a mere phase or passing trend. The concept embodies the core of fostering equitable climate policies which cater to the diverse needs of a myriad of stakeholders: from governments and corporations to scientists, activists, and the everyday folks living on the frontlines of climate change. Including a wider spectrum of voices in the conversation could serve as stepping stones for bridging the gap between science and society.

The genesis of this divergence between climate science and policy-making can be traced to our education system. When we fail to emphasize critical thinking and environmental awareness in favor of rote learning, the foundations of our literacy in climate change are likely to give way during times of essential action. Climate education can be the silver lining. By incorporating environmentally-grounded subjects into curriculums at all levels, students can be equipped with a foundational understanding of climate and its implications. Furthermore, diverse pedagogical methods, from gamification to social learning, prepare students to engage with the subject more meaningfully and empathetically.

Building on this foundation, the next point of attention should be ‘Science Diplomacy’. Through channels of international cooperation, countries can work collectively to pool resources and expertise to enhance climate mitigation and adaptation initiatives while addressing an array of other pressing issues. The UNFCCC and The Paris Agreement present two prime examples of platforms for enabling just such collaborative efforts in combating climate change alongside ensuring participation from developing countries.

The third plank of action could be ‘Public Engagement and Activism’. Although governments are ultimately responsible for implementing policies, instances from history have shown that citizens can drive change through activism. Environmental NGOs and social movements like Fridays for Future serve as beacons of hope – motivated, engaged, and able to influence policy agendas.

Moreover, incorporating inclusive policy-making into educational curriculums and facilitating effective channels of communication between scientists, politicians, activists, and everyday people could help dissipate the bureaucratic jargon. Common language in policy documentation, for example, could be crucial to achieving this. It means that anyone with an interest in the subject could understand what’s being debated, obscuring neither the intent nor the outcome of the proposed laws.

The question then remains, how do we uphold such rhetoric? Continuously, striving towards transparency, accountability, and justice in climate policymaking could be one of the ways. What’s more, integrating deliberative mechanisms like citizen assemblies, town-hall meetings, or consensus conferences into the policy-making process can bring about a semblance of social licence. These measures imbue the stakeholders with a sense of ownership, enabling them to hold governments and decision-makers accountable for their endeavours.

In essence, inclusive policy-making in climatology paves the way for a world that breathes harmoniously, where science finds its voice, society collaborates, and privilege no longer plays a part in decisions that press for the future of our planet. Bridging the gap between science and society is by no means a simple feat.

However, by truly understanding the gravity of climate change, weaving climate education across all strata of society, using diplomacy to work collectively, fostering public engagement, breaking down the jargon, and democratising policy-making, we can lay the foundations for inclusive climate policies. It is only through such bridging that we strengthen our collective resolve to fight climate change and strike a harmonious balance that honours our planet long into the future.
