The Impact of Climate Change on Aquatic Ecosystems


Climate change is an increasingly pressing issue for not only human societies, but also for the vast array of wildlife inhabiting our planet. One area that is particularly vulnerable and impacted by these changes is aquatic ecosystems. Marine and freshwater habitats face direct and indirect consequences as a result of the warming of our planet, leading to consequences that echo down the food chain and dramatically affect the planet’s life support systems.

Temperatures within our oceans and lakes are rising at a rate much faster than the rest of the planet. This dramatic increase in temperature is resulting in several profound changes to our aquatic environments and the lifeforms that depend on them. Several impacts of climate change on aquatic ecosystems are worthy of comment:

Altered Habitats

Few of our aquatic environments are left untouched by the warming climate. As water temperatures increase, so too does the amount of dissolved oxygen diminish. Lower oxygen levels can have a dramatic effect on the flora and fauna of our aquatic habitats, disrupting the natural balance of said ecosystems.

For example, coral reefs are among the most affected aquatic environments. Coral reefs support a vast array of marine life, but of course, this balance has been significantly disrupted due to the warming of the oceans.

Changes in Seasonal Patterns

Seasonal changes that we all take for granted are being severely disrupted due to climate change. These changes manifest in a variety of ways, but perhaps most notable is the evidence of warming waters resulting in the earlier onset of springtime. These changes could result in serious mismatches in predator prey dynamics, with species times to reproduce or molt falling out of sync with that of their prey.

Further, newer, longer Winters are leading to winterkill of organisms unable to survive in extended period of severe cold. This has significant repercussions for aquatic ecosystems, where populations of fish and other organisms pan out. It also complicates the management of these coupled natural-human systems as changes in temperature impacts are SMEs and the professionals to take a proactive approach.

Ocean Acidification

Rising ocean levels due to melting ice caps is just one facet of the change to our oceans. Another, known as Ocean Acidification, is a byproduct of the increase in carbon dioxide levels. Ocean Acidification occurs when the excess carbon dioxide in the atmosphere is absorbed by the oceans, causing the ocean’s pH level to drop and leading to increased acidity.

The impact this has on marine life cannot be overstated. Shellfish, mollusks, and other organisms that rely heavily on carbonate ions to build their shells and skeletons are particularly vulnerable. The release of acid into our oceans threatens the entire marine food chain, and as such, humanity as a whole.

Adaptation and Future Solutions

The recurrent topic of the impact of climate change on aquatic ecosystems asks us to think scientifically, in terms of dealing with the future, what can be done? Several solutions bear consideration. Mitigation of productivity loss and organizing communities at the local level could play a significant role in ensuring that necessary conservation approaches do not fall short.

We need to destigmatize the conversation surrounding sustainable fishing and the use of new technology in tracking fish populations for sustainable numbers as well. Local communities will also need support for habitat restoration and protection.

Conclusion

The consequences of climate change are far reaching and have upset the balance of not only aquatic ecosystems, but human societies as well. The increasing temperatures of our oceans and lakes have disrupted entire eco-systems, with several species falling prey to the changes in habitat and seasonal patterns.

This has resulted in acidification of our oceans, a problem that has repercussions as great as the melting of our polar icecaps. In a human perspective, these enormously complex changes to aquatic systems stress the need for local community led organizational conservation approaches and technologies to work efficiently.

It is incumbent upon all of us to reduce carbon emissions, whether by individual carbon offset, transitioning to renewables, government policy changes or otherwise. After all, the impacts of climate change on aquatic ecosystems are but one of many reminders of our responsibility to our planet as we strive to be successful in our pursuits on Blue Planet earth.
