Botany in Architecture: The Roots of Biophilic Design


Botany – the biological study of plants – has long inspired architects and designers as they grapple with the challenges of creating sustainable and harmonious living spaces. As our world becomes increasingly urbanized and alienated from nature, the connection between people and plants has taken on paramount importance. Enter biophilic design, a movement that utilizes the healing and rejuvenating power of nature within our built environments. Intrigued by its potential to revolutionize architecture, let’s explore the compelling fusion of botany and design, and the fascinating evolution of biophilic spaces.

Connecting with Nature

Biophilic design taps into humanity’s innate need for a connection with the natural world, predicated on the belief that the more we engage with our environment, the happier and healthier we will be. Since time immemorial, humans have sought solace in the tranquility and beauty of the botanical world, so why not bring a piece of the forest, a burst of floral fragrance, or a waterfall’s majestic murmur into our man-made habitats?

When humans surround themselves with plants, be it through a lush urban garden, a potted succulent, or an indoor forest, their wellbeing is positively affected. Studies show that simply viewing nature improves cognitive function, decreases stress, and accelerates healing. Convinced yet? Let’s dive into the wonderful world of biophilic design.

The Evolution of Biophilic Design

The term “biophilia” comes from the Greek words “bios,” meaning life, and “philos,” meaning love. Biophilic design, then, is the modern embodiment of this love affair with all things botanical.

While biophilic design has gained considerable traction in the past decade, the concept has ancient roots. After all, the first civilizations were born in natural settings, where people lived in harmony with the rhythms of nature. As urbanization gathered pace, the appreciation and utilization of plants in construction increasingly diminished. However, now, as we reassess our relationship with the Earth, architectural biophilic design is blossoming.

A hallmark of the biophilic design trend is the reintroduction of nature into our lives. Nature-centric design elements, such as living walls, vertical gardens, koi ponds, indoor water features, and dedicated spaces for nature appreciation, are all manifestations of biophilia. The integration of living elements not only contributes to the aesthetic quality of spaces but also enhances occupant comfort and productivity, thereby boosting financial returns.

Design Principles of Biophilic Architecture

Biophilic architectural design encompasses a variety of strategies that fuse nature with the built environment. Below are some design principles upon which biophilic spaces are founded:

  1. Connectivity with Nature: Incorporating natural elements, like flowers, shrubs, water, or even components of nature, such as stone or wood, can help mitigate stress and evoke a sense of tranquility.

  2. Daylight and Dynamic Light: Exposure to nature illuminates our days and strengthens our sense of rhythm. Incorporating natural light into architecture can enhance occupant mood and alertness, fostering productivity and reducing energy consumption.

  3. Thermal, auditory, and olfactory connections: People respond positively to natural variations in heat, sound, and smell. Biophilic design acknowledges these connections and incorporates environmental nuances to make spaces truly comfortable and alive.

  4. Legibility of Natural Processes: Using visual biomimicry – patterns and details inspired by the natural world – adds life to a space. Natural patterns can be discovered in floor plans, furniture arrangements, and even the architecture’s structural elements.

  5. Non-linear processes and4 time scales: Biophilic design acknowledges the passage of time and the cyclical nature of life. By embracing the ever-changing nature of the natural world, architects can create living spaces that evolve and adapt to the human life cycle.

Case Studies: Biophilic Spaces

Innovators in architecture and interior design have embraced biophilic concepts across the globe, creating spaces that enhance human experience through a connection with nature. Here are a few projects that do an outstanding job of intertwining botany and design:

  1. The LUMA Arches in Arles, France: An experimental art and innovation center, the LUMA Arches features expansive galleries and spacious public zones, adorned by vibrant botanical installations. The building’s translucent canopy allows natural light to interact with the lush greenery, stimulating awe and wonder in the people who experience them.

  2. The Bullitt Center in Seattle, United States: Dubbed the “greenest commercial building in the world,” the Bullitt Center prides itself on harnessing the power of nature. From its solar-powered roof to the living wall cloaking its facade, the Bullitt Center sets a precedent for sustainable growth and a connection with the environment.

  3. The Canopy in Brasília, Brazil: The Canopy, designed by Studio MK27, is a showcase of understated elegance and architectural biophilia. With its nature-centric design – including a swimming pool and an aquarium – the Canopy invites a deeper connection with natural environments.

Future of Biophilic Design: Pioneering Sustainability

The future of architecture promises to be greener, and more conscious of our planet’s delicate balance. By integrating botany and design principles, architects, and designers can enhance our experience of the built environment through connections with nature. As the world focuses on sustainability and human health, biophilic design – the roots of which lie firmly in botany – is guaranteed to grow ever stronger.

Biophilic design is a natural fit in our struggle to harmonize with the environment, performing double-duty by creating enduring structures while simultaneously reconnecting people with nature. Hold onto the roots of biophilic and botanical design – the way forward for architecture and beyond – lies in an appreciation of nature as the foundation of our very existence.
