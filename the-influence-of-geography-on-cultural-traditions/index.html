The Influence of Geography on Cultural Traditions


Landscapes, climates, and borders greatly shape our customs, beliefs, and art. Geography has played a significant role in the evolution and preservation of cultural traditions throughout history. In this essay, we will explore the influence of geography on cultural traditions.

Geography’s most apparent impact on culture comes via the environment. Take, for example, the people of the Nordic countries. Their seafaring culture was deeply influenced by the abundance of waterways and maritime resources that surrounded them. It is no accident that Odin, the All-Father in Norse Mythology, is often depicted with vessels and the deep sea itself.

On the other hand, consider the desert-dwelling Tuareg people of North Africa. The harsh sun and sandy terrain fostered strong social bonds and traditions centered around protection against the elements and survival. Similarly, in the context of India, the fertile Indus River valley became a crucial aspect in the cultivation of agriculture, laying the foundation of Early Harappan civilization. So extensive was this civilization that their unique, individualized seals were found as far as Mesopotamia.

Moreover, borders have long dictated cultural traditions. The division between countries can result in completely unique expressions of art, language, and lifestyle. For instance, the Celtic and Germanic tribes saw themselves as distinct entities, filling their efforts to draw these linguistic and cultural lines even sharper in the face of Roman conquest. The practice of erecting protective ramparts or walls, as seen in the Dover castle or the Great Wall of China, demonstrate society’s fortification against outside forces.

Topographical and geological features remain as powerful cultural shapers. Perhaps nowhere is this more evident than in Japan, where the mountain, Shinto, and Buddhism blend together to form a deep, symbiotic relationship. Mountains are seen as sacred and divine - a sentiment repeatedly reflected in Japan’s rich cultural history, from sumi-e paintings to No drama.

Even where physical geography does not play a direct role, the societal geography leaves an indelible mark. The Mediterranean Sea not only shows the beauty of Cleopatra’s Egypt and the grandeur of Rome, but also geographically connects the world’s three continents, permitting the movement of ideals and innovations. So, the exchange of culture flows, weaving themselves into unique patterns and histories around the world.

In conclusion, the influence of geography on cultural traditions is undeniable. These geographical influences, whether through climate, terrain, or borders, shape and guide societies. Sometimes subtle, and other times profound, the details of our geography inscribe themselves into the foundations of our cultural existence. From the myths of the Nordic North to the rich traditions of Japan’s sacred mountains, geography continues to weave rich, diverse, and unique stories around the world.
