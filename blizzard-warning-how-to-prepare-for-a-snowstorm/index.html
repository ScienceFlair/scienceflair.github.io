Blizzard Warning: How to Prepare for a Snowstorm


A blizzard is one of the most dangerous and unpredictable weather events that can hit during the winter months. With heavy snowfall, extremely cold temperatures, and fierce winds, a blizzard can quickly turn a peaceful winter day into a hazardous and life-threatening situation. In the event of a blizzard warning, it’s essential to be prepared and know how to protect yourself, your family, and your property. In this guide, we will go through all the necessary steps to take before, during, and after a snowstorm, ensuring that you stay safe and can manage through one of the most severe winter storms.

Understanding a Blizzard

Before we get into the specifics of preparation, it’s crucial to understand what a blizzard is. According to the National Weather Service, a blizzard is defined by three main characteristics:

  • Winds of 35 mph or more: Blizzards are characterized by very strong winds that can cause snow to drift and reduce visibility significantly.
  • Reduced Visibility: A blizzard must feature visibility of less than a quarter-mile for an extended period of time (at least 3 hours).
  • Heavy Snow: Snowfall intensity can vary, but blizzards usually come with a heavy snowstorm that reduces visibility and causes dangerous conditions on roads and sidewalks.

It’s important to remember that a blizzard does not necessarily have to bring massive snow accumulation, but the dangerous combination of snow, wind, and reduced visibility makes blizzards deadly.

Preparing Before the Snowstorm Hits

Preparation is the key to surviving a blizzard and minimizing the risks associated with these extreme weather conditions. Let’s break down the steps you should take before the snowstorm arrives:

1. Monitor Weather Updates

Before a snowstorm strikes, stay updated with weather forecasts. The National Weather Service, local weather stations, and weather apps are your best friends when it comes to anticipating a snowstorm. Pay attention to blizzard warnings, which are issued when the weather conditions meet the necessary criteria. In addition to watching the weather, sign up for emergency alerts on your phone or via other services to receive real-time updates.

2. Stock Up on Essential Supplies

When a blizzard hits, travel becomes hazardous, and stores may close or become difficult to access. Make sure you have the following items in sufficient quantities to last for at least 72 hours:

  • Non-perishable foods: Canned goods, dry foods (such as pasta, rice, and oats), energy bars, and frozen meals can be vital in case you lose power or cannot leave your home.
  • Water: Store a minimum of one gallon of water per person per day for drinking and sanitation.
  • Batteries: Stock up on extra batteries for flashlights, lanterns, and radios. Power outages during blizzards are common, and it’s crucial to have reliable light sources.
  • Medications: Make sure you have enough prescription medications for several days. If you need any over-the-counter remedies (such as pain relievers or cold medicine), stock up as well.
  • Blankets and Warm Clothing: Layering your clothing and having extra blankets can help you stay warm during power outages. Consider investing in thermal or wool clothing for added warmth.
  • First Aid Kit: A well-stocked first aid kit is essential for addressing any injuries or health issues that arise during the storm.
  • Pet Supplies: If you have pets, make sure you have enough food, water, and any necessary medications for them.

3. Secure Your Home

Your home needs to be ready for the worst-case scenario. Here are a few steps to make sure your home is safe and secure during the storm:

  • Seal Windows and Doors: Make sure all windows and doors are tightly closed. Insulate drafty windows with heavy curtains or blankets to keep the cold out.
  • Check Your Heating System: Make sure your heating system is functioning properly. If you use a fireplace, ensure the chimney is clean and free of blockages.
  • Clear Gutters and Drains: Remove any debris from gutters and drains around your property to prevent ice dams from forming, which could lead to water damage once the snow melts.
  • Prepare Your Car: If you must travel, ensure your car is prepared for winter driving. Check tire pressure, make sure your antifreeze levels are adequate, and fill your gas tank before the snowstorm hits. Keep an emergency kit in the car with blankets, non-perishable snacks, water, a flashlight, and a phone charger.

4. Plan for Power Outages

Blizzards often bring heavy snow and ice, which can lead to power outages. Losing electricity can make your home cold and dark, so it’s important to be prepared:

  • Use Backup Power: If you have a generator, make sure it’s in working order and that you have enough fuel to keep it running for several days. Never operate a generator indoors, as it produces carbon monoxide, which can be deadly.
  • Unplug Electronics: Unplug sensitive electronics to prevent damage from power surges when the electricity is restored.
  • Create a Warm Room: If your home becomes too cold during a power outage, designate one room where everyone can gather. Close off other rooms to conserve heat, and use blankets and sleeping bags to keep warm.

5. Establish a Communication Plan

In the event of a blizzard, staying in touch with your family and friends is essential, especially if you get separated or trapped. Establish a communication plan with your loved ones so that everyone knows what to do and where to go if the weather turns severe. Make sure your phone is fully charged, and consider investing in a portable power bank.

During the Blizzard: How to Stay Safe

Once the blizzard hits, your primary concern should be staying inside and safe. Here’s how to navigate the storm once it arrives:

1. Stay Indoors

When the snowstorm begins, avoid going outside unless absolutely necessary. With strong winds, snowdrifts, and limited visibility, traveling during a blizzard can be incredibly dangerous. Even short trips to a neighbor’s house or the local store can turn life-threatening. Stay inside and wait for the storm to pass.

2. Keep Warm

The combination of freezing temperatures and wind chill can cause hypothermia and frostbite if you’re not properly protected. To stay warm, layer your clothing in a way that traps heat close to your body. Focus on wearing warm socks, gloves, and hats, as these areas of the body are particularly susceptible to cold-related injuries. Keep your home as warm as possible, using space heaters safely, and avoid opening doors unnecessarily to conserve heat.

3. Monitor Your Carbon Monoxide Levels

If you’re using a fireplace, space heater, or generator, make sure the space is well-ventilated and that carbon monoxide levels do not rise to dangerous levels. Carbon monoxide poisoning is a serious concern during power outages, so keep an eye out for symptoms such as dizziness, nausea, and confusion, and evacuate your home immediately if you suspect carbon monoxide poisoning.

4. Be Careful When Shoveling Snow

If you have to shovel snow outside, take breaks and avoid overexerting yourself. The cold and physical strain of shoveling can lead to heart attacks or muscle injuries, especially for older adults. Use a sturdy shovel, wear appropriate clothing, and pace yourself to prevent injury.

After the Snowstorm: What to Do Next

Once the snowstorm passes, the cleanup and recovery phase begins. Here are steps to follow once the blizzard is over:

1. Wait for Official Safety Updates

Before venturing out, wait for official updates from local authorities. Make sure the roads are safe and that conditions are no longer life-threatening. Avoid driving until plows and emergency responders have cleared major roads, and only venture out if necessary.

2. Clear Snow and Ice from Your Property

Once it’s safe to do so, start clearing snow and ice from your driveway, walkways, and stairs to avoid falls and injuries. Use ice melt or sand to prevent slips, and be cautious of slippery spots that may still remain, even after shoveling.

3. Inspect Your Home for Damage

Check your home for any signs of damage that may have occurred during the storm, such as roof leaks from ice dams or damage to windows. Take action to prevent further damage if necessary, and report any significant damage to your insurance company.

4. Check on Neighbors

After a blizzard, it’s important to check on elderly or vulnerable neighbors who may have had trouble preparing or weathering the storm. Offer assistance if you can, whether it’s helping to clear snow from their driveway or delivering food and water.

Conclusion

Blizzards are among the most dangerous and unpredictable weather events, but with the right preparation, you can protect yourself, your family, and your home. By staying informed, stocking up on essentials, securing your property, and knowing what to do during and after the storm, you’ll be better equipped to handle the challenges of a snowstorm. Remember, preparation is key—ensure that you’re ready well in advance of any blizzard warnings to minimize risks and stay safe. Stay warm, stay safe, and don’t take unnecessary chances during a snowstorm.
